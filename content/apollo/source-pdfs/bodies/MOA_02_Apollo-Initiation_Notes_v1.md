Action 1: Set a Fixed Wake Time
Starting tomorrow, you wake up at the same time every day. Not "weekdays." Every day. Including
weekends.

This is non-negotiable, and here's why it matters.

Your body runs on what's called a circadian rhythm - a roughly 24-hour internal clock that regulates
nearly every biological process: hormone release, body temperature, digestion, cognitive function,
immune activity. This clock is primarily set by two things: light exposure and wake time. When you
wake up at different times every day, you're essentially giving your body jet lag repeatedly. You're
forcing it to constantly recalibrate, and that recalibration is metabolically expensive and hormonally
disruptive.

Research from the field of chronobiology has consistently shown that irregular sleep-wake patterns
are associated with increased body fat, impaired glucose metabolism, elevated cortisol, and
reduced cognitive performance. One study published in Scientific Reports found that for every hour
of variability in wake time, subjects showed measurable increases in metabolic dysfunction markers.

The fix is simple: pick a wake time and stick to it.

I'd recommend somewhere between 6:00 and 7:00 AM for most men. Early enough to give you time
for a proper morning routine before work, but not so early that you're fighting your biology. If your
current schedule has you waking at 9 AM, don't try to suddenly shift to 5 AM. Move it back in 30-
minute increments over a few days until you hit your target.

Once you set your wake time, protect it. This means your bedtime becomes non-negotiable too -
you need to be in bed early enough to get 7-8 hours of sleep before your alarm goes off. We'll cover
the sleep side in more detail, but understand that the wake time is the anchor. Everything else
follows from it.

Set your alarm. Put your phone across the room so you have to physically get up to turn it off. No
snooze button. When the alarm goes off, you get up. That's it.

This single change, done consistently, will improve your energy, hormone profile, and mental clarity
more than most supplements or "biohacks" ever could.

Action 2: Morning Sunlight + Walking
Within the first hour of waking, get outside and expose your eyes to natural sunlight. Combine this
with a walk - even 10-15 minutes is enough. Even opening your window and putting your head out
to breathe in fresh air is enough.

This isn't wellness fluff. This is hard neuroscience.

When light enters your eyes in the morning, it hits specialised cells in your retina called intrinsically
photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs). These cells don't process vision - they signal to a
region of your brain called the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), which is the master clock controlling
your circadian rhythm. That signal does several things simultaneously: it triggers a cortisol pulse that
promotes wakefulness and alertness, it sets a timer for melatonin release roughly 12-14 hours later
(which is why morning light exposure improves sleep that night), and it helps calibrate your entire
hormonal cascade for the day.

Dr. Andrew Huberman, a neuroscientist at Stanford, has talked extensively about this. The data is
clear: people who get morning sunlight exposure within the first hour of waking have better sleep
quality, more stable energy throughout the day, improved mood, and better cognitive performance
compared to those who don't. The effect is particularly strong when the light exposure is combined
with movement.

Here's how to implement it:

Wake up at your fixed time, drink a glass of water, and get outside within 30-60 minutes. Leave your
sunglasses off - you need the light hitting your eyes (don't stare directly at the sun, obviously, but
you need unfiltered natural light). Walk for at least 10-15 minutes. If you have more time, walk longer.
If it's cloudy, still go outside - overcast sky light is still significantly brighter than indoor lighting and
will still trigger the circadian response.

If you live somewhere with genuinely dark mornings (northern latitudes in winter), you may need a
light therapy lamp rated at 10,000 lux. Use it for 20-30 minutes while you have your morning coffee.
But real sunlight is always preferable when available.

This practice also has a secondary benefit: it starts your day with a win. You've already done
something positive for your health before most people are even awake. That momentum carries
forward.

The walk doesn't need to be intense. This isn't cardio. It's movement combined with light exposure.
Think of it as calibrating your system for the day ahead.

Action 3:Remove Liquid Calories and Processed Food
This week, we make one clean dietary cut: no more liquid calories, and no more ultra-processed
snacks.

I'm not asking you to overhaul your entire diet yet. That comes in Week 2. But these two categories
are so fundamentally damaging and so easy to eliminate that we're removing them immediately.

Liquid Calories

This means sodas, fruit juices, sweetened coffees, energy drinks, alcohol, and anything else you
drink that contains calories. Water, black coffee, plain tea, and sparkling water are fine. Everything
else goes.

Here's why this matters: liquid calories don't register the same way solid food does. When you eat
400 calories of steak, your body knows it. Satiety hormones rise, hunger drops, and you feel full.
When you drink 400 calories of soda, almost none of that satiety signaling happens. Your body
barely notices. You've consumed the energy without any of the feedback that tells you to stop
eating.

A study published in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition compared solid versus liquid calorie
consumption and found that people who consumed calories in liquid form showed significantly less
compensation — meaning they didn't eat less food to make up for the liquid calories. They just
added them on top. This is a major driver of unintentional caloric surplus and fat gain.

Beyond the calorie issue, most liquid calories come with a massive glycemic hit. Fruit juice spikes
your blood sugar nearly as much as soda. That spike is followed by a crash, which triggers hunger
and cravings. It's a cycle that keeps you hungry, tired, and overfed.

Cutting liquid calories is one of the highest-leverage changes you can make. Many men lose
noticeable body fat in the first two weeks just from this single adjustment.

Ultra-Processed Snacks

This means chips, cookies, crackers, candy, pastries, and anything that comes in a package with a
long ingredient list of things you can't pronounce. If it was engineered in a lab to be hyper palatable,
it's out.

These foods are designed to override your satiety signals. Food scientists call it the "bliss point" -
the precise combination of salt, sugar, and fat that makes you unable to stop eating. You're not
weak for struggling to eat just one serving of Doritos. You're fighting against millions of dollars of
research specifically designed to make you over consume.

Eliminating these foods removes the constant background noise of cravings. Within a few days,
you'll notice that your appetite becomes more regulated. You'll eat when you're hungry and stop
when you're full, instead of eating because the food is there and it's engineered to be irresistible.

Don't try to moderate these foods. Don't keep them in the house "for guests" or "for emergencies."
Get them out. If they're not there, you can't eat them. Make the environment work for you, not
against you.

Replace snacks with whole food options: a handful of nuts, Greek yogurt, fruit, boiled eggs, cheese,
sliced vegetables with hummus. Real food that actually nourishes you instead of hijacking your
brain.

Action 5: 10-Minute Daily Mobility
Every single day this week, you'll do 10 minutes of mobility work. Non-negotiable. Even on rest
days. Even when you're tired. Even when you don't feel like it.

Here's why this matters:

Most men are tight, restricted, and moving poorly. Years of sitting at desks, looking at phones, and
neglecting flexibility have created chronic tension patterns that limit performance and increase injury
risk. Your hip flexors are short. Your thoracic spine doesn't rotate properly. Your ankles don't
dorsiflex enough. These restrictions don't just affect your training — they affect how you carry
yourself, how you breathe, and how you feel throughout the day.

Mobility work isn't stretching for the sake of stretching. It's restoring your body's ability to move
through full ranges of motion without compensation. When your joints move properly, your muscles
can fire properly. When your muscles fire properly, you get stronger faster and stay injury-free longer.

The 10-minute daily protocol is in the Mobility module (Course Tile 6), but here's the approach:

Focus areas:

  • Hips (hip flexor stretches, 90/90 positions, deep squats)

  • Thoracic spine (rotations, extensions, cat-cow variations)

  • Ankles (wall stretches, elevated stretches)

  • Shoulders (wall slides, band pull-aparts, hanging if available)

When to do it: Morning is ideal - your body is slightly stiffer, so you're working against more
resistance, which means greater adaptive stimulus. Post-training is also good if morning doesn't
work. The key is consistency. Same time every day creates a trigger that makes it automatic.

How to approach it: This isn't passive stretching where you zone out. Move with intention. Breathe
into tight positions. Spend more time on areas that are particularly restricted. If your hips are a
disaster, spend 6 of your 10 minutes there. Address your weak links.

Ten minutes isn't long, but it compounds dramatically over time. That's 70 minutes per week, 280
minutes per month, over 50 hours per year of dedicated mobility work. Men who do this consistently
move differently after 90 days. Their posture improves. Their training improves. Their daily comfort
improves.

Start today. Pull up the mobility protocol and do your first 10 minutes.

Action 6: Digital Wind-Down Routine
Starting tonight, you'll implement a digital wind-down routine in the hour before bed. This means no
screens - no phone, no laptop, no TV - for the final 30-60 minutes before you intend to sleep.

This is going to be one of the hardest habits for most men to implement. I know that. But it's also
one of the most impactful.

Here's the science: screens emit blue light in wavelengths that are particularly effective at
suppressing melatonin production. Melatonin is the hormone that signals to your body that it's time
to sleep. When you're scrolling your phone at 11 PM, you're actively telling your brain that it's still
daytime. Your melatonin release gets delayed, your sleep quality suffers, and you wake up unrested
even if you technically got "enough" hours.

But it's not just the light. It's the content. Social media, news, email - these are designed to trigger
emotional and cognitive responses. Outrage, comparison, anxiety, stimulation. You're activating
your nervous system at the exact time you should be down-regulating it. You're making it
physiologically harder to fall asleep and psychologically harder to quiet your mind.

The research on this is robust. A meta-analysis published in Sleep Medicine Reviews found that
screen use before bed was consistently associated with delayed sleep onset, reduced sleep
duration, and poorer sleep quality across all age groups.

Here's how to implement the wind-down:

Set a hard cutoff time. If you need to be asleep by 10:30 PM to get 7-8 hours before your wake
time, your screen cutoff is 9:30 PM/10 PM LATEST. Put your phone in another room. Turn off the TV.
Close the laptop.

Create an alternative routine. The hour before bed should be low-stimulation activities that allow
your nervous system to calm down. Read a physical book (not on a screen). Do your mobility work if
you haven't done it earlier. Take a warm shower or bath (the subsequent cooling helps trigger sleep).
Journal or review your day. Have a conversation with someone you live with. Prepare your clothes
and bag for the next day.

Make your bedroom a sleep environment. Dark, cool, and screen-free. No TV in the bedroom.
Phone charges in another room. If you use your phone as an alarm, buy a cheap alarm clock and
remove the excuse.

The first few nights will feel uncomfortable. You'll be bored. You'll feel the urge to check your phone.
That urge is a withdrawal response from a genuine addiction. Sit with it. It passes.

Within a week, you'll start noticing that you fall asleep faster. Within two weeks, you'll notice your
sleep feels deeper and you wake up more refreshed. This one habit change can be transformative if
you actually stick to it.

Action 1: Choose Your Training Path
It's time to commit to a training path: Gym-Based or Home-Based.

Both paths will get you results. Both are built on the same principles: compound movements,
progressive overload, full-body or push/pull/legs structures, and athletic conditioning. The difference
is equipment access.

Gym-Based Training is for men who have consistent access to a commercial gym or well-equipped
home gym. You'll have access to barbells, dumbbells, cable machines, and a full range of
equipment. This path allows for heavier loading and more exercise variety.

Home-Based Training is for men who are training with minimal equipment: a kettlebell or dumbbell,
resistance bands, pull-up/dip bars, and their own bodyweight. This path emphasizes calisthenics
progressions, unilateral work, and creative loading strategies. Don't mistake "minimal equipment"
for "less effective." Men have built exceptional physiques with nothing but a pull-up bar and the
floor.

Here's how to choose:

If you have reliable gym access (4+ days per week, no excuses about crowding or travel), go Gym-
Based. If your gym access is inconsistent, or if you simply prefer training at home, go Home-Based.
If you travel frequently, Home-Based is probably your better long-term option because you can train
anywhere.

Don't overthink this. Pick the path that you will actually stick to. The best program is the one you'll
do consistently. A perfect gym program done inconsistently will always lose to a good home
program done religiously.

Once you've chosen, go to the Training module and familiarise yourself with your path's specific
programming. Your training frequency increases to 4 sessions this week (we're adding one), and the
structure becomes more defined.

Action 2: Lock In the Apollo Plate Method
This week, you implement the Apollo Plate Method for every main meal.

This is not a diet. It's a framework - a visual and practical guide for building meals that support your
goals without counting, tracking, or obsessing.

The Plate Breakdown:

Protein (roughly 1/4 to 1/3 of your plate): This is your anchor. Every meal starts with a quality
protein source. Beef, chicken, fish, eggs, lamb - prioritise animal proteins because they're complete
(containing all essential amino acids) and more bioavailable than plant sources. You're aiming for

approximately 0.8-1 grams of protein per pound of body weight per day, spread across your meals.
For most men, that's 30-50g of protein per meal.

Vegetables and Fruits (roughly 1/3 to 1/2 of your plate): Fill a significant portion of your plate with
fibrous vegetables and some fruit. These provide fiber, micronutrients, and volume that helps with
satiety. Prioritise variety - different colours mean different phytonutrients. Leafy greens, cruciferous
vegetables, root vegetables, berries. Don't fear fruit; the fibre content slows sugar absorption.

Carbohydrates (roughly 1/4 of your plate, adjusted based on activity): Clean carb sources like
rice, potatoes, sweet potatoes, oats and quinoa. The amount here flexes based on your training. On
days you train, go for a full portion. On rest days, reduce it or skip it entirely. Carbohydrates are your
performance lever - use them strategically around activity.

Fats (incorporated naturally): Don't add excessive fats, but don't fear them either. Cook with olive
oil, butter, or ghee. Include fatty fish. Use whole eggs, not just whites. Fats support hormone
production (including testosterone), joint health, and satiety. They're naturally present in quality
protein sources and cooking methods.

What's NOT on the plate:

No processed foods. No seed oils (canola, soybean, corn oil - these are inflammatory and
ubiquitous in processed food). No added sugars. No artificial ingredients. If you can't pronounce it,
don't eat it.

Practical implementation:

Every meal you eat this week should follow this template. Breakfast: eggs, vegetables, maybe some
fruit or a slice of quality sourdough bread. Lunch: large serving of protein, big salad or roasted
vegetables, rice or potatoes if you're training that day. Dinner: protein, vegetables, lighter on carbs
especially if you're eating later.

You don't need to weigh anything. You don't need an app. Just look at your plate. Is there a palm-
sized or larger portion of protein? Good. Is half the plate vegetables? Good. Is there a reasonable
portion of clean carbs appropriate to your activity level? Good. That's it.

The Nutrition module goes deeper on meal ideas, grocery lists, and specific templates. Review it
and plan your meals for the week.

Action 3: Hit Your Protein Floor
This week, protein becomes a non-negotiable priority. You need to hit your protein floor every single
day.

Your protein floor is 1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight (0.8-1g per pound). That's the
minimum for supporting muscle protein synthesis while in a recomposition phase (losing fat while
maintaining or building muscle). If you're training hard and recovering well, you can push toward
2.2g/kg, but 1.6g/kg is the floor you never drop below.

For a 80kg man, that's a minimum of 128g of protein per day. For a 90kg man, it's 144g. For a 70kg
man, it's 112g.

Why protein matters this much:

Protein is the only macronutrient that directly provides the building blocks for muscle tissue. When
you train, you create micro-damage in your muscle fibres. Protein provides the amino acids needed
to repair and rebuild those fibres stronger than before. Without adequate protein, you can train as
hard as you want - you won't build muscle optimally, and you'll recover slower.

Protein also has the highest thermic effect of food (TEF) of any macronutrient. Your body burns
approximately 20-30% of protein calories just digesting and processing them, compared to 5-10%
for carbs and 0-3% for fats. This means a high-protein diet inherently supports a leaner body
composition.

Protein is also the most satiating macronutrient. It keeps you full longer and reduces cravings. Men
who struggle with overeating almost always find it easier to control their intake when protein is high.

How to hit it:

Spread your protein across 3-4 meals. Each meal should contain 30-50g of protein. For reference:

  • 3 whole eggs = ~18g protein

  • 200g chicken breast = ~40g protein

  • 200g beef steak = ~40-50g protein

  • 200g salmon = ~40g protein

  • 250g Greek yogurt = ~25g protein

  • 150g cottage cheese = ~15-18g protein

If you're consistently hitting your protein floor, you can be more flexible with everything else. Miss
your protein floor, and everything else becomes harder.

Action 4: Add Zone 2 Conditioning
This week, you add two Zone 2 conditioning sessions to your routine. These are in addition to your
strength training sessions.

Zone 2 refers to a specific heart rate zone - roughly 60-70% of your maximum heart rate. For most
men, this translates to a pace where you can maintain a conversation without gasping. It's not easy,
but it's sustainable. You could theoretically hold this pace for an hour or more.

Why Zone 2 matters:

Zone 2 training builds your aerobic base - the foundation of your cardiovascular system. It
specifically develops your mitochondrial density and function. Mitochondria are the power plants of
your cells; they produce ATP, the energy currency your body runs on. More mitochondria and better-
functioning mitochondria mean more energy, better fat oxidation, faster recovery between sets, and
improved metabolic health overall.

Dr. Peter Attia, a physician focused on longevity, has talked extensively about Zone 2 as one of the
most underutilised training modalities. The research supports this. Consistent Zone 2 work improves
VO2 max, enhances fat metabolism, reduces resting heart rate, and supports cardiovascular health
markers. It's also low-impact enough that it doesn't interfere with strength training recovery.

Most men either skip cardio entirely or do high-intensity work that leaves them fried. Zone 2 fills the
gap. It's conditioning you can do frequently without burning out.

How to implement it:

Two sessions per week, 30-45 minutes each. Options include:

  • Incline treadmill walking (10-15% incline, 3-4 mph pace)

  • Outdoor walking at a brisk pace, ideally with some elevation

  • Light cycling or stationary bike

  • Swimming at a sustainable pace

  • Rowing machine at a conversational pace

Monitor your intensity. If you can't speak in full sentences, you're going too hard. If you're barely
exerting yourself, push a bit more. The sweet spot is sustainable effort - elevated heart rate, light
sweat, but not gasping.

Schedule these sessions on non-strength days, or do them in the morning if you train in the evening.
Don't skip them. This is how you build an engine that supports everything else.

Action 5: Posture and Breathing Awareness
This week, you're going to start paying attention to two things you probably never think about: how
you hold your body and how you breathe.

Posture:

Modern life wrecks posture. Sitting at desks, looking at phones, driving. All of these reinforce
patterns of forward head position, rounded shoulders, and anterior pelvic tilt. Over time, these
patterns become your default. Muscles that should be engaged are dormant. Muscles that should
be relaxed are chronically tight. The result is a body that looks and moves poorly, regardless of how
much muscle you have.

This week, start noticing. Throughout the day, check in with your posture. Are your shoulders rolled
forward? Is your head jutting out in front of your body? Is your pelvis dumping forward and your
lower back excessively arched?

The fix isn't complicated:

  • Stand tall with your weight balanced over your feet

  • Stack your rib cage over your pelvis (not flared forward)

  • Pull your shoulders back and down slightly

  • Keep your head stacked over your spine, not protruding forward

Set reminders on your phone if needed. Every hour, check your posture and correct it. Over time,
the corrected position becomes the default. We'll go much deeper on posture in the Mobility
module, but awareness is the first step.

Breathing:

Most men are chest breathers. They take shallow breaths using primarily the muscles of the upper
chest and neck. This breathing pattern is associated with stress, tension, and sympathetic nervous
system dominance - the "fight or flight" state.

Proper breathing should be diaphragmatic. When you inhale, your belly should expand as your
diaphragm contracts and pushes down. Your chest should move minimally. This activates the
parasympathetic nervous system, promoting relaxation, recovery, and stress reduction.

Practice this: Sit or lie down comfortably. Place one hand on your chest and one on your belly.
Inhale slowly through your nose for 4 seconds. Your belly should rise while your chest stays
relatively still. Exhale slowly through your mouth for 6 seconds. Your belly should fall. Repeat for 2-3
minutes.

Do this breathing practice at least once today and ideally at the end of your wind-down routine
before bed. It's a simple tool for down-regulating your nervous system and improving your recovery.

Action 1: Progressive Overload Introduced
Progressive overload is the single most important principle in strength training. Without it, your body
has no reason to adapt. With it, you get steadily stronger and more muscular over time.

The concept is simple: you must consistently increase the demands you place on your
musculoskeletal system. If you lift the same weight for the same reps week after week, your body
adapts to that stimulus and stops changing. You plateau.

How to apply progressive overload:

There are several ways to progressively overload. The most straightforward is adding weight — if
you benched 60kg for 8 reps last week, try 62.5kg this week, or try 60kg for 9-10 reps. Both
represent progression.

Option 1: Add weight. Even small increases (1-2.5kg) add up over time. A 2kg increase per week
on a lift means 100kg added over a year. Don't underestimate incremental progress.

Option 2: Add reps. If you can't add weight, add reps within your target range. Going from 8 reps to
10 reps at the same weight is progression. Once you hit the top of your rep range, increase weight
and drop back to the bottom of the range.

Option 3: Add sets. If you did 3 sets last week, try 4 sets this week. More volume is a valid
progression strategy, though it adds training time.

Option 4: Improve execution. Better form, more controlled tempo, fuller range of motion - these all
increase the effective stimulus even at the same weight.

This week's mandate:

Every training session, you attempt to progress on at least one exercise. Track your lifts. Know what
you did last week. Walk into each session with a target: "Last week I squatted 80kg for 3x8. Today
I'm going for 80kg for 3x9, or 82.5kg for 3x8."

This mindset shift from "doing a workout" to "progressing my training" is fundamental. You're not
exercising aimlessly. You're building something, session by session.

A note on recovery: progression requires recovery. If you're not sleeping well, not eating enough
protein, or running yourself into the ground with stress, your ability to progress will stall. The system
works together.

Action 2: Conditioning Intensity Increase
This week, we add one higher-intensity conditioning session to your routine. You'll now have:

  • 2 Zone 2 sessions (30-45 minutes, conversational pace)

  • 1 higher-intensity session (20-30 minutes)

Why the intensity increase:

Zone 2 builds your aerobic base, but your body also needs exposure to higher intensities. Working
at higher heart rate zones (Zone 4-5, roughly 80-90% max heart rate) improves your cardiovascular
system's capacity to handle stress, increases your VO2 max ceiling, and creates metabolic
adaptations that enhance fat burning and work capacity.

The combination of low-intensity steady state (Zone 2) and higher-intensity work creates a well-
rounded aerobic profile. You can sustain long efforts AND handle high-output bursts when needed.
That's athletic conditioning.

Options for higher-intensity work:

  • Hill sprints: Find a hill, sprint up hard (20-30 seconds), walk down, repeat 6-10 times. Total
   session: 20-25 minutes including warm-up.

  • Interval running: 30 seconds hard, 60-90 seconds easy jog or walk. Repeat 8-12 rounds. Total
   session: 20-30 minutes.

  • Assault bike or rower intervals: 20 seconds all-out, 40 seconds easy. Repeat 10-15 rounds.

  • Circuit finishers: After a strength session, add a 10-minute finisher. Example: 10 kettlebell
   swings, 10 burpees, 10 jump squats - AMRAP for 10 minutes.

How to schedule:

Place your high-intensity session on a day when you're not doing heavy lower body strength work.
The systemic fatigue from sprints or intervals will interfere with squat or deadlift performance if done
too close together.

Example weekly structure:

  • Monday: Strength (Push)

  • Tuesday: Zone 2 conditioning

  • Wednesday: Strength (Pull)

  • Thursday: High-intensity conditioning

  • Friday: Strength (Legs)

  • Saturday: Zone 2 conditioning or active recovery

  • Sunday: Rest

Adjust based on your training path, but maintain the principle: distribute intensity across the week,
don't stack everything together.

Action 3: Sleep Consistency Enforced
If you've been inconsistent with sleep, that ends now.

Week 1 established a wake time. Week 2 introduced the wind-down routine. Week 3 is where we
enforce both with no exceptions.

The rule: Same bedtime and wake time every day. Seven days a week. No "catching up" on
weekends. No staying up late because you don't have work tomorrow. No negotiating.

Why this matters:

Sleep is where growth hormone gets released in its highest pulses. Sleep is where muscle protein
synthesis peaks. Sleep is where memories consolidate and cognitive function restores. Sleep is
where cortisol levels normalise. Chronic sleep deprivation - even mild, consistent deficits, impairs all
of these processes.

Research published in JAMA Internal Medicine found that just one week of sleeping 5 hours per
night (instead of 8) reduced testosterone levels by 10-15% in young healthy men. That's the
hormonal equivalent of aging 10-15 years. And that's just one week of mild restriction.

Social jet lag - the phenomenon of shifting your sleep times on weekends, disrupts your circadian
rhythm and produces measurable metabolic dysfunction. It's not just about total hours; it's about
consistency.

Implementation:

Calculate your required bedtime based on your fixed wake time. If you wake at 6:00 AM and need
7.5 hours of sleep, you should be asleep by 10:30 PM. That means in bed by 10:00-10:15 PM, wind-
down routine starting at 9:30 PM.

Set alarms on both ends. A wind-down alarm at 9:30 PM. A "be in bed" alarm at 10:00 PM. Treat
these with the same non-negotiability as your wake time.

If you're struggling to fall asleep, address the likely causes: too much caffeine (cap it at noon),
screen use before bed (enforce the wind-down), inconsistent light exposure (get morning sunlight),
or excessive stress (use the breathing techniques). Don't reach for sleeping pills or alcohol - both
destroy sleep quality even if they help you fall asleep faster.

Your body will adapt. After 7-10 days of strict consistency, you'll start waking before your alarm,
feeling genuinely rested. That's when you know the system is working.

Action 4: Hunger Management Strategies
As you train harder and eat cleaner, your appetite will shift. Some men find hunger decreases as
blood sugar stabilises and cravings fade. Others find hunger increases as their metabolism adapts
to the training load.

Both are normal. What matters is having strategies to manage hunger without breaking the
nutritional framework.

If you're hungry between meals:

First, check if you're actually hungry or just bored/stressed/habituated. Real hunger comes on
gradually and is satisfied by food in general. Cravings are specific (you want something sweet, or
salty, or that specific food you're thinking about). Learn to distinguish between them.

If it's real hunger:

  • Drink water first. Thirst often masquerades as hunger.

  • If still hungry after 15 minutes, eat a high-protein snack: Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, hard-
    boiled eggs, a handful of nuts.

  • Don't reach for carb-heavy snacks, which will spike blood sugar and create more hunger later.

If it's a craving:

  • Remove yourself from the situation if possible. Walk outside. Do something with your hands.

  • Drink water or black coffee/tea. (I would recommend to limit coffee though)

  • Wait 15-20 minutes. Cravings typically peak and fade.

  • If the craving persists, ask yourself what you're really looking for. Often cravings are emotional,
    not physical - stress, boredom, seeking comfort. Address the root cause.

If you're ravenous post-workout:

This is normal, especially after intense sessions. Your body has depleted glycogen and needs fuel.
This is the best time for a higher-carb meal. Don't fight this hunger - feed it with quality food. A post-
workout meal with protein, carbs, and some fruit is exactly what your body needs.

If you're hungry before bed:

Eating late can interfere with sleep quality, so ideally you're not going to bed hungry. But if you are, a
small protein-rich snack is fine - some cottage cheese, a small portion of Greek yogurt. Avoid heavy
meals or high-carb options close to bedtime, which can disrupt sleep.

General principles:

  • High protein intake helps manage hunger (which is why we emphasise hitting your protein floor)

  • Fibre-rich vegetables add volume without excessive calories

  • Adequate fat intake supports satiety hormones

  • Eating slowly and mindfully allows satiety signals time to reach your brain

  • Consistent meal timing reduces random hunger spikes

Hunger is information, not a problem. Learn to read it.
