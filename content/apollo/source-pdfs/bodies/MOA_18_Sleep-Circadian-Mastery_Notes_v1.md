Intro
Phase 1 taught you that sleep is non-negotiable. Phase 2 took you deeper into sleep architecture,
supplements, and recovery tracking. You know the basics — 7-9 hours, consistent timing, dark
room, cool temperature.

This module goes beyond the basics into the science of circadian biology - the 24-hour internal
clock that governs not just sleep, but hormone production, cognitive function, metabolic efficiency,
body temperature, and athletic performance.

Understanding circadian biology means understanding that there is an optimal time for everything —
when to train, when to eat, when to do your most demanding cognitive work, when to wind down.
Most men fight their biology by doing the wrong things at the wrong times and wonder why they feel
perpetually off.

> You will not fight your biology. You will align with it.

Sleep Positions and Spinal Health
Mastery
How you sleep shapes how you feel.

> You spend approximately one-third of your life in a sleep position. The position you choose
directly affects spinal alignment, breathing quality, recovery, and even facial aesthetics.

Side Sleeping — The Optimal Position

Side sleeping improves glymphatic clearance which is the brain's waste removal system that is
most active during sleep. This means side sleeping may support better cognitive recovery and
reduced risk of neurodegenerative disease.

Optimising side sleep:

  • Pillow height: The pillow should fill the gap between your ear and the mattress, keeping your
   cervical spine neutral. Too thick — neck bends up. Too thin — neck bends down. Both create
   tension and impaired breathing.

  • Knee pillow: Place a pillow between the knees to maintain pelvic alignment and prevent the top
   knee from dropping forward (which rotates the lumbar spine).

  • Arm position: Bottom arm extended forward or under the pillow — not tucked under your body
   (this compresses the shoulder and restricts blood flow).

  • Alternate sides. Sleeping exclusively on one side can create muscular imbalances. Train
   yourself to alternate.

Left side specifically may be superior for digestion due to the anatomical position of the stomach
and intestines. If you experience acid reflux, left-side sleeping reduces symptoms significantly
compared to right-side or back sleeping.

Back Sleeping — Acceptable With Modifications

Back sleeping maintains neutral spinal alignment but can worsen snoring and sleep apnea due to
gravitational effects on the tongue and soft palate.

If you back-sleep: use a thinner pillow to avoid pushing the head forward. Place a pillow under the
knees to reduce lower back strain. If you snore, consider transitioning to side sleeping.

Stomach Sleeping — Eliminate This

Stomach sleeping forces the cervical spine into sustained rotation (your head must turn to one side
to breathe) and exaggerates lumbar extension. This creates neck pain, lower back tension, and can
contribute to nerve impingement over time.

> If you are a habitual stomach sleeper: use body pillows placed on either side to prevent rolling
onto your stomach during the night. This takes 1-2 weeks to adapt but is worth the effort.

The Circadian System Deep Dive
Understanding your internal clock.

Every cell in your body has a clock. These cellular clocks are synchronised by a master pacemaker
in the hypothalamus called the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN). The SCN takes input primarily from
light exposure through the retina and coordinates the timing of virtually every biological process.

> Your body literally runs on a 24-hour schedule, and fighting that schedule has measurable
consequences.

What the Circadian System Controls:

  • Cortisol — Peaks in the morning (cortisol awakening response), declines through the day,
   reaches its lowest point around midnight. This curve drives alertness in the morning and wind-
   down in the evening.

  • Melatonin — Begins rising 2-3 hours before natural sleep onset (dim light melatonin onset/
   DLMO), peaks during sleep, suppressed by light. Not just a sleep hormone, it is a powerful
   antioxidant that supports immune function and cellular repair.

  • Testosterone — Peaks in the early morning. This is why morning levels are used for testing.
   Disrupted sleep directly reduces testosterone.

  • Growth Hormone — Released primarily during the first deep sleep cycle. Delayed sleep onset
   or disrupted early sleep architecture reduces GH secretion.

  • Body Temperature — Rises through the morning, peaks in late afternoon, drops in the evening.
   Physical performance peaks when body temperature peaks — this is why most athletic records
   are set in the afternoon.

  • Insulin Sensitivity — Highest in the morning, lowest in the evening. This has direct implications
   for nutrient timing.

  • Cognitive Function — Most people have peak analytical performance 2-4 hours after waking, a
   dip in early afternoon (the circadian trough), and a secondary peak in late afternoon.

Circadian Disruption — What It Costs You:

Shift workers provide the clearest evidence. Meta-analyses consistently show that chronic circadian
disruption is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, obesity,
depression, and certain cancers. Even the difference between sleep timing on workdays versus free
days is associated with increased body mass, metabolic dysfunction, and poorer cognitive
performance.

> You do not have to be a shift worker to experience circadian disruption. Inconsistent sleep timing,
late-night screen exposure, eating at irregular hours, and training at varying times all send conflicting
signals to the circadian system.

> The Non-Negotiable: Consistency

The single most important action for circadian health is consistent timing - waking and sleeping at
approximately the same time every day, including weekends.

Your body cannot optimise hormone production, cognitive function, and metabolic processes if it
does not know what time it is. Give it a consistent schedule and it will reward you.

Light as a Tool
Strategic light exposure for energy and sleep.

> Light is the most powerful tool you have for circadian regulation. More powerful than any
supplement, more impactful than any sleep hack. Get light exposure right and everything
downstream improves.

Morning Light — The Foundation

Get bright light exposure within 30-60 minutes of waking. This is not optional. This sets the cortisol
awakening response, initiates the countdown to melatonin release approximately 14-16 hours later,
and anchors your circadian phase.

Protocol:

  • 10-15 minutes of direct outdoor sunlight. Even on cloudy days, outdoor light intensity
   (5,000-100,000 lux) far exceeds indoor lighting (100-500 lux).

  • Do not wear sunglasses during this window. The light needs to enter through the retina.

  • Combine with your morning movement practice from Module 1 for efficiency.

Daytime Light — Maintain the Signal

Bright light exposure throughout the day reinforces the circadian signal that "it is daytime - be alert
and active."

  • Work near windows when possible.

  • Take outdoor breaks, even 5-10 minutes of midday light exposure strengthens the circadian
   rhythm.

  • If your workspace is poorly lit, consider supplemental task lighting that provides bright, cool-
   white light (5000-6500K colour temperature).

Evening Light — Protect the Wind-Down

This is where most men sabotage their sleep without realising it. Bright light and especially blue light
(460-480nm wavelength) after sunset suppresses melatonin production and delays sleep onset.

Protocol:

  • 2-3 hours before bed: switch to dim, warm lighting. Overhead lights off. Use table lamps, salt
   lamps, or candles.

  • If using screens: blue light blocking glasses (amber or red lenses)

  • Better than glasses: stop using screens 60-90 minutes before bed entirely.

  • Install warm-toned smart bulbs (2700K or lower) that you can schedule to dim automatically in
   the evening.

  • Red or orange night lights in the bathroom - that middle-of-the-night bright bathroom light
   destroys melatonin more than you think.

Light and Mood

Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) is the extreme version, but subclinical effects of inadequate light
exposure affect many men, particularly those in northern climates or who work indoors all day.
Symptoms include low energy, reduced motivation, increased cravings, and mild depression during
darker months.

Morning light therapy (10,000 lux for 20-30 minutes within the first hour of waking) is the first-line
treatment for SAD and can benefit anyone experiencing winter-related drops in mood and energy.

Chronotype Optimisation
Working with your natural rhythm, not against it.

Not every man is wired the same way. Chronotype which is your genetic predisposition toward
earlier or later sleep/wake timing varies significantly and is largely determined by genetics rather
than discipline.

The Chronotype Spectrum:

Morning Type (Early Bird) — Naturally wakes early (5-7am), most alert and productive in the
morning, energy declines by early evening, falls asleep easily by 9-10pm. Approximately 25% of the
population.

Intermediate Type (Bear) — Follows the solar cycle roughly. Wakes 7-8am, productive mid-
morning through early afternoon, winds down naturally by 10-11pm. Approximately 50% of the
population.

Evening Type (Wolf/Night Owl) — Naturally wakes later (8-10am+), peak productivity in the
afternoon and evening, difficulty falling asleep before midnight. Approximately 25% of the
population.

Key insight: Your chronotype is not a character flaw. Evening types are not lazy. Morning types are
not more disciplined. These are genetic differences in clock gene expression specifically PER3 gene
variants that determine when your body naturally wants to be active and when it wants to rest.

Working With Your Chronotype:

The goal is to align your most demanding activities with your peak performance windows.

Morning Type:

  • Train in the morning or early afternoon

  • Schedule your most demanding cognitive work in the first 4 hours after waking

  • Protect the evening wind-down — your energy drops early, do not fight it

  • Risk factor: social obligations that push bedtime later cause more disruption for morning types

Intermediate Type:

  • Most flexible. Can train effectively at most times.

  • Peak cognitive performance approximately 10am-2pm

  • Maintain consistent timing and you will be fine

Evening Type:

  • If possible, train in the afternoon or early evening when body temperature and performance peak

  • Your best cognitive work is often in the late morning through evening

  • Do not force an unnaturally early wake time if your schedule permits — fighting chronotype
   consistently leads to chronic sleep debt

  • Use bright morning light aggressively to pull your circadian phase earlier if early waking is non-
   negotiable

Social Jet Lag

The mismatch between your biological clock and your social obligations (work schedule, family
demands) creates "social jet lag." This is particularly problematic for evening types who must wake
early for work, they accumulate sleep debt during the week and attempt to recover on weekends by
sleeping in, which further disrupts their circadian rhythm.

The solution is not to sleep in on weekends. The solution is to find a sustainable schedule that
respects your chronotype as much as possible while maintaining consistency. Even evening types
can adapt to earlier schedules with strategic light exposure and consistent timing, the adaptation
takes 2-3 weeks but is achievable.

Energy Architecture
Designing your day for sustained performance.

> Your energy is not a constant, it fluctuates predictably across the day based on circadian rhythms,
food intake, activity, and environmental cues. Most men experience this as random, "some days I
have energy, some days I don't." It is not random. It is architecture.

The Natural Energy Curve:

Phase 1: Cortisol Rise (Wake to +2 hours) Energy is building. Cortisol rises sharply. This is a good
time for movement, light exposure, and hydration. Not the time for deep cognitive work, your
prefrontal cortex is still warming up.

Phase 2: Peak Performance (+2 to +6 hours after waking) Cortisol has peaked. Body
temperature is rising. Cognitive function is at or near peak. This is when you do your most
demanding work - strategy, writing, problem-solving, complex decisions.

Phase 3: The Circadian Trough (+6 to +8 hours — early-mid afternoon) Body temperature dips
slightly. Alertness drops. This is the post-lunch slump, and it is biological, not just dietary. Many
cultures built napping into this window for good reason e.g. Spain, Italy etc...

Strategies: light exposure, brief walk, cold water on face, or a 10-20 minute nap (no longer - longer
naps enter deep sleep and cause grogginess).

Phase 4: Secondary Peak (+8 to +10 hours) Body temperature reaches its daily maximum.
Physical performance peaks. This is often the best window for training - reaction time, power
output, and flexibility are all highest in the late afternoon.

Phase 5: Wind-Down (+10 hours to sleep) Body temperature begins dropping. Melatonin
production initiates. This is the time to reduce stimulation, dim lights, shift to relaxing activities.
Fighting this with bright screens and stimulating content delays sleep onset.

Designing Your Day:

Map your schedule onto this framework. Schedule deep work during Phase 2. Allow for the trough
in Phase 3 (do not schedule important meetings or decisions here if you can avoid it). Train during
Phase 4 when possible. Begin wind-down during Phase 5.

Caffeine Strategy:

Caffeine has a half-life of approximately 5-6 hours — meaning half the caffeine from a 3pm coffee is
still in your system at 8-9pm. A quarter is still active at 1-2am.

The Apollo Caffeine Protocol:

  • First coffee: 60-90 minutes after waking (allow cortisol to peak naturally first — caffeine during
   the cortisol peak is less effective and builds tolerance faster)

  • Last coffee at midday.

  • Total daily intake: 200-400mg (2-4 cups of coffee). Do not exceed 400mg unless you have a
   specific reason and have verified it does not affect your sleep. I personally stick to two
   maximum.

Meal Timing for Energy:

  • Insulin sensitivity is highest in the morning. If you are eating breakfast (not fasting), this is when
   your body handles carbohydrates most efficiently.

  • Large meals during the circadian trough (early afternoon) worsen the energy dip. Keep lunch
   moderate. If you need carbs, bias them toward breakfast and post-training.

  • Evening meals should be moderate, enough to support recovery but not so large they disrupt
   sleep onset. Finish eating 2-3 hours before bed.

Advanced Sleep Troubleshooting
Solving persistent sleep issues.

> If you have implemented everything from Phase 1 and Phase 2 sleep protocols and are still
struggling, this lesson addresses the most common remaining issues.

Problem: Falling Asleep Takes More Than 20 Minutes

Possible causes:

  • Hyperarousal — the nervous system is still activated. The most common cause. Solution:
   extend your wind-down routine. Add a hot bath or shower 60-90 minutes before bed (the
   subsequent temperature drop triggers sleep onset). Practice the 4-7-8 breathing protocol in bed.
   Consider magnesium L-threonate specifically for cognitive calming.

  • Circadian misalignment — you are going to bed before your body is ready. Solution: track
   when you naturally feel drowsy. If it is consistently 30+ minutes after your target bedtime, you
   may need to shift your circadian phase earlier with morning light exposure and gradually earlier
   wake times (shift by 15 minutes every 3 days).

  • Conditioned arousal — you have trained your brain to associate the bed with wakefulness (from
   scrolling, watching content, or lying awake worrying). Solution: use the bed only for sleep. If you
   are not asleep within 20 minutes, get up, go to another room, do something boring in dim light,
   and return when sleepy.

Problem: Waking Up at 3-4 AM and Cannot Fall Back Asleep

This is often a cortisol pattern issue. Blood sugar dropping overnight can trigger a cortisol spike that
wakes you. Solutions:

  • Ensure you are not in an excessive caloric deficit - undereating disrupts sleep

  • Have a small snack with protein and fat before bed (Greek yoghurt, small handful of nuts)

  • If stress is the driver, journalling before bed to "download" worries can prevent the 3am cortisol
   surge from racing thoughts

Problem: Sleeping Enough Hours But Waking Tired

This suggests poor sleep quality rather than insufficient duration:

  • Sleep apnea — If you snore heavily, gasp during sleep, get screened. Undiagnosed sleep apnea
   destroys recovery regardless of sleep duration.

  • Alcohol — Even 1-2 drinks disrupts sleep architecture. If you drink regularly, try 30 days alcohol-
   free and compare sleep quality. My recommendation is to cut it out completely.

  • Late training — Intense training within 3 hours of bed elevates cortisol and body temperature,
   reducing deep sleep quality.

  • Mouth breathing during sleep — revisit the mouth taping protocol from Module 1.

Problem: Variable Sleep Quality Night to Night

Consistency is the answer. If your sleep timing varies by more than 30-60 minutes day to day, this
alone can explain variable quality. Lock your wake time, this is more important than your bedtime.
Your body adjusts bedtime based on sleep pressure, but the wake time anchors the circadian
rhythm.

When to Seek Professional Help:

If you have consistently applied the protocols in this programme for 4+ weeks and still experience:
chronic difficulty falling or staying asleep, excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate sleep
time, loud snoring or observed breathing pauses during sleep, or restless/periodic limb movements
during sleep - see a sleep specialist. Some sleep disorders require professional intervention beyond
lifestyle optimisation.
