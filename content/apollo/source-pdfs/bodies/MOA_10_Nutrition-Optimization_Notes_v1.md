Caloric Precision When Needed

Introduction
Phase 1 gave you the foundation - protein prioritisation, the Apollo Plate Method, whole foods,
eliminating liquid calories. You have spent 30 days building intuition around food quality and meal
composition.

Now we add precision tools.

This module is not about making nutrition more complicated. It is about giving you the ability to dial
things in when needed. Most of the time, the Phase 1 principles are enough. But there are moments
- a stubborn plateau, a specific body composition goal, preparation for an event - when precision
matters.

Think of this as expanding your toolkit. A craftsman does not use every tool for every job. But when
he needs a specific tool, he has it and knows how to use it.

You will learn when and how to actually track calories, how to manipulate macronutrients
strategically, the real science of nutrient timing, how to eat differently for different goals, which
advanced supplements are worth considering, and how to troubleshoot when things are not
working.

> Use these tools when needed.

Return to intuitive eating when precision is not required. The goal is competence across the
spectrum.

When to Track Calories
Phase 1 deliberately avoided calorie counting. For most people most of the time, focusing on food
quality and protein intake handles calories automatically. Tracking every morsel creates an unhealthy
relationship with food and is unsustainable long-term.

But there are times when precision matters. Knowing how to accurately assess and manipulate
caloric intake is a skill worth having.

When to Track Calories

Track when:

 1. You have plateaued for 3+ weeks despite consistent effort. Tracking reveals whether the issue
   is intake, and by how much.

 2. You are starting a dedicated fat loss phase. Knowing your actual intake versus your target
   ensures you are in a real deficit.

 3. You are struggling to gain weight. Hard gainers often dramatically overestimate intake.
   Tracking reveals the truth.

 4. You need to hit a specific target for an event. Photoshoots, competitions, or meaningful
   deadlines benefit from precision.

 5. You want to build awareness. A few weeks of tracking teaches you what foods contain and
   recalibrates your intuition.

Do not track when:

 1. You are making consistent progress. If it is not broken, do not add complexity.

 2. Tracking triggers obsessive or anxious behaviour. Mental health trumps precision.

 3. You are in a maintenance phase with no specific goals. Intuitive eating is sufficient.

 4. You have tracked before and have solid food intuition. Once you know, you know.

Calculating Your Actual Caloric Needs
Forget the online calculators. They provide rough estimates at best. Here is how to find your real
maintenance calories:

Step 1: Track Current Intake

For 7-10 days, track everything you eat without changing your habits. Weigh food when possible.
Log accurately. Include weekends and any deviations.

Calculate your average daily intake across this period.

Step 2: Track Body Weight

Weigh yourself daily under consistent conditions (morning, after bathroom, before eating). Calculate
your weekly average.

Compare the weekly average at the start and end of your tracking period.

Step 3: Calculate Maintenance

If your weight stayed stable: your average intake equals your maintenance calories.

If you gained weight: your maintenance is lower than your average intake. Subtract approximately
500 calories per 0.5kg gained over the tracking period.

If you lost weight: your maintenance is higher than your average intake. Add approximately 500
calories per 0.5kg lost.

> This method gives you YOUR actual maintenance based on YOUR metabolism, activity level, and
lifestyle - not a formula's guess.

Setting Caloric Targets
Once you know maintenance:

For fat loss: Subtract 20-25% from maintenance. This creates a deficit of roughly 300-500 calories
for most men, producing 0.5-0.75kg loss per week - aggressive enough for progress, sustainable
enough to maintain muscle and sanity.

Example: 2800 maintenance → 2300-2500 target for cutting

For maintenance: Eat at your calculated maintenance. Adjust based on weekly weight trends.

For muscle gain: Add 10-15% to maintenance. This creates a modest surplus for muscle growth
while minimizing fat gain. Larger surpluses do not build muscle faster - they just add more fat.

Example: 2800 maintenance → 3000-3200 target for gaining

The Precision Tracking Protocol
When you do track, do it properly:

 1. Use a food scale. Eyeballing portions is wildly inaccurate. A food scale costs little and changes
   everything.

 2. Weigh food raw when possible. Cooking changes weight. A 200g raw chicken breast is not
   200g cooked.

 3. Log as you go. Do not try to remember at the end of the day. Log before or immediately after
   eating.

 4. Include everything. The oil you cook with. The sauce on the side. The handful of nuts. Small
   things add up.

 5. Use reliable database entries. Check that the entry matches your food. Verify macros make
   sense.

 6. Track consistently for the assessment period. Sporadic tracking teaches nothing.

Moving Beyond Tracking
Tracking is a tool, not a lifestyle. The goal is to build awareness and hit specific targets, then return
to intuitive eating with better calibration.

After 4-8 weeks of tracking:

  • You will know what portion sizes actually look like

  • You will have memorized the macros of foods you eat regularly

  • You will be able to estimate intake with reasonable accuracy

  • You can return to intuitive eating with precision available when needed

Track in phases. Do not track forever.

Macronutrient Manipulation
Calories determine whether you gain or lose weight. Macronutrients determine what that weight is
made of and how you feel along the way.

> Phase 1 established protein as the priority. That does not change. But the manipulation of
carbohydrates and fats around training and goals can optimise results.

The Hierarchy of Nutritional Importance

 1. Calories - Primary determinant of weight change

 2. Protein - Primary determinant of muscle retention/growth

 3. Carbohydrates and Fats - Secondary factors that affect performance, hormones, and
    adherence

Do not obsess over carb/fat ratios while ignoring calories and protein. Get the big rocks right first.

Protein: The Constant

Your protein target remains 0.8-1g per pound of bodyweight regardless of phase. During fat loss,
push toward the higher end to maximise muscle retention. During gaining, the lower end is
sufficient.

> Protein stays relatively constant. Carbs and fats are the variables you manipulate.

Carbohydrates: The Performance Fuel

Carbohydrates are your body's preferred fuel for high-intensity activity. Glycogen stored in muscles
powers your training.

Adequate carbs support:

  • Training performance and intensity

  • Recovery and glycogen replenishment

  • Hormonal function (very low carb can suppress thyroid and testosterone)

  • Sleep quality

  • Mood and cognitive function

When to emphasise carbs:

  • Training days, especially around workouts

  • High-volume training phases

  • When performance is priority

  • When energy and mood are suffering on lower carbs

When to reduce carbs:

  • Rest days (lower energy demand)

  • Fat loss phases (creates deficit while protecting protein)

  • Sedentary periods

  • If you respond well to lower carb approaches

Fats: The Hormonal Foundation

Dietary fat is essential for:

  • Hormone production (testosterone, estrogen, cortisol all require cholesterol)

  • Cell membrane integrity

  • Absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)

  • Satiety and meal satisfaction

  • Brain function

Minimum fat intake should not drop below 0.2g per pound of bodyweight, even during aggressive
dieting. Below this, hormonal function suffers.

When to emphasise fats:

  • Rest days (when carb need is lower)

  • If you feel better and more satisfied on higher fat

  • If you have hormonal concerns

  • Lower carb dietary approaches

When to moderate fats:

  • Training days (prioritise carbs for performance)

  • When calories are very restricted (fat is calorie-dense)

  • If you tend to overeat high-fat foods

Carb Cycling: The Practical Application
Carb cycling means varying carbohydrate intake based on activity level. Higher carbs on training
days, lower carbs on rest days. This is not magic, it is logical matching of fuel to demand.

Simple Carb Cycling Protocol:

Calculate your weekly carb budget based on your caloric targets and protein/fat needs.

Distribute more carbs to training days, fewer to rest days.

Example for someone eating 2200 calories during a cut:

  • Protein: 150g-180 daily (500-720 calories) - constant

  • Fat: 50-70g daily (400-650 calories) - relatively constant

  • Carbs: ~210g average daily (850 calories) - variable

Distribution:

  • 4 training days: 250g carbs each

  • 3 rest days: 160g carbs each

  • Weekly average: 211g carbs

> This keeps weekly calories identical while optimising fuel around training.

Advanced Carb Cycling
High/Medium/Low day approach:

  • High carb days (heavy training): 3g per kg bodyweight

  • Medium carb days (moderate training): 2g per kg bodyweight

  • Low carb days (rest or light activity): 1g per kg bodyweight

> Adjust fat inversely to keep calories consistent - higher fat on low carb days, lower fat on high
carb days.

Protein Pulsing
Some research suggests that varying protein intake - higher on training days, slightly lower on rest
days - may optimize muscle protein synthesis while allowing the anabolic response to remain
sensitive.

This is advanced and the evidence is not definitive. For most people, consistent daily protein is
simpler and equally effective.

If you want to experiment:

  • Training days: Higher end 1g per pound of bodyweight

  • Rest days: Lower end 0.8g per pound of bodyweight

Ensure weekly protein intake remains adequate.

Fat Intake Optimisation
Prioritise fat quality over quantity manipulation:

Emphasise:

  • Monounsaturated fats (olive oil, avocados, nuts)

  • Omega-3 fatty acids (fatty fish, fish oil)

  • Saturated fats from whole food sources (eggs, meat, dairy) - in moderation

Minimize:

  • Industrial seed oils (soybean, corn, canola - especially when heated)

  • Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils)

  • Excessive omega-6 fatty acids

> The omega-3 to omega-6 ratio matters for inflammation and recovery. Most modern diets are
heavily skewed toward omega-6. Prioritise omega-3 sources.

Timing
Does when you eat matter? The fitness industry has swung from "meal timing is everything" to
"meal timing doesn't matter at all." The truth is in between.

The Hierarchy of Importance

 1. Total daily intake (calories and protein) - accounts for ~60% of results

 2. Food quality and macronutrient composition - accounts for ~30% of results

 3. Meal timing and frequency - accounts for ~10% of results

Nutrient timing is the fine-tuning, not the foundation. A perfect meal timing strategy cannot
overcome inadequate total intake. But once the foundation is solid, timing can provide marginal
improvements.

> For most people, those marginal improvements are not worth obsessing over. For those pushing
for optimised results, they matter.

Pre-Workout Nutrition
The Goal: Provide fuel for training without GI distress.

Timing: 1-3 hours before training, depending on meal size.

  • Large meal: 2-3 hours before

  • Moderate meal: 1-2 hours before

  • Small snack: 30-60 minutes before

Composition:

  • Protein: 20-40g to elevate amino acid availability

  • Carbohydrates: 30-60g for glycogen availability

  • Fat: Low to moderate (slows digestion, can cause discomfort)

Example pre-workout meals:

2-3 hours before:

  • Chicken breast, rice, and vegetables

  • Lean beef with potatoes and salad

  • Fish with pasta and olive oil

1-2 hours before:

  • Greek yogurt with fruit and granola

  • Turkey sandwich on whole grain bread

  • Protein shake with banana and oats

30-60 minutes before:

  • Banana and small protein shake

  • Rice cakes with honey

  • Fruit and a handful of nuts

> If training fasted: See Module 2 protocols. Ensure post-workout nutrition is prioritised.

Intra-Workout Nutrition
For most sessions under 75 minutes, water is sufficient.

For longer or very intense sessions, intra-workout nutrition can help:

When to consider intra-workout nutrition:

  • Sessions over 90 minutes

  • Very high volume training (20+ sets)

  • Two-a-day training

  • Training in significant caloric deficit

  • When performance noticeably drops mid-session

Options:

  • EAAs (Essential Amino Acids): 10-15g sipped throughout

  • Fast-digesting carbs: 20-40g eg dates, honey

  • Combination: EAAs + carbs for extended intense sessions

> For standard 45-75 minute sessions, do not overcomplicate. Water and good pre-workout
nutrition are sufficient.

Post-Workout Nutrition
The Anabolic Window: Myth vs Reality

The old-school belief that you must consume protein within 30 minutes of training or lose your gains
is overstated. Research shows the "anabolic window" is much longer than previously thought - likely
4-6 hours surrounding training, not 30 minutes after.

However, post-workout nutrition still matters. Training increases muscle protein synthesis, and
amino acids are needed to capitalize on this elevated state. Waiting 6+ hours to eat after training is
suboptimal.

Practical recommendation: Consume a protein-rich meal within 2-3 hours of training. Sooner is
slightly better than later, but the difference is marginal.

Post-Workout Composition:

  • Protein: 30-50g (essential for MPS)

  • Carbohydrates: 30-60g (replenishes glycogen, enhances protein uptake via insulin)

  • Fat: Moderate (does not need to be avoided, but does not need emphasis)

Post-workout meal examples:

  • Chicken stir-fry with rice

  • Steak with potatoes and vegetables

  • Eggs, toast, and fruit

Does Post-Workout Carb Speed Matter?
The idea that you need "fast-digesting" carbs post-workout is overblown. Unless you are training
again within 8 hours, glycogen replenishment is not time-sensitive. Whole food carbs are fine.

> If you train twice daily or have another session within 8 hours, prioritise faster-digesting carbs
(white rice, potatoes, fruit) over slower options (oats, whole grains, legumes).

Pre-Sleep Nutrition
You will spend 7-9 hours without food while sleeping. Pre-sleep nutrition can support overnight
recovery.

Recommendations:

  • Include protein in your final meal or have a dedicated pre-sleep protein serving

  • Slow-digesting protein sources are ideal: Cottage cheese, Greek yogurt

  • 30-40g protein before bed has been shown to improve overnight muscle protein synthesis

Avoid:

  • Very large meals immediately before bed (disrupts sleep quality)

  • High sugar intake before bed (blood sugar fluctuations)

  • Excessive fluid (nighttime waking)

   **How many meals per day?**

Meal Frequency
For muscle protein synthesis: Research suggests 3-5 protein feedings spread throughout the day
optimises MPS better than 1-2 large feedings or 6+ small feedings. The "muscle full" effect limits
how much protein can stimulate MPS at once (~40-60g), so spreading intake helps.

For fat loss: Meal frequency does not significantly affect fat loss when calories are equated. Choose
the frequency that helps you adhere to your caloric target.

For muscle gain: Higher meal frequency (4-5 meals) can make it easier to consume adequate
calories without feeling stuffed.

> Practical recommendation: 3-4 meals containing 30-50g protein each, spaced 3-5 hours apart.
Adjust based on your eating window if using IF.

Performance-Focused Nutrition
Your nutritional approach should match your current priority. Training for maximum gym
performance requires different fuelling than dieting for a photoshoot. Understanding these
differences allows you to shift strategies as goals change.

When performance is the priority - you want to lift more, train harder, recover faster - nutrition should
support output.

Characteristics:

  • Calories at maintenance or slight surplus

  • Higher carbohydrate intake (3-5g per kg bodyweight)

  • Carbs concentrated around training

  • Adequate fat for hormonal function (0.8-1.2g per kg)

  • Protein at standard targets

Performance nutrition principles:

 1. Never train underfuelled. Pre-workout nutrition is non-negotiable. Glycogen availability directly
   impacts performance.

 2. Prioritise recovery nutrition. Post-workout carbs and protein support adaptation to training.

 3. Do not fear carbohydrates. Carbs are performance fuel. Low-carb approaches compromise
   high-intensity output.

 4. Eat enough. Chronic undereating degrades performance over time. If you are not recovering,
   you may not be eating enough.

 5. Hydration matters. Even mild dehydration impairs strength and power. Drink consistently
   throughout the day.

Example performance day (80kg male, training day):

  • Calories: 3000-3200

  • Protein: 150 - 180g

  • Carbs: 300 - 350g

  • Fat: 80 - 100g

Meals:

  • Breakfast: Eggs, oatmeal, fruit (500 cal)

  • Lunch: Chicken, rice, vegetables (700 cal)

  • Pre-workout: Banana, protein shake (350 cal)

  • Post-workout: Steak, potatoes, salad (800 cal)

  • Evening: Greek yogurt, nuts, berries (450 cal)

Aesthetics-Focused Nutrition
When aesthetics are the priority - you want to get leaner, more defined, more visually impressive -
nutrition shifts toward creating and sustaining a caloric deficit while preserving muscle.

Characteristics:

  • Calories in deficit (20-25% below maintenance)

  • Protein at higher end to preserve muscle

  • Carbohydrates reduced but not eliminated (2-3g per kg)

  • Fats at minimum for hormonal health (0.5-0.8g per kg)

  • Strategic carb placement around training

Aesthetics nutrition principles:

 1. Protein is non-negotiable. When calories are restricted, protein preserves muscle. Do not let
   protein slip.

 2. Protect training performance. Place available carbs around workouts to maintain training
   quality.

 3. Accept some hunger. Fat loss requires deficit. Deficit creates hunger. Learn to distinguish real
   hunger from habit.

 4. Use volume eating. High-volume, low-calorie foods (vegetables, lean proteins, fruits) create
   fullness without caloric excess.

 5. Plan for sustainability. Extreme restriction backfires. Moderate deficits maintained over time
   beat aggressive restriction abandoned after two weeks.

Example aesthetics day (80kg male, cutting):

  • Calories: 2000-2200

  • Protein: 180g

  • Carbs: 180g

  • Fat: 65g

Meals:

  • Meal 1 (12pm): Eggs, vegetables, fruit (400 cal)

  • Meal 2 (4pm): Chicken breast, rice, large salad (550 cal)

  • Pre/Post-workout (around training): Protein shake, banana (300 cal)

  • Meal 3 (8pm): Fish, potatoes, vegetables (550 cal)

  • Pre-bed: Cottage cheese (200 cal)

   Advanced Supplementation

The Transition Between Phases
Do not swing dramatically between performance and aesthetics nutrition. Transition gradually.

From performance to aesthetics (starting a cut):

  • Week 1: Reduce calories by 10%

  • Week 2: Reduce another 10%

  • Week 3: Settle into target deficit

  • Increase protein as calories decrease

  • Reduce carbs first, protect fats until minimum threshold

From aesthetics to performance (ending a cut):

  • Reverse diet: Add 100-150 calories per week

  • Gradually increase carbohydrates

  • Monitor body weight - some regain is normal and expected

  • Do not immediately jump to huge surplus

> Gradual transitions protect metabolic rate, prevent rapid fat regain, and allow psychological
adjustment.

Maintenance: The Underrated Phase

> Many men cycle endlessly between cutting and bulking without spending time at maintenance.
This is a mistake.

Maintenance phases allow:

  • Metabolic rate to stabilise after dieting

  • Psychological relief from restriction

  • Training performance to peak without deficit interference

  • Hormonal recovery from dieting stress

  • Consolidation of new body composition

> Spend at least 2-4 weeks at maintenance between cutting and gaining phases. Longer
maintenance phases (2-3 months) after extended diets allow better long-term results.

Further Supplementation?
Phase 1 covered the foundation - creatine, vitamin D, omega-3s, magnesium, zinc. These remain
your Tier 1 priorities. If you are not taking these consistently, advanced supplements are premature.

> This lesson covers Tier 2 and Tier 3 options - supplements with solid evidence that may provide
benefits for specific situations. None of these are necessary. All of them are secondary to food,
training, sleep, and Tier 1 supplements.

   **Protein Powder**

Tier 2: Evidence-Based Options
These have research support and meaningful effects for specific goals.

What it does: Adaptogen that modulates the stress response. Reduces cortisol, may improve
testosterone, enhances recovery from training.

Evidence: Multiple RCTs showing reduced cortisol (14-28%), improved recovery, modest
testosterone increases in stressed individuals.

Who benefits: Men with high stress, poor recovery, elevated cortisol, or difficulty sleeping. Less
benefit for already low-stress individuals.

Dose: 300-600mg daily of standardised extract (KSM-66 or Sensoril)

Timing: Evening or split AM/PM

> Notes: One of the most well-supported adaptogens. Effects build over 4-8 weeks.

What it does: Central nervous system stimulant. Improves alertness, reduces perceived exertion,
enhances endurance and strength performance.

Evidence: Extensively researched. Consistent performance benefits of 2-5% across various metrics.

Who benefits: Most people, unless caffeine-sensitive or consuming excessive amounts.

Dose: 3-6mg per kg bodyweight, 30-60 minutes before training

Timing: Pre-workout. Avoid within 8 hours of sleep.

> Notes: Tolerance develops with chronic use. Consider cycling or periodic reduction to maintain
sensitivity. My personal cut off is at midday and don't generally use it as a preworkout for afternoon
or evening sessions, only if I train in the mornings.

What it does: Increases nitric oxide production, improving blood flow. Enhances endurance and
reduces fatigue during high-rep training.

Evidence: Good evidence for improved performance in higher rep ranges (8+), less clear benefit for
low-rep strength work.

Who benefits: Those doing high-volume or hypertrophy-focused training. Pump-chasers.

Dose: 6-8g citrulline malate (or 3-4g L-citrulline) 30-60 minutes pre-workout

Timing: Pre-workout

> Notes: More effective than arginine for nitric oxide. Effects are acute (each session), not
cumulative.

What it does: Increases carnosine levels in muscle, buffering acid accumulation during high-intensity
exercise.

Evidence: Good evidence for improved performance in activities lasting 1-4 minutes. Benefits
endurance in moderate-to-high rep ranges.

Who benefits: Those doing high-rep training, circuit work, or conditioning with repeated high-
intensity efforts.

Dose: 3-6g daily. Does not need to be timed around training - carnosine builds over weeks.

Timing: Any time. Can split dose to reduce tingling sensation.

> Notes: The tingling (paresthesia) is harmless. Loading phase optional but speeds saturation.

What it does: Convenient protein source for meeting daily targets.

Evidence: Protein is protein. No magic in powder form. Benefits come from meeting protein targets,
which powder makes easier.

Who benefits: Anyone struggling to hit protein targets from whole foods, busy individuals, those who
need post-workout convenience.

Types:

  • Whey concentrate: Cheapest, effective, may cause issues for lactose-sensitive

  • Whey isolate: Lower lactose, faster absorption, higher protein per gram

  • Casein: Slow digesting, ideal for pre-sleep

  • Plant blends: For those avoiding dairy, combine sources for complete amino profile

Dose: As needed to meet protein targets. Typically 25-50g per serving.

> Notes: Whole food protein is preferable when practical. Powder is a convenience tool. Like I've
said before, if you choose any protein powder go with the unflavoured option with minimal additives
that means no artificial sweeteners etc...

   **Boron**

Tier 3: Situational Options
These have evidence but niche applications. Consider only if Tier 1 and 2 are covered and you have
specific needs.

What it does: Regulates sleep-wake cycle. Helps with sleep onset.

Who benefits: Those with delayed sleep phase, jet lag, or shift work. Not effective for general
insomnia unrelated to circadian disruption.

Dose: 0.3-1mg, 30-60 minutes before bed. Higher doses are not more effective and may cause
grogginess.

> Notes: Use sparingly. Not for nightly long-term use. Address sleep hygiene first.

What it does: May support testosterone levels, particularly in stressed or aging men.

Evidence: Some positive RCTs, particularly in stressed populations. Effects modest.

Who benefits: Men with suboptimal testosterone or high stress. Less benefit for healthy young men
with normal levels.

Dose: 200-400mg daily of standardised extract

> Notes: Quality varies dramatically between products. Source reputable standardised extracts.

What it does: Adaptogen that may reduce fatigue and improve stress resilience.

Evidence: Some positive studies on fatigue and stress. Less robust than ashwagandha.

Who benefits: Those with high fatigue, mental stress, or demanding work + training schedule.

Dose: 200-600mg daily of standardised extract

> Notes: Can be stimulating. Take in the morning.

What it does: Trace mineral that may support testosterone and reduce SHBG (increasing free
testosterone).

Evidence: Some positive studies, but effects are modest.

Who benefits: Those potentially deficient, older men, those seeking modest testosterone
optimisation.

Dose: 6-10mg daily

> Notes: Easy to obtain from diet (fruits, nuts, vegetables). Supplement if dietary intake is low.

What to Avoid...
Testosterone boosters (proprietary blends): Most are underdosed combinations with no
meaningful effect. Save your money.

Fat burners: Stimulant combinations that create jitters without meaningful fat loss. The effective
ingredients (caffeine, green tea extract) can be purchased separately and cheaply.

Mass gainers: Expensive carbohydrate powders. Real food is cheaper and better. Also this stuff is
full of bs...

BCAAs (Branched-Chain Amino Acids): Redundant if you consume adequate protein. EAAs are
superior if amino acid supplementation is needed.

Most "pre-workouts": Proprietary blends of underdosed ingredients plus excessive caffeine. Build
your own with caffeine, citrulline, and beta-alanine if desired.

The Supplement Hierarchy
Before adding anything beyond Tier 1:

 1. Is your diet dialled in? Supplements cannot fix poor nutrition.

 2. Is your sleep optimised? No supplement replaces quality sleep.

 3. Is your training consistent? Supplements are the last 1-2%, not a shortcut.

 4. Are you taking Tier 1 consistently? Foundation first.

> If yes to all four, Tier 2 supplements may provide marginal benefits. If no to any, fix the foundation
before adding complexity.

Fat Loss Problem
Despite best efforts, problems arise. Plateaus happen. Energy crashes. Digestive issues. This lesson
provides diagnostic frameworks for common nutrition problems.

Problem: Fat Loss Has Stalled

You have been in a deficit, losing weight, and now progress has stopped for 2+ weeks.

Diagnostic Questions:

 1. Are you actually in a deficit?

  • Has tracking accuracy slipped? Weekend overeating? Liquid calories creeping in?

  • Solution: Track meticulously for one week. Weigh everything. Include everything.

 1. Has your metabolism adapted?

  • Extended dieting reduces metabolic rate. Your "deficit" may now be maintenance.

  • Solution: Recalculate needs. Either reduce calories further (if sustainable) or implement a diet
   break.

 1. Are you retaining water?

  • Increased cortisol from dieting stress causes water retention, masking fat loss.

  • Solution: Diet break at maintenance for 1-2 weeks. Often produces a "whoosh" as water
   releases.

 1. Are you sleeping poorly?

  • Poor sleep elevates cortisol and disrupts hunger hormones.

  • Solution: Prioritise sleep quality before adjusting nutrition further.

 1. Have you been dieting too long?

  • Extended deficits cause adaptive thermogenesis and hormonal downregulation.

  • Solution: Take 2-4 weeks at maintenance, then resume if needed.

The Diet Break Protocol
When fat loss stalls after 8+ weeks of dieting:

  • Raise calories to maintenance for 1-2 weeks

  • Keep protein high

  • Increase carbohydrates primarily

  • Maintain training

  • Allow weight to stabilise (some increase is normal - mostly water/glycogen)

  • Resume deficit after the break

Diet breaks restore metabolic rate, reduce cortisol, improve leptin signalling, and provide
psychological relief.

Problem: Can't Gain Weight

You are trying to build muscle but the scale will not move despite "eating a lot."

Diagnostic Questions:

 1. Are you actually eating enough?

  • Hard gainers consistently overestimate intake.

  • Solution: Track everything for two weeks. Most "hard gainers" discover they eat far less than
   they thought.

 1. Are you consistent daily?

  • Eating big on Monday then barely eating Wednesday averages out to inadequate.

  • Solution: Consistency matters more than occasional big meals.

 1. Are you burning too much?

  • Excessive cardio, active job can outpace intake.

  • Solution: Reduce energy expenditure or increase intake to compensate.

Hard Gainer Protocol
 1. Calculate needs accurately (track and assess as per Lesson 1)

 2. Add 300-500 calories above true maintenance

 3. Prioritise calorie-dense foods: nuts, oils, dried fruit, whole milk, fattier meats

 4. Drink some calories: shakes, milk, smoothies

 5. Eat on schedule regardless of hunger

 6. Reduce unnecessary cardio

 7. Track weight weekly - adjust if not gaining

Problem: Low Energy

You feel fatigued, workouts suffer, and daily energy is poor.

Diagnostic Questions:

 1. Are you eating enough?

  • Chronic undereating causes fatigue. This is common during cuts.

  • Solution: Assess whether deficit is too aggressive. Consider diet break or smaller deficit.

 1. Are carbohydrates too low?

  • Very low carb diets can cause fatigue, especially initially.

  • Solution: Increase carbohydrates, particularly around training.

 1. Is fat too low?

  • Below 0.5g/kg fat can impair hormonal function.

  • Solution: Ensure minimum fat intake is met.

 1. Are you deficient in micronutrients?

  • Iron, B12, vitamin D deficiencies cause fatigue.

  • Solution: Assess diet variety. Consider blood work if persistent.

 1. Is it actually nutrition?

  • Sleep deprivation and overtraining cause fatigue that nutrition cannot fix.

  • Solution: Assess sleep and training load before blaming food.

Problem: Digestive Issues
Bloating, gas, discomfort, irregular bowel movements.

Diagnostic Questions:

 1. Did you change something recently?

  • New foods, increased fiber, new supplements can cause temporary issues.

  • Solution: Introduce changes gradually. Give your gut time to adapt.

 1. Are you eating too fast?

  • Rushed eating impairs digestion and increases air swallowing.

  • Solution: Slow down. Chew thoroughly. Eat without distraction.

 1. Is fibre too high or too low?

  • Both extremes cause issues. Optimal is typically 25-40g daily.

  • Solution: Adjust fiber gradually toward optimal range.

 1. Are you consuming problematic foods?

  • Common culprits: dairy, gluten, FODMAPs, artificial sweeteners, excess fiber.

  • Solution: Elimination protocol - remove suspect foods for 2-3 weeks, reintroduce one at a time.

 1. Are you hydrating adequately?

  • High fibre with low water causes constipation and discomfort.

  • Solution: Increase water intake, especially with fibre increase.

   **Problem: Constant Hunger**

Digestive Optimisation Protocol
 1. Eat slowly, chew thoroughly

 2. Include fermented foods (yogurt, sauerkraut, kimchi)

 3. Maintain consistent meal timing

 4. Ensure adequate but not excessive fibre (25-40g)

 5. Stay hydrated (3+ litres daily)

 6. Identify and limit personal trigger foods

 7. Consider digestive enzymes if issues persist

You are always hungry despite eating adequate calories.

Diagnostic Questions:

 1. Is protein high enough?

  • Protein is the most satiating macronutrient.

  • Solution: Ensure protein is at 0.8-1g/pound of bodyweight

 1. Are you eating enough volume?

  • Low-volume, calorie-dense foods do not fill you up.

  • Solution: Add high-volume, low-calorie foods: vegetables, salads, lean proteins, fruits.

 1. Are you eating enough fibre?

  • Fibre increases satiety.

  • Solution: Include fibre at every meal.

 1. Are you actually hungry or just craving?

  • Cravings for specific foods differ from genuine hunger.

  • Solution: If craving something specific (sweets, chips), it is often psychological not physiological.

 1. Are you sleeping enough?

  • Sleep deprivation increases ghrelin and hunger.

  • Solution: Prioritise sleep.

Satiety Protocol
  • Start every meal with protein and vegetables

  • Drink water before and during meals

  • Eat slowly - satiety signals take 20 minutes

  • Include fibre at every meal

  • Use larger plates with more food volume, not more calories

  • Manage sleep and stress (both increase hunger)

  • Differentiate hunger from boredom, habit, or emotion
