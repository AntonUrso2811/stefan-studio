Recomp vs Cut vs Bulk - The Decision Framework

Introduction
You have the training knowledge. You have the nutrition tools. You understand recovery. Now we
bring it all together into strategic approaches for changing your physique.

Body recomposition is the holy grail - building muscle while losing fat simultaneously. It is possible,
but not for everyone in every situation. Understanding when recomp works, when to cut, and when
to bulk allows you to make intelligent decisions about your approach rather than spinning your
wheels with the wrong strategy.

This module gives you the framework to assess where you are, decide what approach fits your
situation, and execute that approach with precision.

> No more guessing. No more following generic advice that does not apply to you.

By the end of this module, you will know exactly what approach to take and how to execute it.

Which ones what?
Three paths exist for changing your physique:

Recomposition: Building muscle and losing fat simultaneously. Eating around maintenance calories
while training hard.

Cutting: Prioritizing fat loss. Eating in a caloric deficit while preserving muscle through training and
protein.

Bulking: Prioritizing muscle gain. Eating in a caloric surplus while training to maximize hypertrophy.

Each has its place. The right choice depends on your current situation, your goals, and your
timeline.

When Recomposition Works
Recomposition - gaining muscle while losing fat at the same time - sounds ideal. But it requires
specific conditions to work effectively.

Recomp works best for:

 1. Beginners (first 1-2 years of proper training) Untrained individuals have massive adaptation
   potential. Their bodies respond dramatically to the novel stimulus of resistance training. Muscle
   gain is rapid. Fat loss occurs easily with improved activity and basic nutrition. This is the "newbie
   gains" window - exploit it.

 2. Returning lifters (muscle memory) If you trained seriously before and took significant time off,
   you can regain lost muscle faster than building new muscle. Your body remembers. During this
   regain phase, simultaneous fat loss is achievable. This was the case for me. I fell off the wagon
   and lost track of my physique but then I got back in the gym and due to muscle memory I was
   able to progress fast.

 3. Overfat beginners/intermediates If you carry significant body fat (over 20-25%), your body has
   ample energy reserves to support muscle building even without a surplus. The higher your body
   fat, the more viable recomp becomes.

When to Cut
Cutting - dedicated fat loss while preserving muscle - is the right choice when:

 1. You are over 18-20% body fat Higher body fat impairs insulin sensitivity, nutrient partitioning,
   and hormonal profile. Getting leaner improves all of these, setting you up for better muscle gain
   later. Cut first, then build from a leaner base.

 2. You have specific aesthetic goals with a timeline Vacation, event, photoshoot, competition -
   when you need to look a certain way by a certain date, a dedicated cut is most efficient.

 3. You have finished a gaining phase and added too much fat Bulking inevitably adds some fat.
   When you have reached your target weight or body fat has crept too high, cut back down before
   continuing to build.

 4. You are an intermediate or advanced lifter who wants to get leaner Recomp is too slow. A
   dedicated cut achieves fat loss faster while proper training and protein intake preserve muscle.

When to Bulk
Bulking - dedicated muscle gain with caloric surplus - is the right choice when:

 1. You are already relatively lean (under 15% body fat) Lean individuals partition nutrients
   toward muscle better than overfat individuals. Your body is primed for growth. Take advantage of
   it.

 2. You have maximized newbie gains and want more muscle After the initial adaptation phase,
   muscle gain slows. A dedicated surplus phase accelerates progress.

 3. You are an intermediate or advanced lifter prioritizing size Building muscle as a trained
   individual requires a surplus. You cannot consistently build muscle in a deficit once newbie gains
   are exhausted.

 4. You have the patience to cut afterward Bulking adds fat. This is unavoidable. Only bulk if you
   are willing to cut later to reveal what you built.

The Decision Flowchart
Ask these questions in order:

1. What is your current body fat percentage?

Over 20%: Cut first until 15% or below 15-20%: Either cut to get leaner or recomp if newer to
training Under 15%: Good position to bulk or recomp

2. How long have you been training properly?

Under 1 year: Recomp is highly viable regardless of body fat 1-2 years: Recomp still works but
slowing; consider dedicated phases Over 2 years: Dedicated cut/bulk cycles likely more effective

3. What is your primary goal right now?

Look better as fast as possible: Cut if overfat, bulk if lean and small Build maximum muscle: Bulk (if
lean enough) or cut first (if overfat) Improve body composition gradually: Recomp

4. Do you have a specific deadline?

Yes: Choose the dedicated approach (cut or bulk) that achieves the goal.

   The Recomposition Protocol

Realistic Expectations
Fat loss rates:

  • Aggressive but sustainable: 0.75-1% of bodyweight per week

  • Moderate: 0.5-0.75% per week

  • Conservative: 0.25-0.5% per week

For an 80kg man, this means 0.4-0.8kg per week is reasonable.

Muscle gain rates (natural, trained):

  • Beginner (year 1): 0.5-1kg per month possible

  • Intermediate (years 2-3): 0.25-0.5kg per month

  • Advanced (years 4+): 0.1-0.25kg per month

> These are maximums with optimal training, nutrition, and recovery. Most people achieve less.

Recomp rates:

  • Slower than dedicated approaches in both directions

  • Expect 0.25-0.5kg muscle gain per month AND 0.25-0.5kg fat loss per month

  • Scale may not move much - track with measurements and photos

The Recomp Equation
If you have determined that recomposition is the right approach for your situation, here is how to
execute it effectively.

The Recomp Equation

Recomposition requires threading a needle:

  • Enough calories to support muscle growth

  • Not so many calories that you gain fat

  • Enough protein to maximize muscle protein synthesis

  • Training stimulus that demands adaptation

> This typically means eating at or slightly below maintenance while training hard.

Caloric Targets for Recomp
Option 1: Maintenance Calories

Eat at your calculated maintenance. The energy for muscle building comes from fat stores. Works
best for those with higher body fat (18%+).

Option 2: Slight Deficit (10-15% below maintenance)

A small deficit accelerates fat loss while still allowing muscle gain in those with adaptation potential.
Works for beginners and those with moderate body fat.

Option 3: Calorie Cycling

Higher calories on training days, lower on rest days. Weekly average at or slightly below
maintenance.

Example for 2500 calorie maintenance:

  • Training days (4x): 2700 calories

  • Rest days (3x): 2200 calories

  • Weekly average: 2486 calories (slight deficit)

> This provides more fuel around training when you need it.

Protein: The Non-Negotiable
For recomp to work, protein must be high. This is not optional.

Target: 2.0-2.4g per kg of bodyweight

Higher protein during recomp serves multiple purposes:

  • Maximizes muscle protein synthesis

  • Preserves existing muscle

  • Increases satiety (important if in slight deficit)

  • Has higher thermic effect (burns more calories to digest)

An 80kg man should target 150-180g protein daily. Do not compromise on this.

Training for Recomp
The training stimulus is what drives muscle gain during recomp. Without hard training, you are just
maintaining or slowly losing.

Requirements:

 1. Progressive overload - You must be getting stronger over time. If weights are not going up,
   muscle is not being built.

 2. Adequate volume - 10-20 hard sets per muscle group per week. Toward the higher end for
   recomp since you are not in a surplus.

 3. High effort - RPE 7-9 on working sets. Recomp requires pushing hard since you do not have
   excess calories supporting recovery.

 4. Frequency - Hit each muscle group 2-3 times per week to maximize MPS opportunities.

The Phase 2 training program is designed for this. Follow it.

Cardio During Recomp
Moderate cardio supports recomp by:

  • Increasing energy expenditure (supports fat loss)

  • Improving nutrient partitioning

  • Enhancing recovery (Zone 2)

  • Supporting cardiovascular health

Recommendations:

  • 2-3 Zone 2 sessions per week (30-45 minutes)

  • 1 higher intensity session optional

  • Do not overdo it - excessive cardio increases stress and can impair muscle gain

   Strategic Fat Loss

Tracking Progress During Recomp
The scale is not your primary metric during recomp. You may be gaining muscle and losing fat at
similar rates, making scale weight stable.

Track these instead:

 1. Weekly scale weight - Note the trend even if changes are small

 2. Waist measurement - Most reliable indicator of fat change. Measure at navel, same time each
   day.

 3. Progress photos - Monthly, same lighting and conditions. Visual change is often apparent
   before scale change.

 4. Strength progression - Are your lifts going up? If yes, you are likely building muscle.

 5. How clothes fit - Pants looser in waist, tighter in thighs? Shirts tighter in shoulders, looser in
   midsection? This is recomp working.

Expected timeline:

Visible recomp results typically take 8-12 weeks of consistent execution. This is not a fast process.
Trust the process and track objectively.

Switch to cutting if:

  • Fat loss has stalled for 4+ weeks despite consistent execution

  • You want faster fat loss for an event or goal

  • Body fat is still higher than desired and progress is too slow

Switch to bulking if:

  • You have reached a lean state (12-15% body fat)

  • Muscle gain has stalled and you want to push growth

  • You have been recomping for 6+ months and want to accelerate

Continue recomping if:

  • Progress is still happening in both directions

  • You are content with the pace of change

  • You do not have specific timeline pressure

Fat loss
When it is time to cut, do it right. A well-executed cut preserves muscle, maintains performance,
and achieves sustainable fat loss. A poorly executed cut loses muscle, crashes performance, and
leads to rebound.

Setting Your Deficit

Calculate your target:

 1. Determine maintenance calories (Lesson 1 of Module 3)

 2. Subtract 20-25% for aggressive but sustainable fat loss

 3. This creates a deficit of 400-600 calories for most men

Example: 2800 maintenance → 2100-2240 calorie target

Do not go lower than this unless you are very short or very light. Larger deficits increase muscle
loss, crash hormones, and are unsustainable.

Macronutrient Targets for Cutting
Protein: 2.2-2.4g per kg bodyweight

Higher than normal. This is critical for muscle preservation during a deficit. Research consistently
shows higher protein intakes preserve more muscle during weight loss.

80kg man: 170-190g protein daily (704-768 calories from protein)

Fat: 0.5-0.8g per kg bodyweight

Minimum threshold for hormonal function. Do not go below 0.5g/kg.

80kg man: 40-64g fat daily (360-576 calories from fat)

Carbohydrates: Remaining calories

After protein and fat are set, carbs fill the remainder.

Example for 80kg man at 2100 calories:

  • Protein: 180g (720 cal)

  • Fat: 55g (495 cal)

  • Carbs: 220g (880 cal)

> Carbs are lowest during a cut because they are the most flexible macronutrient.

Protein and fat have minimum thresholds; carbs do not.

Training During a Cut
The goal: maintain strength and muscle mass.

You are not trying to build significant muscle during a cut (though some beginners will). You are
trying to keep what you have.

Key principles:

 1. Maintain intensity (weight on the bar) Do not drop weight preemptively. Continue lifting as
   heavy as you can with good form. Strength may decrease slightly, but fight to maintain it.

 2. Reduce volume if needed You are not recovering as well in a deficit. If fatigue accumulates,
   reduce sets per exercise by 1-2. Keep intensity, reduce volume.

 3. Keep compound movements Now is not the time to switch to machines and isolation only.
   Compound movements maintain the most muscle with the least volume.

 4. Do not add excessive training The urge to "do more" during a cut is counterproductive. More
   training + fewer calories = excessive stress. Trust the deficit to create fat loss.

Cardio During a Cut
Cardio is a tool for increasing energy expenditure. Use it strategically.

Recommendations:

  • Start with 2-3 Zone 2 sessions per week (30-45 min)

  • Add more only if fat loss stalls

  • Do not exceed 4-5 cardio sessions per week

  • Prioritize low-impact options (walking, cycling, rowing)

  • HIIT is optional - 1 session per week maximum

Do not use cardio to create your entire deficit. It is easier and more sustainable to eat 400 fewer
calories than to burn 400 extra through cardio.

Use a combination.

Managing Hunger
Hunger is inevitable during a cut. Manage it rather than fight it.

Strategies:

 1. High protein intake - Most satiating macronutrient

 2. High volume foods - Vegetables, salads, lean proteins, fruits. Fill your stomach without filling
    your calories.

 3. Adequate fiber - 25-40g daily for satiety and digestive health

 4. Hydration - Drink water consistently. Thirst is often mistaken for hunger.

 5. Meal timing - Some people do better with fewer larger meals, others with more frequent smaller
    meals. Experiment.

 6. Accept some hunger - You are in a deficit. Some hunger is normal. Distinguish between "I
    could eat" and genuine hunger.

 7. Sleep - Poor sleep increases hunger hormones. Protect sleep during a cut.

Diet Breaks and Refeeds
Extended dieting causes metabolic adaptation and psychological fatigue. Strategic breaks help.

Refeed days:

One day per week at maintenance calories with increased carbohydrates. This:

  • Replenishes glycogen

  • Temporarily boosts leptin

  • Provides psychological relief

  • Supports training performance

> Protocol: Increase calories to maintenance, add 50-100g carbs, keep protein high, reduce fat
slightly.

Diet breaks:

After 8-12 weeks of continuous dieting, take 1-2 weeks at maintenance calories. This:

  • Allows metabolic rate to recover

  • Reduces cortisol and stress

  • Restores hormonal function

  • Resets psychologically

> You will not lose your progress. You will come back more effective.

Ending the Cut: Reverse Dieting
Do not immediately return to high calories after a cut. Your metabolism has adapted downward.
Jumping to previous intake causes rapid fat regain.

Reverse diet protocol:

 1. Add 100-150 calories per week

 2. Primarily from carbohydrates

 3. Continue training normally

 4. Monitor weight - some increase is normal (glycogen, water)

 5. Continue until reaching new maintenance

> This allows metabolism to adjust upward while minimising fat regain.

   Lean Gaining

How Long to Cut
Recommended maximum: 12-16 weeks of continuous dieting

Beyond this, metabolic adaptation, muscle loss risk, and psychological fatigue increase significantly.

If you need to lose more fat than achievable in 16 weeks:

  • Cut for 12-16 weeks

  • Maintain for 4-8 weeks

  • Cut again if needed

> This phased approach preserves muscle and metabolism better than one extended cut.

How to lean gain?
When it is time to build muscle, do it intelligently. The goal is maximum muscle gain with minimum
fat gain. This is not an excuse to eat everything in sight.

The Surplus Sweet Spot

More calories do not mean more muscle. Beyond a certain surplus, extra calories become fat, not
muscle.

Research indicates:

  • 200-300 calorie surplus is sufficient for muscle gain in most trained individuals

  • Larger surpluses do not accelerate muscle growth

  • Larger surpluses do accelerate fat gain

Recommended surplus: 10-15% above maintenance

Example: 2800 maintenance → 3080-3220 calorie target

This is enough to support muscle growth without excessive fat accumulation.

Macronutrient Targets for Gaining

Protein: 1.6-2.0g per kg bodyweight

Lower end of the range compared to cutting. You have adequate calories; you do not need protein
as high.

80kg man: 130-160g protein daily

Fat: 0.8-1.2g per kg bodyweight

Higher than cutting. Supports hormonal function and provides calories efficiently.

80kg man: 60-90g fat daily

Carbohydrates: Remaining calories (high)

Carbs fuel training and recovery. During a surplus, carbs should be high.

Example for 80kg man at 3100 calories:

  • Protein: 150g (600 cal)

  • Fat: 85g (765 cal)

  • Carbs: 430g (1720 cal)

This high carb intake supports training intensity and glycogen replenishment.

Training for Muscle Gain

Now is the time to push training. You have the calories to support it.

Key principles:

 1. Progressive overload is primary Add weight, add reps, add sets over time. The surplus allows
   recovery from more training stress.

 2. Higher volume is tolerable 15-25 sets per muscle group per week. You are eating enough to
   recover.

 3. Push harder RPE 8-9 on working sets. Take sets closer to failure. The surplus supports
   recovery.

 4. Frequency remains 2-3x per muscle per week Maximises MPS opportunities.

 5. Include isolation work Compounds build the foundation, but isolation work can target specific
   muscles for additional growth.

Cardio During a Bulk
Cardio is not the enemy of muscle gain, but excessive cardio is.

Recommendations:

  • 2 Zone 2 sessions per week (20-30 min) for health and recovery

  • Do not add more unless conditioning is a specific goal

  • High-intensity cardio optional - 1 session per week max

  • If you are struggling to eat enough, reduce cardio

The goal is muscle gain. Cardio supports health but should not compromise the primary goal.

Rate of Weight Gain

Target: 0.25-0.5% of bodyweight per week

For an 80kg man: 0.2-0.4kg per week, or approximately 1-1.5kg per month.

Faster than this means excessive fat gain. Slower may indicate insufficient surplus.

Monitor and adjust:

  • Gaining too fast: Reduce calories by 100-200

  • Gaining too slow: Increase calories by 100-200

  • Weight stable: Increase calories by 200-300

Duration of a Gaining Phase
Recommended: 4-6 months

This allows meaningful muscle accumulation before fat gain necessitates cutting.

End the bulk when:

  • Body fat reaches 18-20%

  • You have gained your target weight

  • You have a deadline requiring fat loss

  • Progress has stalled for multiple weeks despite adequate calories

After the bulk:

Either maintain for 4-8 weeks to consolidate gains, or begin a cut to reveal the muscle you built.

Minimizing Fat Gain
Some fat gain during a bulk is inevitable. Minimize it with:

 1. Modest surplus - 10-15%, not 30%+

 2. High protein - Even at lower end of range, protein is still priority

 3. Quality calories - Whole foods over processed junk

 4. Consistent training - Never miss sessions during a bulk

 5. Adequate sleep - Poor sleep impairs nutrient partitioning

 6. Moderate cardio - 2 Zone 2 sessions maintain health and partitioning

 7. Monitor weekly - Adjust quickly if gaining too fast

   Body Composition Tracking

The "Lean Bulk" Mindset
Old-school bulking mentality - "eat big to get big" - leads to excessive fat gain that requires
extended cutting to fix.

The Apollo approach:

  • Modest surplus

  • Controlled weight gain

  • Regular assessment

  • Willingness to adjust

  • Acceptance that muscle gain is slow

> You are not "bulking." You are lean gaining. The distinction matters.

Tracking
What gets measured gets managed. Effective body composition change requires consistent
tracking beyond just stepping on a scale.

The Problem with Scale Weight

Scale weight fluctuates dramatically based on:

  • Water retention

  • Glycogen levels

  • Gut contents

  • Sodium intake

  • Stress and cortisol

  • Menstrual cycle (for women)

  • Time of day

  • Recent training

A man can fluctuate 2-4kg in a single day without any change in actual fat or muscle.

> Using daily scale weight to assess progress leads to frustration and poor decisions. You see the
scale up 1kg and think your cut is not working, when it is actually water from extra carbs yesterday.

The Multi-Metric Approach
Use multiple data points to assess progress:

1. Scale Weight (Weekly Average)

Weigh yourself daily under consistent conditions:

  • Morning

  • After using the bathroom

  • Before eating or drinking

  • Wearing the same thing (or nothing)

Calculate the weekly average. Compare weekly averages, not daily weights.

This smooths out fluctuations and reveals actual trends.

2. Waist Circumference

The single most reliable indicator of fat loss or gain.

Measure:

  • At the navel

  • Standing relaxed (not sucking in)

  • Same time of day

  • Weekly

A decreasing waist with stable or increasing weight indicates recomp (losing fat, gaining muscle).

3. Other Circumference Measurements

Additional measurements provide more data:

  • Chest (at nipple line)

  • Shoulders (widest point)

  • Arms (flexed, at peak)

  • Thighs (at widest point)

  • Hips (at widest point)

During a cut, waist should decrease while other measurements stay stable. During a bulk, other
measurements should increase while waist stays relatively stable.

4. Progress Photos

Photos reveal changes that numbers miss. Take photos:

  • Monthly (minimum)

  • Same time of day (morning is best)

  • Same lighting

  • Same poses (front relaxed, front flexed, side, back)

  • Same location

Review photos over time. Monthly comparison may show little; 3-month comparison often shows
dramatic change.

5. Strength Benchmarks

Your performance in the gym reflects muscle mass.

During a cut: Maintaining strength indicates muscle preservation During a bulk: Increasing strength
indicates muscle gain During recomp: Increasing strength with stable/decreasing waist indicates
success

Track your key lifts:

  • Squat (or leg press)

  • Deadlift (or RDL)

  • Bench press (or dumbbell press)

  • Row (or pull-up)

  • Overhead press

6. How Clothes Fit

Subjective but useful. Note how clothes fit over time:

  • Pants waist getting looser = fat loss

  • Shirt shoulders getting tighter = muscle gain

  • Both simultaneously = successful recomp

7. Visual Assessment

Look at yourself with objective eyes. Note:

  • Vascularity (more visible veins = leaner)

  • Muscle definition (more visible separation = leaner)

  • Muscle fullness (fuller looking = muscle gain or glycogen)

  • Face definition (leaner face = fat loss)

Interpreting Your Data
Scenario: Scale down, waist down, strength maintained Interpretation: Successful cut. Continue.

Scenario: Scale stable, waist down, strength up Interpretation: Successful recomp. Continue.

Scenario: Scale up, waist stable, strength up Interpretation: Successful lean bulk. Continue.

Scenario: Scale down, waist stable, strength down Interpretation: Losing muscle. Increase
calories and/or protein.

Scenario: Scale up, waist up, strength stable Interpretation: Gaining too much fat. Reduce
calories.

Scenario: Scale stable, all metrics stable for 3+ weeks Interpretation: Plateau. Adjust approach
based on goal.

The Assessment Cadence

Weekly:

  • Calculate scale average

  • Measure waist

  • Review training performance

  • Assess energy and recovery

  • Decide: continue as planned or adjust?

Monthly:

  • Take progress photos

  • Full body measurements

  • Review 4-week trends

  • Larger adjustments if needed

Quarterly:

  • Compare photos from 3 months ago

  • Assess overall progress toward goals

  • Decide: continue current approach or change phases?

Avoiding Data Paralysis
Tracking is a tool, not an obsession. If tracking creates anxiety or disordered thinking:

  • Reduce frequency (weekly weigh-ins only)

  • Focus on subjective markers (energy, performance, how you feel)

  • Take a break from tracking entirely

> The goal is information for better decisions, not a source of stress.

The Long View
Body composition change is slow. Real, meaningful change happens over months and years, not
days and weeks.

Zoom out. Compare yourself to 6 months ago, a year ago. That is where real progress becomes
visible.

Trust the process. Track consistently. Adjust based on data. Be patient.
