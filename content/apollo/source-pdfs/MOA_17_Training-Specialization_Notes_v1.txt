Intro - Training Specialization
For sixty days you have trained with a generalised programme designed to build a foundation of
strength, movement quality, and work capacity. That foundation is now solid.

It is time to specialise.

Not because generalised training stops working, it does not. But because at this stage, you know
enough about your body, your strengths, and your weaknesses to make intelligent decisions about
where to focus your effort for maximum return.

Some of you want to build the most aesthetically impressive physique possible. Some want raw
functional strength. Some are drawn to calisthenics and bodyweight mastery. Many want a
combination.

This module gives you the programming frameworks for each path, plus the tools to build a custom
programme that matches your specific goals, schedule, and training experience.




Identifying Your Training Priority - Training Specialization
Before you write a single programme, you need honest clarity on what you are training for. This
seems obvious. It is not. Most men have vague goals - "I want to look good and be strong" and end
up training in a no-man's-land that serves neither objective optimally.

The Four Archetypes:

The Aesthetic Build — Visual impact is the priority. Proportions, symmetry, muscle density, and low
body fat. Training emphasises hypertrophy across all major muscle groups with particular attention
to the muscles that create the most visual impact — shoulders, upper back, arms, and a developed
chest on a lean frame. The reference physique: Brad Pitt in Fight Club, Daniel Craig in Casino
Royale, Christian Bale in American Pyscho.

The Strength Build — Maximum force production. The ability to move heavy weight through full
range of motion. Training centres on the primary compound lifts with progressive overload as the
non-negotiable. Physique is a byproduct, not the goal, thick, dense, and functional.




                                       Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                3
The Calisthenics Build — Bodyweight mastery. The ability to control your body in space - muscle-
ups, handstands, levers, planches. This builds an exceptional strength-to-weight ratio and a
distinctive physique characterised by wide lats, developed shoulders, and a lean, athletic build.

The Hybrid — Elements of all three. This is what most Apollo men will gravitate toward. The goal is
to look exceptional, be genuinely strong, and have the bodyweight control that demonstrates real
athleticism. This requires more careful programming to avoid spreading effort too thin.

How to Choose:

Be honest. Which one matters most to you right now? Not which sounds best on paper. Which one
would make you most satisfied to achieve?

  • If you look in the mirror and the primary thing you want to change is how you look → Aesthetics
   first.

  • If you want to lift objectively heavy weight and the physique follows → Strength first.

  • If you want skills — muscle-ups, handstands, impossible-looking bodyweight movements →
   Calisthenics first.

  • If you genuinely want all three and are willing to progress slightly slower in each → Hybrid.

> Choose one as your primary focus for the next 12-16 weeks. You can always shift emphasis in the
next training block. But trying to optimise everything simultaneously optimises nothing.




Aesthetic-Focused Programming - Training Specialization
Training for visual impact and proportions.

Hypertrophy training for aesthetics is not just about getting bigger everywhere. It is about strategic
muscle development that creates an impressive visual silhouette.

The Muscles That Create Visual Impact:

 1. Shoulders (all three heads, particularly lateral delts) — Wide shoulders create the V-taper.
   This is the single most impactful muscle group for visual impressiveness. Lateral raises, face
   pulls, overhead pressing.

 2. Upper Back (traps, rhomboids, rear delts) — Thickness from behind and the 3D look from the
   side. Rows, face pulls, shrugs.

 3. Lats — Width. The V-taper from behind. Pull-ups, lat pulldowns, single-arm rows.

 4. Arms — Disproportionately visible because they are always exposed. Biceps and triceps both
   matter. Triceps are 2/3 of arm size.

 5. Chest (upper chest emphasis) — An underdeveloped upper chest makes the pecs look
   droopy. Incline pressing, low-to-high cable flyes.


                                          Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                 4
 6. Abs/Core — Visible abs require low body fat more than heavy training, but direct ab work
   improves definition and the "blocky" look.

Aesthetic Training Principles:

Volume: Hypertrophy responds to volume — the total amount of work performed.

Intensity: The 6-15 rep range is the sweet spot for hypertrophy, though reps as high as 20-30 can
drive growth when taken near failure. The key variable is proximity to failure, you should be within
1-2 reps of failure on most working sets.

Mind-Muscle Connection: For isolation and accessory work, the ability to feel the target muscle
working significantly impacts hypertrophy. This applies to isolation movements, not heavy
compounds.

Tempo: Control the eccentric (lowering) phase - 2-3 seconds minimum. This increases time under
tension and mechanical damage, both of which drive hypertrophy.

Strength-Focused Programming - Training Specialization
Training for maximum force production.

Strength training is fundamentally different from hypertrophy training. The adaptations you are
chasing are primarily neural - improved motor unit recruitment, rate coding, inter-muscular
coordination, and skill under heavy load.

The Core Principles:

Intensity (load) is king. To get strong, you must lift heavy. The 1-5 rep range with 80-95% of your
one-rep max is where the primary strength adaptations occur. Higher reps build muscle, but they do
not maximally recruit the high-threshold motor units that produce maximum force.

Volume is lower. You cannot sustain the same volume at high intensities that you can at moderate
loads. Strength training typically involves fewer total sets but higher relative intensity per set.

Rest periods are longer. Full phosphocreatine recovery between heavy sets requires 3-5 minutes.
Rushing rest in strength training is counterproductive.



                                         Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                               6
Skill matters. Lifting heavy is a skill. The more you practice heavy singles, doubles, and triples, the
more efficient your nervous system becomes at producing maximum force. Frequency of practice
trumps volume.

The Big Lifts:

Your strength programme should centre on these movements or close variations:

 1. Squat — back squat or front squat

 2. Hinge — conventional deadlift or sumo deadlift

 3. Horizontal Press — bench press or close-grip bench

 4. Vertical Press — standing overhead press

 5. Pull — barbell row or weighted pull-up

Progression Model:

Use a linear periodisation approach over 4-week blocks:

  • Week 1: Working sets at 80% of max — building volume

  • Week 2: Working sets at 83-85% — moderate intensity

  • Week 3: Working sets at 87-90% — peak intensity

  • Week 4: Deload — 60-70%, reduced volume

After each 4-week block, retest or estimate a new max based on performance. Adjust working
weights accordingly.

When to Test Maxes:

Test a true 1RM no more than every 8-12 weeks. Frequent maximal testing is draining and increases
injury risk. Use rep maxes to estimate, if you can hit 3 reps at a given weight with 1-2 reps in
reserve, your estimated 1RM is approximately 90-95% of that weight.




Calisthenics Foundations - Training Specialization
Bodyweight mastery and progression frameworks.

Calisthenics is the art of controlling your body in space. It builds a distinctive physique - wide lats
from extensive pulling, developed shoulders from handstand work, and a lean, athletic build that
comes from the strength-to-weight ratio required for advanced skills.

Why Calisthenics Deserves a Place in Your Training




                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                    8
Even if you do not want to pursue advanced skills, calisthenics elements improve your overall
training in several ways:

  • Body awareness. Heavy barbell work builds force production. Calisthenics builds
   proprioception, knowing where your body is in space.

  • Relative strength. Being strong relative to your bodyweight is arguably more functional than
   absolute strength. A man who can do 20 pull-ups and hold a handstand has a different kind of
   capability than a man who can bench 150kg but cannot do a muscle-up.

  • Joint health. Many calisthenics progressions require training through full ranges of motion under
   control, which builds tendon and ligament strength that heavy lifting alone does not.




The Calisthenics Progression Ladder - Training Specialization
Each skill has a progression — a series of increasingly difficult variations that take you from
beginner to advanced.

Pull-ups → Muscle-Ups:

 1. Dead hang (build grip, 30+ seconds)

 2. Scapular pull-ups (engage lats from dead hang)

 3. Negative pull-ups (jump up, lower slowly)

 4. Pull-ups (clean, chin over bar)

 5. Chest-to-bar pull-ups (pull higher)

 6. High pull-ups (explosive, pulling to sternum)

 7. Muscle-up negatives (start on top, lower slowly through transition)

 8. Muscle-up (kipping first, then strict)

Push-ups → Handstand Push-ups:

 1. Wall push-ups

 2. Incline push-ups

 3. Standard push-ups

 4. Decline push-ups

 5. Pike push-ups (hips high, shoulders working)

 6. Elevated pike push-ups (feet on box)

 7. Wall handstand holds (build time)

 8. Wall handstand push-ups (half range, then full)

 9. Freestanding handstand (balance work)

Programming Calisthenics - Training Specialization
For men using calisthenics as a supplement to weight training: add 2-3 calisthenics skill
progressions to the end of 2-3 sessions per week. For example, practice muscle-up progressions
after your pull day and handstand work after your push day. Or the other way around, start with
calisthenics to have the highest energy to maximise the exercise. Remember, it all depends where
your priorities are.

For men pursuing calisthenics as a primary focus: train 4-5 days per week with dedicated skill
practice (15-20 minutes), strength work using progressions (3-4 sets of the hardest variation you can
perform for 3-8 reps), and volume work at easier progressions for hypertrophy.



                                       Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                              10
> The Non-Negotiable: Patience

Calisthenics progression is slower than barbell progression. You cannot simply add 2.5kg to a pull-
up every week in the same way. Progression happens through increased reps, improved form,
harder variations, and longer holds. It takes months, not weeks. This is a practice.




Identifying Weak Points - Training Specialization
Targeted protocols for lagging areas.

After sixty days of consistent training, you know where you are strong and where you are not. This
lesson teaches you how to bring up weak points without neglecting the rest.

Identifying Weak Points:

Visual: Take progress photos — front, back, both sides, front and rear double bicep. Compare left to
right. Compare upper to lower. Where is the imbalance? Common patterns: underdeveloped rear
delts relative to front delts, weak upper chest, small calves, lacking hamstrings, underdeveloped
lats.

Performance: Where do you fail in your lifts? If the bench press fails at the bottom, pec strength is
the limiter. If it fails at lockout, triceps are the issue. If your squat fails in the hole, quad strength is
lacking. If you cannot maintain your back position in a deadlift, your posterior chain is the weak link.




The Specialisation Protocol - Training Specialization
Increase volume for the weak point by 50-100% while maintaining (not increasing) volume for
everything else.

Practical implementation:

  • Add 2-3 dedicated sets for the weak point to every training session, regardless of the session's
    primary focus. For example, if rear delts are weak, add 3 sets of face pulls or reverse flyes to
    every session. This accumulates significant weekly volume (12-15+ sets per week for that
    muscle group) without adding substantial fatigue.

  • Prioritise the weak point early in your session when you are freshest. If your upper chest is
    underdeveloped, begin your push session with incline work rather than flat bench.

  • Run specialisation for 6-8 weeks. Reassess. If the weak point has improved, return to balanced
    programming.

Common Weak Points and Fixes - Training Specialization
Lagging Shoulders: Add lateral raises to every session (3x15-20). Train them daily if needed, the
lateral delts recover quickly and respond to frequency. Use slow eccentrics and partial reps at the
end of sets for maximum stimulus.

Weak Upper Chest: Replace flat pressing with incline for 6-8 weeks. Low-to-high cable flyes
3x12-15, twice per week. Dumbbell incline pressing allows greater range of motion than barbell.

Underdeveloped Back: Add pull-ups or lat pulldowns and face pulls to every session. Prioritise
rowing variations with a pause at contraction to ensure you are actually using the lats rather than
relying on biceps.

Small Arms: Train them directly 3-4 times per week with 6-9 total sets per session. Biceps respond
to stretch (incline curls) and peak contraction (concentration curls). Triceps respond to overhead
extension and heavy pressing.

Weak Legs: Front squats build quads more effectively than back squats for most people. Add
Bulgarian split squats — arguably the best single exercise for leg development. Increase training
frequency to 3 lower body sessions per week during specialisation.




Building Your Custom Programme - Training Specialization
Putting it all together.

You now have the frameworks for aesthetic, strength, and calisthenics training, plus the tools to
address weak points. This lesson teaches you to design a programme that matches your specific
goals, schedule, and recovery capacity.

Define Your Primary Goal

Choose one: aesthetics, strength, calisthenics, or hybrid. Write it down. This determines your
training split, rep ranges, and exercise selection.

Determine Available Training Days

Be realistic. Not aspirational. How many days per week can you consistently train for the next 12-16
weeks?

  • 3 days: Full body or upper/lower/full body. Best for strength or hybrid goals.

  • 4 days: Upper/lower split. Works for all goals. The sweet spot for most men.

  • 5 days: Push/pull/legs or upper/lower/push/pull/legs. Best for aesthetics or high-volume
   calisthenics.




                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                12
  • 6 days: Push/pull/legs repeated. Only appropriate for advanced trainees with excellent recovery.

Select Your Exercises

Every session needs:

  • 1-2 compound movements (primary drivers of progress)

  • 2-3 accessory movements (support the compounds, target weak points)

  • Optional: 1 isolation movement for weak point specialisation

Choose exercises you can progressively overload. If you have never done a movement before,
spend 2-3 sessions learning the pattern with light weight before loading it.

Programme Your Progression

Every programme needs a progression model — how you will systematically increase the challenge
over time.

  • Double progression (aesthetics): Increase reps within a range (e.g., 8-12). When you hit the top
   of the range for all sets, increase weight and drop to the bottom of the range.

  • Linear periodisation (strength): Increase intensity across 3-4 week blocks, then deload and
   restart with a higher baseline.

  • Variation progression (calisthenics): Master a variation, then advance to the next in the
   progression ladder.

Build In Deloads

Every 4th or 5th week. Reduce volume by 40-50% and intensity by 10-15%. This is not optional.
Continuous accumulation without deloads leads to staleness, overuse injuries, and burnout.

Step 7: Write It Down

Your programme should be documented in full before you begin. Every session, every exercise,
sets, reps, rest periods. Walk into the gym with a plan. Execute the plan. Log the results.

> If you do not log your training, you are not training, you are exercising. There is a difference.
