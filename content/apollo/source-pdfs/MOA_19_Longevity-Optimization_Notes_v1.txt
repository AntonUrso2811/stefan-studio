Intro - Longevity & Optimization
Most men optimise for the next 30 days. You are going to optimise for the next 30 years.

Longevity is not about living to 120. It is about extending your healthspan - the years you are fully
functional, capable, strong, sharp, and independent. The gap between lifespan and healthspan in
most men is devastating. The average man spends the last 10-15 years of his life in declining health
- chronic disease, cognitive decline, loss of independence, and a body that no longer serves him.

That is not the Apollo trajectory.

The protocols in this module are not speculative. They are drawn from the most robust research in
longevity science. The interventions are practical, measurable, and within your control.

> You have already been building the foundation for longevity throughout this programme without
realising it. Resistance training, adequate protein, sleep optimisation, stress management, gut health
- these are all longevity interventions. This module adds the specific markers and strategies that
take your long-term trajectory from average to exceptional.




The Longevity Mindset - Longevity & Optimization
> The biggest shift this module requires is temporal. Most men think about their health in terms of
the next competition, the next vacation, the next photo. Longevity thinking means asking a different
question: what will the cumulative effect of today's decisions be in twenty years?

The Four Horsemen of Chronic Disease:

Four primary killers that account for roughly 80% of deaths in developed nations:

 1. Cardiovascular Disease — Heart attacks and strokes. The leading cause of death globally.

 2. Cancer — Second leading cause. Multiple types, many partially preventable.

 3. Neurodegenerative Disease — Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, dementia. The cruelest, robbing your
   mind before your body.

 4. Metabolic Disease — Type 2 diabetes and its cascading complications. The most preventable
   of the four.

The strategies in this module target all four, not with medications, but with the same lifestyle
interventions you have been building. Longevity is not a separate pursuit from what you have been
doing. It is the long-term consequence of doing it well.

Biomarkers That Matter - Longevity & Optimization
Annual blood work is the minimum standard. But most standard check-ups test for obvious disease
rather than optimal function. The Apollo approach tests for optimisation - catching trends before
they become problems.

Tier 1 — Annual Minimum (Everyone Should Test These):

Lipid Panel (Advanced):

Standard lipid panels measure total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides. This is a start but
insufficient.

Request an advanced lipid panel that includes:

  • ApoB (Apolipoprotein B) — The single best predictor of cardiovascular risk. Each atherogenic
    particle (the ones that form plaques) carries one ApoB molecule. Optimal: under 80 mg/dL. Ideal:
    under 60 mg/dL. This is more predictive than standard LDL cholesterol.

  • Lp(a) (Lipoprotein a) — Genetically determined and a significant independent cardiovascular
    risk factor. Test once — if elevated (>50 nmol/L or >30 mg/dL), it requires more aggressive
    management of other risk factors. It does not respond to lifestyle interventions.

  • Triglycerides — Marker of metabolic health. Optimal: under 100 mg/dL. Fasting triglycerides
    above 150 indicate metabolic dysfunction.

  • HDL — Higher is generally better. Above 40 mg/dL minimum, above 60 mg/dL is optimal.

Metabolic Markers:

  • Fasting Glucose — Optimal: 72-85 mg/dL. Above 100 is pre-diabetic. This is basic but
    important.

  • Fasting Insulin — Often not tested in standard panels. This is a mistake. Insulin rises years
    before glucose does - elevated fasting insulin (above 8-10 uIU/mL) is an early warning of
    metabolic dysfunction that standard glucose testing misses. Optimal: under 6 uIU/mL.

 • HbA1c (Glycated Haemoglobin) — Average blood sugar over 3 months. Optimal: under 5.4%.
   Pre-diabetic: 5.7-6.4%. This connects directly to the glycation/skin aging discussion from
   Module 1.

 • HOMA-IR — Calculated from fasting glucose and insulin. Measures insulin resistance. Optimal:
   under 1.0.

Inflammatory Markers:

 • hs-CRP (High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein) — Systemic inflammation marker. Optimal:
   under 1.0 mg/L. Above 3.0 indicates significant inflammation and increased cardiovascular risk.
   Chronically elevated hs-CRP in the absence of acute illness warrants investigation - gut health
   (Module 2 of Phase 3), sleep quality, and body fat percentage are common drivers.

Hormonal:

 • Total Testosterone — Normal range is broad (300-1000 ng/dL). Optimal is individual, but most
   men feel and function best above 500 ng/dL. Test in the morning (7-10am) when levels peak.
   Fasted.

 • Free Testosterone — The bioavailable fraction. More clinically relevant than total T for
   symptoms. Normal ranges vary by lab.

 • SHBG (Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin) — Binds testosterone and makes it unavailable. High
   SHBG with normal total T can produce low free T and low-T symptoms.

 • Thyroid (TSH, Free T3, Free T4) — Thyroid function affects metabolism, energy, and body
   composition. TSH alone is insufficient - free T3 and T4 provide the full picture.

Other:

 • Vitamin D (25-OH) — Optimal: 40-60 ng/mL. Most men are deficient. Supplement accordingly.

 • Ferritin — Iron storage. Too low impairs energy and recovery. Too high (above 200-300 ng/mL in
   men) indicates iron overload, which increases oxidative stress and cardiovascular risk.

 • Complete Blood Count (CBC) — Screens for anaemia, infection, and blood disorders.

 • Liver function (ALT, AST) — Elevated levels indicate liver stress. Common causes: alcohol,
   medications, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease.

 • Kidney function (Creatinine, eGFR) — Ensures kidney function is adequate, especially
   important if consuming high-protein diets long-term.

Tier 2 — Worth Adding If Optimising:

 • Homocysteine — Elevated levels (above 10 umol/L) associated with cardiovascular risk and
   impaired methylation. B vitamins (B12, folate, B6) typically reduce it.

 • Omega-3 Index — Measures the percentage of omega-3 fatty acids in red blood cell
   membranes. Optimal: above 8%. Below 4% is associated with significantly increased
   cardiovascular risk.

Testosterone Optimisation - Natural Protocols - Longevity &
Optimization
Testosterone is the master male hormone. It drives muscle protein synthesis, fat metabolism, bone
density, mood, motivation, cognitive function, and sexual health. It has been declining across
populations. Modern men have measurably lower testosterone than their fathers did at the same
age.

The causes are multifactorial: endocrine-disrupting chemicals, sedentary lifestyles, obesity, poor
sleep, chronic stress, and dietary changes.

The Lifestyle Factors (In Order of Impact):

1. Sleep — This is number one for a reason.

You already know the sleep protocols. This is additional motivation to execute them.

2. Body Fat — Adipose tissue contains aromatase, the enzyme that converts testosterone to
oestrogen. Higher body fat means more aromatase activity, which means lower testosterone and
higher oestrogen. Research consistently shows that losing excess body fat is one of the most
effective natural interventions for increasing testosterone. Getting body fat to 12-18% (the range
where health and hormonal function are optimal for most men) should be a priority.

3. Resistance Training — Compound movements under heavy load acutely increase testosterone
and growth hormone. Chronic resistance training maintains the stimulus for testosterone production.
Overtraining, however, does the opposite - excessive volume without adequate recovery suppresses
testosterone via chronically elevated cortisol.

4. Stress Management — Cortisol and testosterone have an inverse relationship. Chronic elevation
of cortisol directly suppresses testosterone production through the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal
(HPG) axis. The stress management protocols from Phase 2 are directly relevant here.




                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                 6
5. Nutrition — Adequate caloric intake (chronic severe dieting suppresses testosterone), adequate
dietary fat (cholesterol is the precursor to all steroid hormones — diets below 20% fat are
associated with lower testosterone), adequate zinc and vitamin D (both directly involved in
testosterone synthesis), and avoidance of excessive alcohol (alcohol suppresses testosterone
acutely and promotes aromatase activity).

6. Environmental Toxin Reduction — Endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) are ubiquitous in
modern life. The most impactful reductions:

  • Avoid plastic food containers, especially when heated. Use glass or stainless steel.

  • Filter drinking water (activated carbon or reverse osmosis removes most EDCs).

  • Choose natural personal care products - conventional deodorants, shampoos, and lotions often
   contain parabens, phthalates, and other endocrine disruptors.

  • Avoid BPA-lined canned foods. Choose BPA-free options or fresh food.

  • Minimise receipt paper handling (thermal paper contains BPA).

Supplements That May Support Testosterone (Evidence-Based):

  • Vitamin D (2000-5000 IU daily) — Deficiency directly correlates with low testosterone.
   Supplementation to optimal levels (40-60 ng/mL) has been shown to increase testosterone in
   deficient men.

  • Zinc (15-30mg daily) — Essential for testosterone synthesis. Already in your stack.

  • Magnesium (300-400mg daily) — Supports testosterone, sleep, and recovery. Already in your
   stack.

  • Ashwagandha (300-600mg KSM-66 extract daily) — An adaptogen with evidence for reducing
   cortisol and modestly increasing testosterone. A 2019 study in American Journal of Men's Health
   found that ashwagandha supplementation increased testosterone by approximately 15% in
   overweight men over 16 weeks.

  • Tongkat Ali (Eurycoma longifolia) (200-400mg daily) — Evidence for mild testosterone support,
   particularly in stressed and aging men. A 2012 study in Journal of the International Society of
   Sports Nutrition found improvements in stress hormones and testosterone status.

  • Boron (6-10mg daily) — A trace mineral with evidence for reducing SHBG and increasing free
   testosterone. A small 2011 study found significant increases in free testosterone after one week
   of 10mg daily supplementation.

> Important perspective: These supplements produce modest effects. They are not replacements
for the lifestyle factors above. A man who sleeps 5 hours, carries 30% body fat, and is chronically
stressed will not supplement his way to optimal testosterone. Fix the foundation first. Supplements
are the final 5-10%.

Metabolic Health and Insulin Sensitivity - Longevity &
Optimization
Insulin resistance is arguably the most dangerous health condition most men have never been
diagnosed with. It underlies type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, non-alcoholic fatty liver
disease, and is associated with increased cancer risk. It is estimated that up to 40% of adults in
Western countries have some degree of insulin resistance, most undiagnosed.

What Insulin Resistance Actually Is:

When you eat carbohydrates (and to a lesser extent, protein), blood glucose rises. The pancreas
releases insulin to shuttle glucose into cells for energy or storage. In a healthy, insulin-sensitive
system, a small amount of insulin efficiently clears glucose from the blood.

In insulin resistance, cells become less responsive to insulin's signal. The pancreas compensates by
producing more insulin. Blood glucose may stay normal for years while insulin levels climb silently.
Eventually, the pancreas cannot keep up and blood glucose rises, at this point, you have pre-
diabetes or type 2 diabetes.

But the damage begins years earlier when insulin is chronically elevated. High insulin promotes fat
storage (particularly visceral fat around organs), drives inflammation, and accelerates arterial
damage.

How to Know If You Are Insulin Resistant:

  • Fasting insulin above 8-10 uIU/mL (test this — most doctors do not include it in standard panels)

  • Fasting glucose above 95 mg/dL

  • Triglycerides above 150 mg/dL

  • Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio above 2.0 (a simple, powerful marker)

  • Waist circumference above 94cm / 37 inches (this correlates strongly with visceral fat and insulin
   resistance)

  • HbA1c above 5.5%

  • HOMA-IR above 1.5

If multiple markers are elevated, you have metabolic dysfunction that needs to be addressed.

The Protocol for Improving Insulin Sensitivity:

1. Resistance Training — Muscle is the primary glucose sink in the body. More muscle mass
means more capacity to clear glucose without requiring high insulin levels. A single resistance
training session can improve insulin sensitivity for 24-72 hours. Consistent training creates lasting
improvements.




                                         Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                 8
2. Zone 2 Cardio — Moderate-intensity cardio (2-3 sessions per week, 30-45 minutes) improves
mitochondrial function in muscle cells, enhancing their ability to oxidise glucose and fatty acids. This
directly improves insulin sensitivity.

3. Body Fat Reduction — Visceral fat is metabolically active and produces inflammatory cytokines
that worsen insulin resistance. Reducing body fat — particularly abdominal fat — is one of the most
effective interventions.

4. Post-Meal Walking — A 10-15 minute walk after meals significantly blunts the blood glucose
spike.

5. Meal Composition — Eating protein and vegetables before carbohydrates within a meal reduces
the glycaemic impact.

6. Time-Restricted Eating — Allowing adequate fasting periods between meals (covered in Phase
2) improves insulin sensitivity by reducing the total time insulin is elevated.

7. Sleep — Poor sleep directly impairs insulin sensitivity. A single night of sleep deprivation can
reduce insulin sensitivity by 25-30%. You already know this.

Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGMs):

CGMs (like Levels, Dexcom, or Freestyle Libre) measure blood glucose in real-time throughout the
day. Wearing one for 2-4 weeks provides extraordinary insight into how your body responds to
specific foods, exercise, stress, and sleep. You will learn exactly which foods spike your glucose
and which do not, how much post-meal exercise helps, and how sleep quality affects your
metabolic function.

This is not a permanent tool. Two weeks of data will teach you more about your metabolic health
than years of guessing.




Inflammation, Cardiovascular Health, and Aging - Longevity &
Optimization
Chronic low-grade inflammation, sometimes called "inflammaging" is the common thread running
through all four horsemen of chronic disease. It accelerates arterial plaque formation, promotes
cancer cell growth, damages neurons, and worsens insulin resistance.

Measuring Inflammation:

hs-CRP is the primary accessible marker. Below 1.0 mg/L is optimal. Above 3.0 is concerning.
Persistent elevation in the absence of acute illness or injury warrants investigation.

The Anti-Inflammatory Lifestyle (You Are Already Doing Most of This):

  • Resistance training with adequate recovery (you are doing this)

  • Adequate sleep (you are doing this)

  • Stress management (you are doing this)

  • Omega-3 intake of 2-3g EPA/DHA daily (you are doing this)

  • Gut health optimisation (Phase 3 Module 2)

  • Body fat in a healthy range (ongoing)

  • Minimised processed food, sugar, and alcohol intake (Phase 1 nutrition)

Additional Interventions:

  • Curcumin (500-1000mg with piperine for absorption) — Potent anti-inflammatory with evidence
   for reducing hs-CRP and inflammatory markers. Use a formulation with enhanced bioavailability
   (Meriva, Longvida, or curcumin with piperine/BioPerine).

  • Sulforaphane — Found in broccoli sprouts and cruciferous vegetables. Activates the Nrf2
   pathway, the body's master antioxidant defence system. Broccoli sprouts contain 20-50x more
   sulforaphane than mature broccoli. Grow them yourself or supplement.

Cardiovascular Health Beyond Standard Advice:

ApoB Management — If your ApoB is above 80 mg/dL, lifestyle interventions should be
aggressive: reduce saturated fat intake (replace some with monounsaturated fats like olive oil),
increase soluble fibre (10-25g daily — oats, psyllium, legumes), maintain a healthy weight, and
exercise regularly. If lifestyle does not bring ApoB below 80, discuss statin therapy with your
physician. The evidence for ApoB reduction and cardiovascular risk reduction is robust.

VO2 Max — Your maximal aerobic capacity is the single strongest predictor of all-cause mortality.

Improving VO2 max requires dedicated cardiovascular training — Zone 2 for base building (3-4
sessions per week) and occasional higher-intensity intervals (1-2 sessions per week). This is not
optional for longevity. Men who only lift weights and never do cardio are leaving years of life on the
table.

Resting Heart Rate — A lower resting heart rate generally indicates better cardiovascular fitness.
Optimal: 50-65 bpm. Above 80 bpm at rest is associated with increased cardiovascular risk.

The Longevity Protocol - Longevity & Optimization
The Five Pillars of the Apollo Longevity Protocol:

1. Maintain Muscle Mass — You will naturally lose 3-8% of muscle mass per decade after 30 if you
do not actively fight it (sarcopenia). This loss accelerates after 60. Resistance training 3-4x per week
with adequate protein is the primary defence. This is not negotiable at any age.

2. Maintain Cardiovascular Fitness — Target VO2 max in the top 25th percentile for your age,
minimum. Zone 2 training 3-4x per week, high-intensity intervals 1-2x per week. Get a VO2 max test
if possible to establish your baseline and track progress.

3. Maintain Metabolic Health — Keep body fat in a healthy range (12-20% for most men), maintain
insulin sensitivity through diet and exercise, monitor biomarkers annually, and address any
deterioration immediately.

4. Manage Inflammation and Stress — Keep hs-CRP below 1.0, manage psychological stress,
prioritise sleep, support gut health, and maintain anti-inflammatory nutrition.

5. Maintain Cognitive Function — Exercise (both resistance and cardiovascular) is the single most
evidence-backed intervention for cognitive longevity. Add: social engagement, continued learning,
adequate sleep, and omega-3 intake. Emerging evidence supports the role of the gut-brain axis
(Module 2 of Phase 3) in cognitive health.

Annual Protocol:

  • Comprehensive blood work (Tier 1 markers from Lesson 2) — annually

  • Body composition assessment — quarterly

  • VO2 max or proxy assessment — annually

  • Resting heart rate tracking — ongoing (most smart watches track this)

  • Blood pressure monitoring — quarterly minimum

The Daily Stack for Longevity:

You already take most of these. The additions here are specific to the longevity focus:

  • Omega-3: 2-3g EPA/DHA

  • Vitamin D: 2000-5000 IU (titrate to blood level of 40-60 ng/mL)

  • Magnesium: 300-400mg glycinate or threonate

  • Creatine: 5g daily (cognitive benefits in addition to physical — emerging research supports
   neuroprotective effects)

  • Curcumin: 500mg with piperine (anti-inflammatory)

  • Optional: Astaxanthin 4-12mg (antioxidant, skin, cardiovascular)

maintenance keeps you off the medications that most men your age will be taking.

This is not about perfection. It is about consistency across decades. The man who trains 3-4 times
per week for thirty years will be unrecognisable compared to the man who trained hard for two years
and then stopped.
