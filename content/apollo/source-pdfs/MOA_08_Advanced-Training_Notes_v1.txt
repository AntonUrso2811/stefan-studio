   Progressive Overload Mastery



Introduction - ADVANCED TRAINING
You have trained consistently for 30 days. You have built the habit. You understand the movement
patterns. You have felt what it means to show up regardless of how you feel.

Now we sharpen the blade.

Phase 1 training was about installation - getting you moving correctly and consistently. Phase 2
training is about optimization - making every session count for more, understanding the
mechanisms that drive adaptation, and building a system that will carry you for years.

The men who look exceptional after five years of training are not the ones who trained hardest in
month one. They are the ones who trained intelligently, progressively, and sustainably. They
understood that the body responds to strategic stress, not random punishment.

This module gives you that understanding.




Progressive Overload - ADVANCED TRAINING
The single most important principle in training is progressive overload. Everything else is secondary.
You can have perfect exercise selection, optimal rest periods, and flawless technique - but without
progressive overload, you will not grow.

Your body is an adaptation machine. It responds to stress by rebuilding stronger, but only to the
degree necessary to handle the current demand. Lift the same weight for the same reps week after
week, and your body has no reason to change. It has already adapted to that stress. You are
maintaining, not building.

Progressive overload forces continued adaptation by systematically increasing demand over time.

The Science of Why This Works - ADVANCED TRAINING
When you lift a weight, you create mechanical tension in muscle fibres. This tension, combined with
metabolic stress and muscle damage, triggers a signalling cascade that tells your body to
synthesise new muscle protein. The muscle rebuilds slightly stronger and larger than before.

But here is the critical point: this response is proportional to the novelty of the stress. The first time
you squat 100kg, your body responds dramatically. The tenth time, less so. The hundredth time,
barely at all. Your body has fully adapted to that stimulus.

Research consistently shows that progressive overload is the primary driver of hypertrophy and
strength gains. A 2017 meta-analysis in the Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research
confirmed that programs with built-in progression significantly outperform those without, regardless
of other variables.

This applies beyond muscle. Progressive overload stimulates bone density increase, tendon and
ligament strengthening, and neural adaptations. You are not just building muscle, you are building a
more resilient system.




The Methods of Progression - ADVANCED TRAINING
Most men think progressive overload means "add more weight." That is one method, but it is not
the only one. Understanding all the variables you can manipulate gives you options when one path
stalls.

Load Progression

Adding weight to the bar. The most straightforward method and the primary driver of strength
adaptation.

When you can complete all prescribed reps with good form and appropriate effort, add weight. For
upper body movements, increase by 2.5kg. For lower body movements, increase by 2.5 - 5kg.
Small jumps compound into massive changes over months and years.

The key phrase is "good form and appropriate effort." If you are grinding reps with breakdown in
technique, you are not ready to add weight. If the set felt easy with reps in reserve, you are ready.

Rep Progression

Adding reps within a prescribed range. This is the foundation of the double progression model that
works exceptionally well for most lifters.




                                         Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                      5
Your program prescribes a rep range - say, 8-12 reps. You start at the bottom of the range with a
given weight. Each session, you attempt to add reps while maintaining form. When you hit the top of
the range for all sets, you add weight and reset to the bottom.

Example:

  • Week 1: 80kg x 8, 8, 8

  • Week 2: 80kg x 9, 8, 8

  • Week 3: 80kg x 10, 9, 9

  • Week 4: 80kg x 11, 10, 10

  • Week 5: 80kg x 12, 12, 11

  • Week 6: 82.5kg x 8, 8, 8 (weight increase, reps reset)

This allows for weekly progress even when load increases are not possible, and it builds work
capacity alongside strength.

Set Progression

Adding volume by increasing the number of sets. When you are recovering well and progress has
stalled despite adequate load and rep progression, adding one set to key movements can restart
adaptation.

Use this sparingly. Volume is a tool, not the goal. More is not always better - more is better until it
exceeds your recovery capacity, then it becomes counterproductive.

A practical application: if you have been stuck on a movement for 3+ weeks, add one set for 4
weeks, then reassess. If progress resumes, you needed the volume. If not, volume was not the
limiting factor.

Density Progression

Performing the same work in less time by reducing rest periods. This improves conditioning and
work capacity while maintaining the strength stimulus.

If you currently rest 3 minutes between sets of squats, gradually reduce to 2.5 minutes, then 2
minutes over several weeks. You are performing the same total work with less recovery - a form of
progressive demand.

This is particularly useful during fat loss phases when load progression slows due to caloric deficit.

Range of Motion Progression

Increasing the range through which you move the weight. A deeper squat creates more tension
through longer muscle lengths. A fuller stretch at the bottom of a Romanian deadlift increases
hamstring stimulus.




                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                    6
As your mobility improves through the protocols in Phase 1, your effective range of motion in
exercises should increase. This is progression that many men overlook. The man squatting to full
depth at 100kg is training harder than the man quarter-squatting 140kg.

The Double Progression Model

For Phase 2, you will use a double progression model as your primary system. This is how it works:

Every exercise has a prescribed rep range (e.g., 6-8, 8-10, 10-12). The range is determined by the
exercise type:

  • Primary compounds: 5-8 reps

  • Secondary compounds: 8-10 reps

  • Isolation work: 10-15 reps

Start at the bottom of the range with a weight that is challenging but allows good form. Each
session, aim to add reps. When you achieve the top of the range for all working sets, increase the
weight by the minimum increment and reset to the bottom of the range.

This system works because it provides structure while allowing for natural variation in performance.
Some days you will hit more reps. Some days fewer. The system accommodates this while still
driving progress over time.




Tracking: The Non-Negotiable - ADVANCED TRAINING
You cannot progressively overload what you do not measure. A training log is not optional - it is
essential equipment, as important as your shoes.

Record for every working set:

  • Exercise

  • Weight

  • Reps achieved

  • RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion, 1-10 scale)

  • Any notes on form or feel

Review your log before every session. Know what you did last time. Have a specific target for this
session. This intentionality separates training from exercising.

Apps work. A notebook works. A spreadsheet works. The format does not matter. Consistent
recording does.

When Progression Stalls



                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                 7
You will not progress every session. That is normal. But patterns of stagnation require response.

If you have not progressed on a movement for 3+ weeks despite consistent effort, ask these
questions in order:

 1. Sleep: Are you getting 7-9 hours consistently? Sleep debt kills progress before anything else.

 2. Nutrition: Are you eating enough protein? Are you in too aggressive a deficit? Underfuelling
   stalls progress.

 3. Recovery: What is your overall stress load? Work stress, relationship stress, and training stress
   all draw from the same recovery pool.

 4. Technique: Has your form degraded? Sometimes "stalling" is actually your body protecting you
   from injury as technique breaks down.

 5. Programming: Is it time for a de-load? Is volume too high or too low? Has the exercise run its
   course?

Address these in order. Most stalls are lifestyle issues, not programming issues. Fix the foundation
before changing the plan.




Action - ADVANCED TRAINING
  • Set up a training log if you do not have one - digital or physical, choose what you will actually
   use

  • Review your Phase 1 training and identify your current working weights for all main movements

  • Calculate your rep range targets using the double progression model

  • Set specific, written progression goals for the next 4 weeks

Periodization - ADVANCED TRAINING
Training the same way indefinitely stops working. Your body adapts, progress stalls, fatigue
accumulates, and motivation erodes. Periodization is the solution - strategically varying training
variables over time to continue driving adaptation while managing fatigue.

This is not complicated theory. It is a practical necessity. Every successful strength athlete uses
some form of periodization. The research supports it for everyone, not just competitors.

Why You Cannot Train Maximally Forever

Training creates fatigue. Some fatigue is acute - you feel tired after a hard session, but recover
within 24-48 hours. Some fatigue is cumulative - it builds over weeks of hard training and requires
more than a day or two to dissipate.

Cumulative fatigue is sneaky. You do not always feel it acutely, but it manifests as:

  • Stalled progress despite consistent effort

  • Decreased motivation and gym enthusiasm

  • Minor aches and joint discomfort

  • Disrupted sleep despite being tired

  • Decreased performance on movements that were previously progressing

If you train at maximum intensity indefinitely, cumulative fatigue eventually exceeds your recovery
capacity. You either burn out, get injured, or plateau. Usually all three.

Periodization prevents this by building in strategic phases of different intensities, volumes, and
focuses. You push hard, then you recover. You accumulate fatigue, then you dissipate it. This rhythm
allows for continued progress over months and years rather than a few hard weeks followed by
breakdown.




                                          Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                               9
Types of Periodization - ADVANCED TRAINING
Linear Periodization

The classic approach. Start with higher volume and lower intensity, then progressively shift to lower
volume and higher intensity over a training cycle.

Example over 12 weeks:

  • Weeks 1-4: 4 sets of 10 reps at 65-70% of max

  • Weeks 5-8: 4 sets of 8 reps at 75-80% of max

  • Weeks 9-12: 4 sets of 5 reps at 85-90% of max

This works well for building toward a peak - a competition, a test, or a personal record attempt. The
early phases build muscle and work capacity. The later phases express that fitness as strength.

Limitation: you only train each quality once per cycle. By the time you are lifting heavy in weeks
9-12, some of the muscle built in weeks 1-4 may have already begun to detrain.

Undulating Periodization

Vary intensity and volume within the week rather than across months. Different sessions target
different adaptations.

Example weekly structure:

  • Monday: Heavy (4x5 at 85%)

  • Wednesday: Moderate (3x10 at 70%)

  • Friday: Light (3x15 at 60%)

Research suggests undulating periodization may be superior for hypertrophy because it provides
varied stimulus more frequently. You train strength, hypertrophy, and muscular endurance qualities
every week rather than in separate phases.

This is the model we will use in Phase 2.

Block Periodization

Dedicate concentrated blocks of time to specific adaptations.

Example:

  • Block 1 (4 weeks): Hypertrophy focus - high volume, moderate intensity

  • Block 2 (4 weeks): Strength focus - moderate volume, high intensity

  • Block 3 (2 weeks): Peaking - low volume, very high intensity




                                       Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                10
This is used by advanced athletes with specific competition goals. The concentration of focus in
each block drives rapid adaptation in that quality, but other qualities may temporarily decline.

For most men in this community, this level of specialisation is unnecessary. Undulating periodization
provides balanced development without the complexity.




The Phase 2 Periodization Model - ADVANCED TRAINING
Phase 2 uses weekly undulating periodization with planned de-loads. Here is the structure:

Within Each Week:

Day 1 - Heavy: Primary compound movements in the 5-6 rep range, longer rest periods (2-3
minutes), focus on strength expression. RPE 8-9.

Day 2 - Moderate: Primary movements plus accessories in the 8-10 rep range, moderate rest
(90-120 seconds), focus on hypertrophy. RPE 7-8.

Day 3 - Light/Volume: Higher rep ranges (10-15), shorter rest (60-90 seconds), includes conditioning
elements. RPE 6-7.

Day 4 (if training 4 days): Moderate with different movement focus, addresses weak points.

This structure ensures you train each quality weekly while allowing for recovery between demanding
sessions.

Across the 8-Week Phase:

Phase 2 is organised into two 4-week mesocycles:

Mesocycle 1 - Accumulation (Weeks 1-4):

  • Higher volume (more total sets per muscle group)

  • Moderate intensity (working weights challenging but not maximal)

  • Focus on technique refinement, muscle growth, and work capacity

  • RPE targets: 7-8 on most working sets

  • Week 4: Deload

The goal is to accumulate training stress and build muscle. You are depositing into the adaptation
bank.

Mesocycle 2 - Intensification (Weeks 5-8):

  • Moderate volume (slight reduction from accumulation phase)

  • Higher intensity (heavier working weights)



                                       Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                11
  • Focus on strength expression and neural adaptations

  • RPE targets: 8-9 on primary movements

  • Week 8: Deload

The goal is to express the fitness you built in the accumulation phase as increased strength. You are
withdrawing from the adaptation bank.




The De-load Week - ADVANCED TRAINING
Every fourth week is a de-load. This is not optional. It is programmed recovery that allows
cumulative fatigue to dissipate.

During de-load weeks:

  • Reduce volume by 40-50% (cut sets, not exercises)

  • Maintain intensity (keep weights similar, do fewer sets)

  • Reduce RPE (stop sets further from failure)

  • Maintain frequency (still train the same days)

A common mistake is treating de-load as complete rest. This is suboptimal. Light training maintains
neural patterns and promotes active recovery better than total inactivity. You are recovering, not
stopping.

Another mistake is skipping de-loads because you "feel fine." Cumulative fatigue is not always
obvious until it manifests as injury or illness. Trust the program. Take the de-load. You will come
back stronger in week 5 than if you had pushed through.

Signs You Need an Unplanned De-load

Sometimes life intervenes - high work stress, poor sleep, illness. If you experience these signs
before a scheduled de-load, take one early:

  • Two consecutive sessions where you significantly underperform previous bests

  • Joint pain that persists between sessions

  • Elevated resting heart rate (5+ beats above baseline) for multiple days

  • Sleep disruption despite good sleep hygiene

  • Getting sick or feeling run down

It is better to de-load early and preserve progress than to push through and dig a recovery hole that
takes weeks to escape.

 • Understand your week structure: identify which day is heavy, moderate, and light

 • Mark your deload weeks on the calendar now - Week 4 and Week 8

 • Set RPE targets for each session type and track them

 • Commit to taking de-loads as programmed, regardless of how you feel

Intro - ADVANCED TRAINING
Phase 1 established your movement patterns. You know the squat, hinge, push, pull, and carry. Now
we refine exercise selection to address your specific needs and introduce strategic variation to
prevent staleness and continue driving adaptation.

The goal is not to do as many exercises as possible. The goal is to select the right exercises for your
goals and rotate them intelligently.

The Exercise Hierarchy

Not all exercises are equal. Some should form the foundation of every program. Others play
supporting roles. Understanding this hierarchy prevents you from majoring in minors.




Tier 1 - Primary Compounds - ADVANCED TRAINING
These are your foundation. Multi-joint movements that load heavy and drive the most adaptation.
They should receive priority in your programming - trained first when fresh, tracked most carefully,
progressed most consistently.

Squat Variations: Back squat, front squat, safety bar squat

Hinge Variations: Conventional deadlift, sumo deadlift, trap bar deadlift

Horizontal Press: Barbell bench press, dumbbell bench press

Horizontal Pull: Barbell row, dumbbell row, chest-supported row

Vertical Press: Overhead press, push press, seated press Vertical

Pull: Pull-ups, chin-ups, lat pulldown

Every program should include at least one exercise from each category. These are non-negotiable.




                                         Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                14
Tier 2 - Secondary Compounds - ADVANCED TRAINING
Movements that support the primary lifts. They build muscle in patterns that transfer to Tier 1
movements and address areas that primary lifts miss.

Lower Body: Romanian deadlifts, lunges, Bulgarian split squats, leg press, hip thrusts, step-ups

Upper Push: Incline press, dips, close-grip bench, landmine press

Upper Pull: Cable rows, face pulls, Meadows rows, single-arm work

These add volume to muscle groups without the systemic fatigue of primary compounds. They also
address unilateral balance and target specific weak points.




Tier 3 - Isolation Work - ADVANCED TRAINING
Single-joint movements for targeted development. They add volume to specific muscles without
significant systemic fatigue.

Arms: Bicep curls, tricep extensions, hammer curls

Shoulders: Lateral raises, front raises, rear delt flies

Legs: Leg extensions, leg curls, calf raises

Core: Cable crunches, hanging leg raises, ab wheel

Isolation work is not essential for beginners, but it becomes valuable as you advance. It addresses
weak points, adds volume where needed, and allows training around injuries.




When to Vary Exercises - ADVANCED TRAINING
Do not change exercises because you are bored or saw something new on Instagram. Change them
strategically.

Reasons to rotate an exercise:

Progress has stalled for 3+ weeks despite adequate recovery. Sometimes the body simply needs
a different stimulus. A new exercise in the same movement pattern can restart adaptation.

Pain or discomfort appears. If a movement consistently causes discomfort that is not related to
muscle fatigue, switch to a variation that does not. Barbell bench causing shoulder issues? Try
dumbbell bench or a slight incline. Low bar squat bothering your elbows? Try high bar or safety bar.



                                          Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                              15
Weak points become apparent that the current exercise does not address. If your lockout is weak
on bench press, you might rotate in close-grip bench or floor press. If your quads are lagging, you
might rotate in front squats or high bar squats.

You enter a new training block with different goals. The accumulation phase might use more
variation and higher rep ranges. The intensification phase might use fewer exercises with heavier
loads.




How to Rotate Intelligently - ADVANCED TRAINING
When you rotate, stay within the same movement pattern. Do not abandon squatting for leg press
because squats got hard. Do not replace deadlifts with back extensions because you hit a plateau.

Within a movement pattern, move to a variation that addresses your current limitation or provides a
novel stimulus:

If back squat stalls → try front squat, pause squat, or tempo squat.

If conventional deadlift stalls → try sumo, deficit, or pause deadlift.

If flat bench stalls → try incline, close-grip, or pause bench.

If barbell row stalls → try Pendlay row, Meadows row, or chest-supported row

Maintain your Tier 1 movement pattern. Vary the specific exercise within that pattern.




Exercise Variation for Specific Goals - ADVANCED TRAINING
For Bigger Legs:

Quad Emphasis:

  • Primary rotation: Back Squat → Front Squat → High Bar Squat

  • Secondary: Leg press, hack squat, Bulgarian split squats, walking lunges

  • Isolation: Leg extensions, sissy squats

Hamstring Emphasis:

  • Primary rotation: Romanian Deadlift → Stiff-Leg Deadlift → Good Mornings

  • Secondary: Glute-ham raises, Nordic curls

  • Isolation: Leg curls (seated and lying variations)




                                         Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                               16
For Bigger Chest:

  • Primary rotation: Flat Bench → Incline Bench → Close-Grip Bench

  • Secondary: Dumbbell press variations, dips, machine press

  • Isolation: Cable flyes, pec deck, dumbbell flyes

Upper chest often lags. If this is you, prioritise incline work and perform it before flat pressing.

For Bigger Back:

Width (Lats):

  • Primary: Pull-ups → Chin-ups → Neutral Grip Pull-ups

  • Secondary: Lat pulldowns (various grips), straight-arm pulldowns

Thickness (Mid-Back):

  • Primary rotation: Barbell Row → Pendlay Row → Meadows Row

  • Secondary: Cable rows, chest-supported rows, T-bar rows, dumbbell rows

For Bigger Shoulders:

  • Primary rotation: Overhead Press → Push Press → Seated Press

  • Secondary: Arnold press, landmine press, dumbbell press

  • Isolation: Lateral raises (various angles), face pulls, rear delt work

Side delts create the appearance of width. Prioritise lateral raises and rear delt work if your
shoulders lack the capped look.




The Phase 2 Rotation Protocol - ADVANCED TRAINING
Within Phase 2, rotate your primary compound variations once - at the transition between
mesocycles (after Week 4 deload).

Weeks 1-4: Primary variation A (e.g., back squat, conventional deadlift, flat bench) Weeks 5-8:
Primary variation B (e.g., front squat, sumo deadlift, incline bench)

This provides enough time to progress on each variation while preventing staleness.

Keep secondary and isolation work more stable - these can run the full 8 weeks before rotation,
unless a specific need arises.

Intro - ADVANCED TRAINING
You have the foundation. You understand progression and periodization. Now we add advanced
tools to your arsenal - techniques that increase training intensity without simply adding more
volume.

These are powerful tools. They create greater stimulus in less time when used correctly. But they are
also easy to abuse. Overuse leads to excessive fatigue, impaired recovery, and stalled progress.

The rule: use intensity techniques sparingly and strategically. One, maximum two techniques per
workout. Apply them to isolation and secondary exercises more than primary compounds. Never
use multiple techniques on the same exercise.




Rest-Pause Sets - ADVANCED TRAINING
What it is: Extend a set beyond normal failure using brief intra-set rest periods.

How to execute:

 1. Perform a set to near failure (1-2 reps in reserve)

 2. Rack the weight, rest 10-15 seconds (stay at the station, breathe deeply)

 3. Perform more reps until near failure again

 4. Rest 10-15 seconds

 5. One more mini-set to near failure

A rest-pause set might look like: 10 reps → rest → 4 reps → rest → 2 reps. You have now performed
16 total reps with a weight you could only do 10 with straight.

Why it works: The brief rest allows partial ATP regeneration and clearance of metabolites, enabling
additional reps. This extends time under tension and recruits more motor units than a straight set to
failure.

When to use:

  • Final set of an exercise


                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                19
  • Isolation movements (curls, tricep work, lateral raises)

  • Machine exercises where form is less critical under fatigue

When to avoid:

  • Heavy compound movements (squats, deadlifts, bench press)

  • Exercises where technique degrades significantly under fatigue

  • When you are already accumulating excessive fatigue




Drop Sets - ADVANCED TRAINING
What it is: Immediately reduce weight after reaching failure and continue for more reps, repeating
one or more times.

How to execute:

 1. Perform a set to near failure

 2. Immediately reduce weight by 20-30%

 3. Continue to near failure

 4. Reduce weight again

 5. Continue to near failure

A drop set might look like: 50kg x 10 → 40kg x 8 → 30kg x 10

Why it works: By reducing the load, you can continue training past the point where you would
normally fail. This thoroughly exhausts the target muscle and creates significant metabolic stress.

When to use:

  • Isolation exercises

  • Machine movements where weight changes are fast

  • Final set of a muscle group for maximum pump and exhaustion

When to avoid:

  • Free weight compounds where setup time defeats the purpose

  • Movements requiring technical precision

  • More than once per muscle group per session




                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                20
Tempo Manipulation - ADVANCED TRAINING
What it is: Deliberately slowing portions of the rep to increase time under tension.

How to execute: Use a tempo prescription like 4-1-2-0:

  • 4 seconds eccentric (lowering)

  • 1 second pause at bottom

  • 2 seconds concentric (lifting)

  • 0 seconds pause at top

A set of 8 reps with a 4-second eccentric provides 32+ seconds of eccentric tension, compared to
roughly 8 seconds with a normal tempo. Same reps, dramatically different stimulus.

Why it works: The eccentric (lowering) phase causes the most muscle damage and stimulates
significant hypertrophy. Extending this phase increases the growth stimulus without requiring
heavier weights.

When to use:

  • Any exercise, but particularly effective for hypertrophy phases

  • Deload weeks to maintain stimulus with lighter loads

  • Exercises where you want to reinforce technique and control

  • 1-2 exercises per session

When to avoid:

  • Heavy strength work where tempo naturally slows anyway

  • When you need to prioritise load progression over time under tension




Pause Reps - ADVANCED TRAINING
What it is: Add a deliberate pause at the most difficult portion of the movement.

How to execute: At the bottom of a squat, hold for 2-3 seconds. At the chest on bench press, hold
for 2-3 seconds. Eliminate the stretch reflex and build strength from a dead stop.

Why it works: The stretch reflex - the elastic energy stored during the eccentric that helps you
"bounce" out of the bottom - is eliminated. You must generate force from a dead stop, building
strength specifically at your weakest point.




                                       Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                              21
When to use:

  • Primary compounds when working on sticking points

  • When technique breaks down at a specific position

  • In 2-4 week blocks to address specific weaknesses

When to avoid:

  • As a permanent feature of all training

  • When fatigue is already high

  • With loads that compromise the pause position




Mechanical Drop Sets - ADVANCED TRAINING
What it is: Move from a harder variation to an easier variation of the same movement pattern
without rest.

How to execute: Complete reps to near failure on the harder variation, immediately switch to the
easier variation and continue.

Examples:

  • Close-grip chin-ups → Neutral grip chin-ups → Underhand chin-ups

  • Incline dumbbell press → Flat dumbbell press → Decline dumbbell press

  • Deficit push-ups → Standard push-ups → Knee push-ups

Why it works: Instead of reducing load, you reduce the mechanical disadvantage. This allows
continued work on the target muscles after failure on the harder variation.

When to use:

  • Bodyweight exercises where load reduction is not possible

  • When you want extended sets without weight changes

  • For variety and challenge




Application in Phase 2 - ADVANCED TRAINING
Mesocycle 1 (Accumulation - Weeks 1-4): Focus on tempo work and pause reps. These build
muscle, reinforce technique, and increase time under tension without excessive neural fatigue.




                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                             22
Apply to: 1-2 exercises per session, preferably secondary or isolation movements.

Mesocycle 2 (Intensification - Weeks 5-8): Introduce rest-pause and drop sets on isolation work.
Save neural energy for heavy primary lifts.

Apply to: Final sets of isolation exercises, machine work, arm training.




The Critical Rule - ADVANCED TRAINING
Never use multiple intensity techniques on the same exercise. One technique per exercise,
maximum two techniques per session. These are finishers, not the foundation.

If you find yourself relying on intensity techniques every session for every exercise, you are
overcomplicating your training. Progressive overload on basic movements will always be the primary
driver of results.

Intensity techniques are seasoning, not the meal.




Action - ADVANCED TRAINING

  • Apply it only to the final set of one isolation exercise

  • Note the effect on fatigue and recovery

  • Adjust application based on response

Some conditioning modalities actively support your lifting.

Prioritise these:

Sled Work

Pushing and pulling sleds builds leg strength and conditioning simultaneously. It is concentric-only,

later.

Protocol: 10-15 minutes after lower body training, or as standalone conditioning. Push heavy for
strength, push light and fast for conditioning.

Loaded Carries

Farmer's walks, sandbag carries, trap bar carries. These build grip, core stability, and full-body
conditioning while reinforcing good posture.



                                        Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                               27
Protocol: 3-5 sets of 40-60 seconds at end of training. Go heavy for strength emphasis, lighter for

Rowing

Engages the posterior chain - back, glutes, hamstrings. Low impact on joints. Excellent for Zone 2
and interval work.

Protocol: Primary conditioning modality for most men. Buy a rower for home if possible.

Cycling

Low impact, easy to control intensity. Does not interfere with lower body recovery as much as
running.

Protocol: Good option for Zone 2 and intervals. Outdoor cycling adds enjoyment factor.

Incline Treadmill Walking

The simplest Zone 2 option. Set incline to 10-15%, speed to 3-4 mph. Walk and watch something,
listen to something, or think.

Protocol: Ideal for Zone 2. Can do immediately post-lifting or on rest days.




Conditioning to Avoid - ADVANCED TRAINING
Long Distance Running

High impact. Significant interference with lower body strength and recovery. The adaptations it

muscle and strength.

not optimal for your goals.

Excessive HIIT

The fitness industry has overmarketed HIIT. More than 1-2 high-intensity sessions per week impairs
strength recovery. If you are doing HIIT classes 4-5 times per week and lifting, you are likely not
recovering from either.

True HIIT is brief and brutal. If you can do it for 30+ minutes, it is not high intensity.

How to Identify Weak Points - ADVANCED TRAINING
Visual Assessment

Stand in front of a mirror. Look at yourself with objective eyes. What is underdeveloped compared to
surrounding muscles?

Common visual imbalances:

  • Rear delts vs. front delts (most men are front delt dominant)

  • Hamstrings vs. quads (most men are quad dominant)

  • Upper chest vs. lower chest (lower chest often overpowers upper)

  • Mid-back vs. lats (lats often dominate, mid-back lags)

  • Side delts vs. front delts (front delts get hit by all pressing, sides need direct work)

The Apollo physique is balanced. A massive chest with small shoulders looks wrong. Big quads with
no hamstrings looks wrong. Aesthetics require proportion.

Performance Assessment

Where do your lifts break down? The point of failure reveals the weak link.

Squat weak points:

  • Folding forward coming out of the hole → weak upper back, weak core

  • Getting stuck in the hole → weak quads

  • Hips shooting up first → weak quads, technique issue

  • Knees caving → weak glutes, weak adductors

  • Failing at lockout → weak glutes, weak hamstrings

Bench press weak points:

  • Stuck at the chest → weak chest, weak front delts

  • Stuck at mid-range → weak chest, weak triceps

  • Failing at lockout → weak triceps

  • Shoulder pain → weak rotator cuff, poor scapular control




                                         Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                           31
Deadlift weak points:

  • Cannot break the floor → weak quads, weak positioning

  • Stuck at knees → weak back, weak hamstrings

  • Upper back rounding → weak upper back, weak lats

  • Failing at lockout → weak glutes, weak lower back

Row/Pull-up weak points:

  • Cannot initiate pull → weak lats, weak scapular control

  • Cannot complete contraction → weak rhomboids, weak mid-back




Pain and Discomfort Patterns - ADVANCED TRAINING
Recurring discomfort often indicates weakness or imbalance. Your body compensates for weak
areas by overloading others.

Knee pain during squats → possibly weak VMO, weak glutes, poor ankle mobility

Shoulder pain during pressing → possibly weak rotator cuff, weak rear delts, poor thoracic mobility

Lower back fatigue during deadlifts → possibly weak core, weak glutes, weak upper back

Elbow pain during pressing → possibly weak triceps, poor shoulder mechanics

Note: pain can have many causes. These are possibilities, not diagnoses. If pain persists, see a
professional.




Common Weak Points and Their Corrections - ADVANCED
TRAINING
Weak Glutes

Signs: Poor hip drive, excessive forward lean in squat and deadlift, lower back fatigue after hip-
dominant movements, flat backside visually.

Corrections:

  • Hip thrusts: 3x12-15, squeeze hard at top

  • Banded walks: lateral and monster walks for activation

  • Single-leg work: Bulgarian split squats, step-ups, single-leg RDLs



                                       Men of Apollo — Personal Notes                                32
  • Glute-focused deadlift variations: sumo deadlift, trap bar deadlift

  • Daily activation: glute bridges before every session

Weak Upper Back

Signs: Rounding during deadlifts, folding forward in squats, poor posture, limited row strength.

Corrections:

  • Face pulls: daily, 100 total reps (can be spread throughout day)

  • Chest-supported rows: removes lower back from equation, isolates mid-back

  • Band pull-aparts: between all pressing sets

  • Pause deadlifts: reinforce position at weakest point

  • Snatch-grip work: wider grip emphasises upper back

Weak Core

Signs: Lower back fatigue during compound movements, loss of position under load, poor brace,
ribcage flaring.

Corrections:

  • Dead bugs: 3x10 each side, slow and controlled

  • Pallof press: anti-rotation strength, 3x10 each side

  • Loaded carries: farmer's walks, front rack carries

  • Ab wheel rollouts: 3x8-10

  • Breathing drills: learn to brace properly before loading

Weak Hamstrings

Signs: Quad dominance in squat and deadlift, poor hip hinge pattern, knee discomfort, hips
shooting up in squat.

Corrections:

  • Romanian deadlifts: prioritize over conventional pulls temporarily

  • Nordic curls: eccentric focus, build up gradually

  • Glute-ham raises: if available, excellent hamstring builder

  • Leg curls: seated and lying variations, include both

  • Good mornings: light weight, high reps, stretch focus

Weak Rear Delts

Signs: Rounded shoulder posture, shoulder discomfort with pressing, visually flat back of shoulder,
front delt dominance.

Corrections:

  • Face pulls: already mentioned - cannot overstate importance

  • Reverse flyes: light weight, high reps, squeeze at contraction

  • Rear delt rows: elbows high, pull to face level

  • Band pull-aparts: between pressing sets

  • Reduce pressing volume: if rear delts are severely lagging, you may need to temporarily reduce
   pressing while bringing them up




The Prioritization Protocol - ADVANCED TRAINING
You cannot fix everything at once. Identify your top one or two weak points and prioritise them for
one mesocycle (4 weeks).

How to Prioritise Weak Points:

 1. Train weak points first in the session when you are fresh and neural drive is highest

 2. Add 2-4 extra sets per week specifically for weak points

 3. Use activation work before main movements that require the weak muscle

 4. Reduce volume for strong points to create recovery room for added weak point work

Example: If glutes are weak and interfering with squat and deadlift performance:

  • Begin every lower session with 2 sets of glute bridges

  • Add hip thrusts as first exercise on one lower day

  • Add banded walks to warm-up

  • Reduce quad isolation volume temporarily

After 4 Weeks:

Reassess. Has the weak point improved? Use the same assessment methods:

  • Visual: does it look better?

  • Performance: has the sticking point improved?

  • Discomfort: has pain or fatigue in compensating areas decreased?

If improved: maintain with moderate attention, move to next priority If not improved: continue
emphasis or troubleshoot (are you actually executing the corrections consistently?)
