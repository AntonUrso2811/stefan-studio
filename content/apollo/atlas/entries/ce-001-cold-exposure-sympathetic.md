## The Stance

Stefan's stance: cold exposure is a recovery input, not a hero protocol. Apply it after the work is done, in a dose small enough to repeat, and let the parasympathetic rebound carry the signal.

## Plain English

Short, deliberately cold water raises your alertness sharply and shifts your nervous system from sympathetic dominance toward parasympathetic recovery in the hours after. The actual training is the recovery, not the cold itself.

The Apollo dose is conservative on purpose. Three minutes at around 11°C, three to five times a week. That lands within Søberg's published target of roughly 11 minutes of weekly cold exposure. Members chasing 20 minute sessions are running someone else's protocol.

## Technical

Cold immersion at 10 to 14°C produces a catecholamine surge of approximately 2.5 times baseline within three minutes, dominated by norepinephrine. Brown adipose tissue activation upregulates over weeks of repeated exposure, with measurable changes in mitochondrial density and thermogenic capacity. The post-exposure window shows a vagal-tone shift that is visible on next-day HRV in members who track it.

End on cold for adaptation. End on warm for sleep onset within 90 minutes of bed.

## The Studies

In Cell Reports Medicine (2021), Søberg and colleagues demonstrated altered brown fat thermoregulation and enhanced cold-induced thermogenesis in young, healthy, winter-swimming men, establishing the dose-response framing Apollo follows.

In European Journal of Applied Physiology (2000), Šrámek and colleagues documented the magnitude of catecholamine response to cold immersion at varying temperatures, anchoring the sympathetic-surge claim.
