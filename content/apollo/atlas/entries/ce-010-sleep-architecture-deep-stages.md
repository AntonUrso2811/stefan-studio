## The Stance

Stefan's stance: you do not grow in the gym, you grow when you recover. Of every recovery input on the stack, sleep is the one that cannot be substituted, supplemented, or shortened without consequence. The protocol is duration plus consistency.

## Plain English

Sleep is not a single block. It is a structured progression through four stages, cycling roughly every 90 minutes. The first half of the night is dominated by deep slow-wave sleep, which is when the body releases growth hormone, repairs tissue, and clears metabolic waste from the brain. The second half is dominated by REM sleep, which is when motor learning and emotional processing consolidate.

Cut sleep short and you lose the second half. The protocol is 7 to 9 hours of total time in bed, same window every night, weekdays and weekends.

## Technical

Slow-wave sleep (NREM3) is when growth-hormone pulse release peaks, when sympathetic tone bottoms, and when the glymphatic system flushes cerebrospinal fluid through the brain's interstitial space. REM sleep, distributed primarily in the second half of the night, consolidates motor learning from the day's training. Sleep restriction below 6 hours, sustained over a week, has measurable effects on testosterone, insulin sensitivity, and glucose tolerance in healthy adult men.

## The Studies

In JAMA (2011), Leproult and Van Cauter demonstrated that one week of sleep restriction to 5 hours nightly reduced daytime testosterone levels in young healthy men by 10 to 15 percent, comparable to ageing 10 to 15 years. The site already cites this study on /protocols.

In Sleep Medicine (2008), Van Cauter and colleagues reviewed the metabolic consequences of sleep loss, including reduced glucose tolerance, increased cortisol, and altered appetite-regulating hormones.

Walker (2017) consolidates the broader sleep-architecture literature in a book-length review of the published peer-reviewed work.
