## The Stance

Stefan's stance: a strong man who cannot move well is not capable. The mobility input is not yoga and it is not foam rolling. It is loaded movement at the positions you are weakest. Done weekly, it preserves the body's ability to express strength.

## Plain English

Most men over 35 lose range of motion not because their muscles are tight, but because they have stopped going to the end of their range under load. Passive stretching does not solve this. The fix is loaded end-range work. Jefferson curls. Deep split squats. Hanging holds. Controlled rotation under tension.

The dose is 10 to 15 minutes, three to five times weekly. Bolted onto warm-up or cool-down. Not a separate yoga class.

## Technical

Active end-range loading drives both connective-tissue remodelling (collagen alignment, tendon stiffness adaptation) and motor-control acquisition at positions previously unguarded. The protective effect on the joint comes from neural drive at end-range, not from increased passive flexibility. Passive stretching alone produces transient ROM gains without protective capacity, which is why traditional warm-up stretching often fails to reduce injury rates in the literature.

## The Studies

In European Journal of Applied Physiology (2011), Behm and Chaouachi reviewed the acute effects of stretching modalities on performance, with the key finding that static stretching alone reduces force output without consistently improving function.

In Applied Physiology, Nutrition, and Metabolism (2016), Behm and colleagues reviewed the relationship between stretching, range of motion, and injury rates, supporting the position that loaded movement at end-range is more protective than passive flexibility work alone.
