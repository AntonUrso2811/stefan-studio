## The Stance

Stefan's stance: this is the module most fitness programmes will never give you. Gut health is not a supplement category. It is fibre, variety, and consistency, run as a daily protocol.

## Plain English

The gut microbiome is the colony of bacteria that lives in your large intestine. It eats the fibre you eat. When it has enough, it produces short-chain fatty acids, including butyrate, which feeds the cells lining your colon and sends signals upstream to your immune system and brain.

The protocol is volume and variety. 30 to 50 grams of fibre daily. 30 different plant foods per week (the Sonnenburg lab heuristic). The diversity matters as much as the gross intake.

## Technical

Fermentable fibre (resistant starches, soluble fibre, certain insoluble fibres) is metabolised by gut microbiota into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), primarily acetate, propionate, and butyrate. Butyrate is the dominant fuel for colonocytes, a histone deacetylase inhibitor with epigenetic effects, and a regulator of intestinal barrier function. Low-fibre diets reduce microbial diversity over generations and impair butyrate production, with downstream effects on inflammation, mucosal barrier, and metabolic health.

## The Studies

In Cell Metabolism (2014), Sonnenburg and Sonnenburg demonstrated, in mouse models, that a diet deficient in microbiota-accessible carbohydrates produces compounding losses in microbial diversity across generations, supporting the dietary-fibre claim.

In Cell Host and Microbe (2018), Makki and colleagues reviewed the broader evidence on dietary fibre and gut microbiota in human health and disease, anchoring the volume and variety prescription Apollo carries.
