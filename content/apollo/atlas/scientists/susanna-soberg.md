## Plain English

A Danish physiologist whose lab work on brown adipose tissue and deliberate cold exposure produced the closest thing the field has to a usable dose. Her published findings (eleven minutes per week of cold immersion, around 57 minutes of heat for sauna users) is what Apollo's recovery protocol leans on for its weekly target.

## What Apollo uses

Søberg's work resolves a question Stefan kept getting from members: *how much cold actually matters.*

- **The eleven minutes per week target.** Apollo's M07 cold finisher protocol is dosed to land within that range across three to five sessions weekly. Members are not chasing duration. They are accumulating dose.
- **End on cold for adaptation.** Søberg's published guidance is that contrast protocols should finish on the cold side for the metabolic and adaptive adjustments. M11 carries this verbatim.
- **Cold as a sympathetic stressor with parasympathetic rebound.** The vagal-tone literature she draws on supports Apollo's framing of cold as a recovery input rather than a training input. The post-exposure parasympathetic shift is the actual signal.

## What Apollo does not echo

- Apollo does not romanticise the cold. The Wim Hof and ice-bath subcultures are well outside Apollo's register. Søberg's work is the antidote, not the source, for that culture.
- Apollo does not load cold exposure pre-strength-training. The hypertrophy literature is clear that cold immediately after lifting blunts the adaptive signal. Apollo's protocol accommodates this.
- Apollo does not require expensive equipment. Søberg's work establishes that cold showers and natural cold-water immersion produce most of the signal. M07 protocols are equipment-light.

## Honest note

Søberg's published cold-exposure dose comes from a specific cohort and a specific protocol. The eleven-minute-per-week target is a useful anchor but should not be treated as the only valid dose. Apollo runs it as a target to land near, not a number to maximise.
