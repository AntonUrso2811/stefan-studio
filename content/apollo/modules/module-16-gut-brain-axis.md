## The Microbiome Explained

### Intro
> This is the module most fitness programs will never give you. And it is arguably the one that
matters most for your long-term results.

You can train perfectly. You can eat the right foods in the right amounts at the right times. But if your
gut is not functioning properly, you are not absorbing those nutrients. You are not producing the
neurotransmitters that drive your mood and energy. You are not regulating the inflammation that
determines how well you recover.

Your gut contains approximately 100 trillion microorganisms - bacteria, fungi, viruses, and archaea
collectively known as the gut microbiome. These organisms are not passengers. They are active
participants in your biology.

> They produce neurotransmitters, metabolise nutrients, regulate your immune system, and
communicate directly with your brain via the vagus nerve.

Approximately 95% of your body's serotonin is produced in the gut. Roughly 50% of your dopamine
is produced there. GABA, the calming neurotransmitter that helps you sleep and manage stress,
your gut bacteria produce that too.

> When your gut is dysfunctional, everything downstream suffers. Energy. Mood. Focus. Recovery.
Body composition. Skin quality. Immune function. Everything.

This module takes you through the science, the testing, and the practical protocols to optimise this
system.

### Your Gut
Trillions of organisms that run your biology.

The human gut microbiome contains an estimated 100 trillion microorganisms representing over
1,000 different species. The total genetic material of these organisms called the metagenome —
contains 150 times more genes than the human genome. By gene count, you are more microbe than
human.

This is not abstract biology. These organisms perform functions that your human cells cannot:

Nutrient Metabolism: Gut bacteria break down dietary fibre into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)
primarily butyrate, propionate, and acetate. Butyrate is the primary fuel source for colonocytes (the
cells lining your colon) and plays a critical role in maintaining gut barrier integrity. Without adequate
butyrate production, the gut lining becomes permeable, a condition commonly called "leaky gut" -
allowing partially digested food particles and bacterial endotoxins to enter the bloodstream.

Vitamin Production: Your gut bacteria synthesise several essential vitamins including Vitamin K2,
B12, biotin, folate, and thiamine. Deficiencies in these vitamins despite adequate dietary intake may
indicate microbiome dysfunction.

Immune Regulation: Approximately 70% of your immune system resides in the gut-associated
lymphoid tissue (GALT). The microbiome trains and modulates immune function. Dysbiosis - an
imbalance in microbial composition is associated with both overactive immune responses (allergies,
autoimmunity) and underactive responses (frequent infections).

Neurotransmitter Production: As mentioned, the gut produces the majority of your serotonin and
significant amounts of dopamine and GABA. The enteric nervous system - the network of neurons
embedded in the gut wall contains over 500 million neurons and operates semi-independently from
the central nervous system. This is why scientists refer to the gut as "the second brain."

### What Destroys the Microbiome
  • Antibiotics. A single course of broad-spectrum antibiotics can eliminate up to 30% of gut
   bacteria species. Some species never fully recover without deliberate intervention. Research
   published in Nature Microbiology (2018) found that certain bacterial populations took up to six
   months to partially recover after a single antibiotic course, and some did not recover at all.

  • Ultra-processed foods. Emulsifiers, artificial sweeteners, and preservatives directly damage the
   gut lining and alter microbial composition by promoting intestinal inflammation and metabolic
   dysfunction. They disrupt the mucus layer protecting the intestinal wall.

  • Chronic stress. Cortisol reduces blood flow to the digestive tract, decreases enzyme
   production, and alters gut motility. The vagus nerve - the primary communication highway
   between the brain and gut becomes less active under chronic stress, reducing gut function.

  • Alcohol. Regular alcohol consumption damages the intestinal lining and promotes the growth of
   harmful bacteria while suppressing beneficial species.

  • Poor sleep. Circadian disruption alters the composition of the microbiome. Shift workers
   consistently show different microbial profiles from those with regular sleep schedules.

  • Low-fibre diets. Without fibre, the beneficial bacteria that produce SCFAs starve. When starved,
   some species begin consuming the gut's protective mucus layer instead, thinning it and
   increasing permeability.

### Nutrient Absorption
The direct line between your gut and your results.

This is where it gets practical. How does a dysfunctional gut actually affect your training,
recovery, energy, and body composition?

You are not what you eat. You are what you absorb.

If your gut lining is compromised, inflamed, permeable, or lacking adequate enzyme production, you
can eat a perfect diet and still be functionally malnourished. The nutrients pass through your
digestive tract without being properly broken down and absorbed.

> Signs of poor nutrient absorption: persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep, slow recovery from
training, brittle nails, thinning hair, frequent illness, poor wound healing, mood instability despite
good lifestyle habits.

The Protein Problem:

Protein absorption requires adequate stomach acid (HCl) to denature proteins and activate pepsin,
the primary protein-digesting enzyme. It requires pancreatic enzymes (proteases) to further break
proteins into peptides and amino acids. And it requires a healthy intestinal lining with adequate
surface area (provided by intact villi) to absorb those amino acids.

If any step in this chain is compromised, you are not getting the full benefit of the protein you
consume. A man eating 180g of protein daily with poor digestive function may be effectively
absorbing 120g or less.

> How to assess: If you experience bloating, gas, or heaviness after high-protein meals, this is a
signal that protein digestion is suboptimal. Other indicators include undigested food visible in stool
and a feeling of food "sitting" in the stomach for hours.

### Energy and Mental Clarity
The gut-brain axis is bidirectional - the gut communicates with the brain and the brain
communicates with the gut. When gut inflammation is present, pro-inflammatory cytokines travel via
the vagus nerve and bloodstream to the brain, creating neuroinflammation.

The subjective experience of neuroinflammation is familiar to everyone: brain fog, difficulty
concentrating, low motivation, irritability, and fatigue that persists despite adequate sleep and
caffeine.

> Gut inflammation is associated with reduced activity in brain regions responsible for motivation
and reward processing. In plain terms, an inflamed gut makes you feel tired, unmotivated, and
unable to focus, regardless of how disciplined your habits are.

### Body Composition
Gut microbiome composition directly influences body composition through multiple mechanisms:

 1. Energy Harvest. Different bacterial profiles extract different amounts of energy from the same
   food. Research has consistently shown that obese individuals tend to have gut microbiomes that
   are more efficient at extracting calories from food. A landmark study by Turnbaugh et al. (2006)
   in Nature demonstrated that transferring the microbiome from obese mice to lean, germ-free
   mice caused weight gain even on the same diet!

 2. Inflammation and Insulin Resistance. Bacterial endotoxins (lipopolysaccharides/LPS) that
   cross a permeable gut lining trigger systemic inflammation and promote insulin resistance.
   Insulin resistance makes fat loss harder and fat storage easier.

 3. Appetite Regulation. Gut bacteria influence the production of hunger and satiety hormones
   including ghrelin, leptin, and GLP-1. Dysbiosis can dysregulate appetite signalling, making it
   harder to maintain appropriate caloric intake.

### Recovery
Systemic inflammation from gut dysfunction impairs recovery from training. If your gut is chronically
producing inflammatory signals, your body is fighting a two-front war, the controlled damage from
training and the uncontrolled damage from gut permeability.

> This is why some men train hard, eat well, sleep adequately, and still feel overtrained. The missing
variable is often gut health.

##    How to Eat for Gut Health

### Tests you can do
What tests exist, what they tell you, and what is actually worth doing.

> You cannot optimise what you do not measure. But the gut health testing market is full of
expensive panels that provide marginally useful information. Here is what actually matters.

Tier 1: Accessible and Useful

Comprehensive Stool Analysis

This is the gold standard for gut health assessment:

  • Microbial diversity and composition (which bacteria are present and in what ratios)

  • Digestive function markers (pancreatic elastase, fat absorption markers)

  • Inflammatory markers (calprotectin, lactoferrin)

  • Immune markers (secretory IgA)

  • Parasite and pathogen screening

  • Short-chain fatty acid production

Cost: typically £200-400 / $250-500 depending on the provider and panel.

> What it tells you: whether you have adequate digestive enzyme production, whether you have
bacterial imbalances, whether you have active gut inflammation, and whether there are any
pathogens present. This is actionable information that directly guides protocol design.

Food Sensitivity Testing — The Honest Assessment

IgG food sensitivity panels are widely marketed but the scientific evidence for their clinical utility is
limited. The American Academy of Allergy, Asthma & Immunology does not recommend IgG testing
for food sensitivities.

> The better approach: a structured elimination diet. Remove the most common triggers (dairy,
gluten, eggs, soy) for 3-4 weeks. Reintroduce one at a time, three days between each. Monitor
symptoms - bloating, energy, skin, mood, digestive comfort. Your body is the most sensitive testing
instrument available.

Tier 2: Worth Considering

Breath Tests for SIBO (Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth)

If you experience persistent bloating, especially after meals containing fermentable carbohydrates
(onions, garlic, beans, wheat), SIBO may be a factor. Lactulose or glucose breath tests measure
hydrogen and methane gas production - elevated levels indicate bacterial overgrowth in the small
intestine where bacteria should be relatively sparse.

SIBO affects an estimated 10-15% of the general population and significantly higher percentages in
those with IBS symptoms.

Basic Blood Work

Not specifically a gut test, but relevant markers include:

  • C-Reactive Protein (CRP) — systemic inflammation marker

  • Vitamin D — often low when gut absorption is compromised

  • Iron/Ferritin — can be low despite adequate dietary intake if absorption is impaired

  • B12 — absorption requires intrinsic factor and adequate stomach acid

  • Full blood count — can reveal anaemia or immune dysregulation

Tier 3: Emerging but Not Yet Essential

Microbiome sequencing services (like those offered by various direct-to-consumer companies)
provide detailed species-level composition data. The science of interpreting this data is still
evolving. We know what a healthy microbiome looks like in broad terms, high diversity, adequate
populations of key species but personalised recommendations based on sequencing are still in early
stages.

> If budget allows and you want the data, these tests are interesting and can track changes over
time. But do not expect specific, actionable protocols from the results alone.

> What to Do With Your Results

If you get tested, do not try to interpret complex results yourself. Work with a functional medicine
practitioner or integrative gastroenterologist who specialises in gut health. The protocols in this
module provide a solid foundation for everyone. Testing tells you where to focus additional effort.

### Chewing
Practical nutrition for microbiome optimisation.

Phase 1 and Phase 2 taught you what to eat for body composition and performance. This lesson
teaches you how to eat, the mechanical and practical elements that determine whether your body
actually utilises what you put into it.

Chewing — The Forgotten First Step of Digestion

Digestion begins in the mouth. This is not a figure of speech.

Salivary amylase is an enzyme in saliva it breaks down starches. Lingual lipase begins fat digestion.
Mechanical mastication breaks food into smaller particles, dramatically increasing the surface area
available for enzymatic breakdown in the stomach and small intestine.

Research published in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition (2014) found that increasing
chewing cycles from 15 to 40 per bite resulted in a 12% reduction in caloric intake and significantly
higher levels of satiety hormones (GLP-1 and CCK). In a separate study, thorough chewing
increased nutrient absorption from a test meal by approximately 15%.

Most men chew each bite 5-10 times before swallowing. This is inadequate.

The Chewing Protocol:

  • Minimum 20 chews per bite for soft foods. Rice, bread, cooked vegetables.

  • Minimum 30 chews per bite for dense foods. Meat, raw vegetables, nuts.

  • Put the fork down between bites. This prevents the conveyor belt eating that most men default
   to.

  • A meal should take 15-20 minutes minimum. If you are finishing a full meal in under 10
   minutes, you are not chewing adequately.

> This feels painfully slow at first. You will adapt. The benefits are not subtle - reduced bloating,
improved satiety, better nutrient absorption, and improved digestive comfort.

### Foods That Build the Microbiome
Prebiotic Foods — These are fibres that feed beneficial bacteria. They are the fertiliser for your gut
garden.

Key sources: garlic, onions, leeks, asparagus, Jerusalem artichokes, dandelion greens, bananas
(especially slightly green), oats, apples, flaxseeds, seaweed.

Target: 5-10g of prebiotic fibre daily. This is achievable through a diet rich in the foods above without
supplementation.

Probiotic Foods — These are foods containing live beneficial bacteria.

Key sources: natural yoghurt (with live cultures), kefir, sauerkraut (unpasteurised), kimchi, miso,
tempeh, kombucha (limited - watch sugar content), raw apple cider vinegar

> Target: at least one serving of fermented food daily. This is more effective for microbiome diversity
than probiotic supplements for most people.

Polyphenol-Rich Foods — Polyphenols are plant compounds that selectively feed beneficial
bacteria.

Key sources: dark chocolate (70%+ cacao), blueberries, green tea, red wine (moderate), olive oil,
coffee, colourful vegetables, herbs and spices.

Bone Broth — Contains glycine, glutamine, collagen, and gelatin all of which support gut lining
repair. Homemade bone broth simmered for 12-24 hours is far superior to store-bought versions.
1-2 cups daily is a therapeutic dose for gut repair. If you don't have the time to make it at home then
get the store one, but once you make home made broth it can last you for the whole week.

### Foods That Damage the Microbiome
We covered the major offenders in Phase 1 nutrition - ultra-processed foods, seed oils, excessive
sugar. Here we get specific about the gut-damaging mechanisms:

  • Emulsifiers (polysorbate 80, carboxymethylcellulose) — found in ice cream, salad dressings,
   processed sauces. Directly disrupt the mucus layer protecting the intestinal wall.

  • Artificial sweeteners — sucralose, aspartame, and saccharin have been shown to negatively
   alter gut microbiome composition in human studies. Stick away from all of this junk. You don't
   need it.

  • Excessive alcohol — damages the gut lining and promotes growth of pathogenic bacteria.

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin) — increase intestinal permeability when used regularly. Use
   sparingly and never chronically.

### Meal Structure for Gut Health
  • Begin meals with vegetables or bitter foods (rocket/arugula, bitter greens, apple cider vinegar in
   water). Bitters stimulate digestive enzyme production.

  • Eat protein with adequate stomach acid support — if digestion of protein is difficult, try 1
   tablespoon of raw apple cider vinegar in a small amount of water 15 minutes before meals.

  • Do not drink large volumes of liquid with meals — this dilutes digestive enzymes. Small sips are
   fine. Hydrate between meals.

  • Allow 3-4 hours between meals for the migrating motor complex (MMC) to function. The MMC is
   a sweeping contraction pattern that clears debris from the small intestine between meals.
   Constant snacking prevents this housekeeping function.

### The 5R Protocol for Gut Restoration
Strategic supplementation and lifestyle strategies.

Supplements are the second line of defence. Food comes first. But when the gut has been
compromised through years of poor diet, antibiotic use, stress, or illness, targeted supplementation
can accelerate repair.

This is the framework used by functional medicine practitioners worldwide. It is not a quick fix
expect 8-12 weeks for meaningful change.

1. Remove — Eliminate things that damage the gut. Processed foods, known food sensitivities,
excessive alcohol, unnecessary NSAIDs. If SIBO or pathogenic overgrowth has been identified
through testing, antimicrobial protocols may be needed (work with a practitioner for this).

2. Replace — Restore what is missing for proper digestion.

  • Digestive enzymes — A broad-spectrum digestive enzyme taken with meals can improve
   protein, fat, and carbohydrate digestion. Look for a formula containing protease, lipase, amylase,
   and ideally lactase and cellulase. Take with the first bite of each main meal.

  • Betaine HCl — If low stomach acid is suspected (bloating immediately after eating, feeling of
   food sitting in the stomach, visible undigested food in stool), betaine HCl with pepsin taken at
   the start of protein-containing meals can help. Start with one capsule (600-650mg) and increase
   by one capsule per meal until you feel mild warmth in the stomach, then back off by one
   capsule. That is your dose.

  • Bile support — If fat digestion is poor (greasy or floating stools, nausea after fatty meals), ox
   bile supplements can support fat emulsification and absorption.

3. Reinoculate — Reintroduce beneficial bacteria.

  • Fermented foods daily — As covered. This is first-line.

  • Probiotic supplements — Look for multi-strain formulas containing Lactobacillus and
   Bifidobacterium species with a minimum of 10 billion CFU. Saccharomyces boulardii is a
   beneficial yeast that supports gut barrier function and is particularly useful during and after
   antibiotic use. Soil-based probiotics (Bacillus species) are more resistant to stomach acid and
   may colonise more effectively.

  • Timing: Take probiotics on an empty stomach (first thing in the morning or before bed) for better
   survival through stomach acid.

4. Repair — Support the healing of the intestinal lining.

Key supplements for gut lining repair:

  • L-Glutamine (5-10g daily) — The primary fuel source for enterocytes (intestinal lining cells).
   Research supports its role in maintaining gut barrier function and reducing intestinal
   permeability. Take on an empty stomach.

  • Zinc Carnosine (75mg twice daily) — Specifically targets the gut lining. Research published in
   Gut demonstrated that zinc carnosine stabilised the gut mucosa and reduced intestinal
   permeability.

  • Collagen/Gelatin (10-15g daily) — Provides glycine and proline, the amino acids most
   concentrated in gut lining tissue. Bone broth is the whole-food alternative.

  • Omega-3 Fatty Acids (2-3g EPA/DHA daily) — Anti-inflammatory. Supports resolution of gut
   inflammation.

  • Slippery Elm and Marshmallow Root — Demulcent herbs that form a protective layer over the
   gut lining. Useful during active repair. Available as powders or capsules.

5. Rebalance — Address lifestyle factors that maintain gut health long-term.

  • Stress management (vagus nerve activation through breathing protocols — you already have
   these from Phase 2)

  • Adequate sleep (covered in Module 4 of this phase)

  • Regular exercise (you are already doing this)

  • Time in nature (exposure to environmental microbes supports diversity)

  • Limit unnecessary antibiotic use

  • Fibre intake of 25-40g daily for ongoing prebiotic support

> Disclaimer: I am not prescribing any of this. Seek out professional advice/prescriptions from your
Doctor. I am only saying what I've done and used that has clinical backing and confirmation from
both of my parents that are medical doctors.

### The Apollo Gut Stack (Daily Protocol)
Morning (empty stomach):

  • L-Glutamine: 5g in water

  • Probiotic: multi-strain, 10+ billion CFU

With meals:

  • Digestive enzyme with each main meal

  • 1 tbsp apple cider vinegar before protein-heavy meals (diluted in water)

Evening:

  • Bone broth: 1 cup (or collagen: 10g)

  • Zinc carnosine: 75mg

  • Omega-3: 2-3g EPA/DHA (if not already taking from Phase 1 protocol)

Duration: Run this full protocol for 8-12 weeks. After that, maintain with fermented foods daily,
adequate fibre, and the basic supplement stack from Phase 1. The intensive repair supplements (L-
glutamine, zinc carnosine, digestive enzymes) can be cycled - 8 weeks on, 4 weeks off and
reintroduced if symptoms return.

## Building Your Gut Health Protocol
Putting it all together.

You now understand the science. Here is your execution plan.

Week 1-2: Assessment and Removal

  • Order a comprehensive stool test if budget allows. If not, proceed with the protocol based on
   symptoms.

  • Begin the elimination diet: remove dairy, gluten, artificial sweeteners, and processed foods for 21
   days.

  • Start the chewing protocol — minimum 20 chews per bite, every meal.

  • Introduce 1 serving of fermented food daily.

  • Begin apple cider vinegar before meals (1 tbsp in water).

  • Start L-Glutamine (5g morning, empty stomach).

Week 3-4: Replace and Reinoculate

  • Add digestive enzyme with each main meal.

  • Increase fermented food to 2 servings daily.

  • Start probiotic supplement.

  • Add bone broth or collagen daily.

  • Begin zinc carnosine (75mg, 2x daily).

  • Increase prebiotic food intake — aim for 2-3 servings of prebiotic-rich foods daily.

Week 5-8: Repair and Optimise

  • Continue full supplement protocol.

  • Begin food reintroduction if you did the elimination — one food every 3 days, tracking symptoms
   in a journal.

  • Review stool test results with practitioner if applicable.

  • Adjust protocol based on findings.

  • Track energy, bloating, digestion, mood, and recovery as data points.

Week 9-12: Maintain and Assess

  • Transition to maintenance protocol — fermented foods, adequate fibre, basic supplements.

  • Cycle off intensive repair supplements.

  • Compare energy, recovery, and digestion to baseline.

  • Consider retest if initial testing was done.

What You Should Notice:

By week 4-6 of consistent protocol execution: reduced bloating, more consistent energy throughout
the day, improved mental clarity, better recovery between training sessions, more formed and
regular bowel movements, and potentially improved body composition as nutrient absorption and
inflammation improve.

If you notice minimal improvement after 8 weeks, there may be an underlying condition — SIBO,
parasitic infection, or food sensitivity that requires professional assessment. Do not continue to
guess. Get tested and work with a qualified practitioner.

Non-Negotiables Going Forward:

 1. Chew your food properly. Every meal. For the rest of your life.

 2. Eat fermented food daily.

 3. Eat adequate fibre (25-40g).

 4. Limit processed food, artificial sweeteners, and emulsifiers.

5. Allow 3-4 hours between meals.

6. Manage stress - the gut-brain axis runs both directions.
