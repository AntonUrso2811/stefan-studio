## Summary

Nutrition without the calorie-counting obsession. A plate-based visual framework, a protein floor, and a kitchen-first mentality. Built on five principles: quality over quantity, protein as priority, intuition over obsession, sustainability over perfection, no demonisation of food groups.

## Frameworks / protocols

**The Five Nutrition Principles**
1. Quality over quantity — composition matters more than obsessive calorie counting
2. Protein as the priority — highest-return macronutrient (muscle, satiety, thermic effect)
3. Intuition over obsession — tracking is a learning tool, not an end state
4. Sustainability over perfection — any diet you can't maintain for years is a waste
5. No demonisation of food groups — carbs aren't evil, fat isn't evil, the dose makes the poison

**The Apollo Plate Method**
- **Protein** — 1/4 to 1/3 of plate. Palm-size portion, every meal (~30–50g). Beef, chicken, pork, lamb, fish, seafood, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese.
- **Vegetables & Fruits** — 1/3 to 1/2 of plate. Prioritise variety and colour. Leafy greens, cruciferous, peppers, tomatoes, onions, mushrooms, carrots, zucchini. Berries are nutrient-dense.
- **Carbohydrates** — 1/4 of plate (flex by activity). Rice, potatoes, sweet potatoes, oats, quinoa, whole-grain bread/pasta. Fist-size on training days; reduce on rest.
- **Fats** — incorporated naturally from protein sources and cooking methods. Olive oil, butter, ghee, avocado, nuts, seeds, fatty fish, whole eggs, cheese.

**The Non-Negotiables**
- No ultra-processed foods as meal staples
- No seed oils
- No added sugars in main meals
- No artificial ingredients you can't pronounce

**Protein Floor** — 0.8–1g per pound of bodyweight daily (≈140–176g for an 80kg man). Spread across 3–4 meals, 30–50g per meal. This is a floor, not a ceiling.

**Reference protein amounts** — 3–4 eggs = 18–24g · 200g chicken breast = 40g · 200g beef = 40–45g · 200g salmon = 40g · 250g Greek yogurt = 25g.

**Action Items (Week's implementation)**
1. Audit your kitchen — remove processed junk; stock quality proteins, vegetables, whole-food carbs
2. Track protein for 5 days — just protein, not everything
3. Practice the Plate Method at every main meal
4. Drink 2–3 litres of water daily

The Apollo Nutrition Philosophy
FRAMEWORK (FOUNDATIONS)
Forget everything the fitness industry has told you about eating.

You don't need to weigh your chicken breast to the gram. You don't need to track every calorie in an
app. You don't need to eat six meals a day or drink protein shakes within 30 minutes of training or
your muscles will fall off.

Most of that is bs. Some of it is outright nonsense. And almost all of it creates an unhealthy
relationship with food that makes eating feel like a math problem instead of a normal human activity.

The Apollo Nutrition Framework is built on a different foundation: eat like a capable adult who
gives a damn about his body.

That means:

Quality over quantity. The composition of your diet matters more than obsessive calorie counting.
A man eating 2,500 calories of whole foods — meat, fish, eggs, vegetables, fruits, quality fats, and
unprocessed carbohydrates — will look and feel dramatically different from a man eating 2,500
calories of processed garbage. Same number, completely different outcome. Focus on what you eat
first. The numbers take care of themselves.

Protein as the priority. Of all the things you could focus on, protein intake gives you the highest
return. It builds and maintains muscle. It keeps you full. It has the highest thermic effect, meaning
your body burns more calories digesting it. Hit your protein target, and most other nutritional
problems solve themselves.

Intuition over obsession. The goal is to develop an intuitive understanding of how to eat well, not
to become dependent on apps and spreadsheets forever. Tracking can be a useful learning tool for
a period of time. But the end state is a man who knows how to eat properly without needing to log
every bite. That's freedom.

Sustainability over perfection. Any diet you can't maintain for years is a waste of time. Extreme
approaches produce extreme rebounds. We're building habits that become automatic, ways of
eating that feel normal, not restrictive. You should be able to eat this way at home, at restaurants,
while traveling, at social events etc... If your nutrition approach only works in a controlled
environment, it's not a real approach.

No demonisation of food groups. Carbs are not evil. Fat is not evil. Sugar in moderation won't kill
you. The dose makes the poison. Extreme restriction leads to extreme cravings. We eat a balanced
diet of whole foods, and we don't lose our minds over the occasional bite of tiramisu.

This is how men ate before the fitness industry complicated everything. Real food. Enough protein.
Vegetables. Not too much processed junk.

It worked for generations.

It still works now.

The Apollo Plate Method
FRAMEWORK (FOUNDATIONS)
You don't need to count calories. You need to see your plate.

The Apollo Plate Method is a visual framework for building meals that support your goals without
requiring a food scale or tracking app. Master this, and you'll intuitively eat in a way that builds
muscle, manages body fat, and keeps you energised.

The Framework:

Picture your plate divided into sections:

PROTEIN — 1/4 to 1/3 of the plate

Every main meal includes a substantial portion of quality protein. This is the anchor of the meal,
everything else builds around it.

What counts: Beef, chicken, pork, lamb, fish, seafood, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese. Animal
sources are most bioavailable and complete. If you eat plant-based, you'll need to be more
intentional about combining sources and likely need higher total volume.

Visual guide: A portion roughly the size of your palm, or slightly larger. For most men, this translates
to 30-50g of protein per meal.

VEGETABLES & FRUITS — 1/3 to 1/2 of the plate

This is where most men fail. Vegetables provide fibre, micronutrients, and volume that keeps you full
without excessive calories. They're also where you get the phytonutrients that support everything
from gut health to immune function.

What counts: Any vegetable. Prioritise variety and colour - different colours indicate different
nutrient profiles. Leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, brussel sprouts),
peppers, tomatoes, onions, mushrooms, carrots, zucchini. Fruits in moderation - berries are
particularly nutrient-dense.

Visual guide: Half your plate should be covered in plants. More is fine. Less is a problem.

CARBOHYDRATES — 1/4 of the plate (flex based on activity)

Carbs are fuel. They support training performance, recovery, and hormone function. The amount you
need depends on your activity level.

What counts: Rice (white or brown), potatoes, sweet potatoes, oats, quinoa, whole grain bread,
pasta. Minimally processed sources that your great-grandmother would recognise as food.

Visual guide: About a fist-sized portion on training days. Reduce slightly on rest days when energy
demands are lower. This is where you have flexibility.

FATS — incorporated naturally

You don't need to add a "fat section" to your plate. Fats come naturally from your protein sources
(eggs, fatty fish, meat) and from cooking methods (olive oil, butter, ghee). They also come from
additions like avocado, nuts, cheese, and quality dressings.

What counts: Extra virgin olive oil for cooking and dressing, butter or ghee for cooking, avocado,
nuts and seeds, fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), whole eggs, cheese.

What to avoid: Industrial seed oils (canola, soybean, corn, vegetable oil), anything hydrogenated,
excessive fried foods.

The Non-Negotiables:

  • No ultra-processed foods as meal staples

  • No seed oils

  • No added sugars in main meals

  • No artificial ingredients you can't pronounce

Application:

You don't need to measure. You need to look at your plate and ask: Is there a solid protein source?
Are vegetables taking up significant space? Are my carbs coming from whole food sources? Am I
cooking with quality fats?

If yes to all four, you're eating well. Do this for 90% of your meals, and the results will follow.

Hit Your Protein Floor
(FOUNDATIONS)
If you only focus on one nutritional target, make it protein.

Protein is the most important macronutrient for body composition. It builds and repairs muscle
tissue. It's the most satiating macronutrient, keeping you full longer than carbs or fat. It has the

highest thermic effect of food — your body burns 20-30% of protein calories just digesting it,
compared to 5-10% for carbs and 0-3% for fat.

Research consistently shows that higher protein intakes support muscle retention during fat loss,
improves body composition, and enhances recovery from training. This isn't controversial. It's one
of the most well-established findings in nutrition science.

Your Target: 0.8-1g per pound of bodyweight per day

For an 80kg man, that's 140-176g of protein daily. Aim for the middle to upper end of that range.

This is your floor, not your ceiling. You can eat more. You're unlikely to eat too much unless you're
forcing down protein shakes all day. Most men under eat protein, not overeat it.

Spread It Across Meals

Don't try to hit your entire protein target in one meal. Research on muscle protein synthesis
suggests that spreading protein intake across 3-4 meals optimises the anabolic response. Each
meal should contain 30-50g of protein for most men.

This is why breakfast matters. If you're eating toast and coffee for breakfast, you're starting the day
in a protein deficit you'll struggle to make up.

Reference Amounts:

Use these to build intuition:

  • 3 - 4 whole eggs = 18 - 24g protein

  • 200g chicken breast = 40g protein

  • 200g beef (ground or steak) = 40-45g protein

  • 200g salmon = 40g protein

  • 250g Greek yogurt = 25g protein

  • 200g cottage cheese = 18-22g protein

Track Loosely for One Week

If you've never paid attention to protein intake, spend one week tracking loosely. You don't need an
app, just write down what you eat and look up the protein content. Most men are shocked to
discover how little protein they actually consume.

After a week, you'll have the intuition to hit your target without tracking. You'll know what a high-
protein meal looks like. You'll know which foods are protein-dense and which are protein-poor.

Prioritise Whole Food Sources

Protein shakes are convenient, but they're supplements, not replacements. Get the majority of your
protein from whole food sources: meat, fish, eggs, dairy. These come packaged with other nutrients
(B vitamins, zinc, iron, omega-3s) that isolated protein powders don't provide.

Use shakes when you genuinely can't hit your target through food, or for convenience around
training. Don't use them as a crutch for lazy eating.

And if you ever have protein shakes, stay away from the flavoured ones and try to find an
unflavoured option with minimal additives.

Eating Structure — When and How Often
NUTRITION FRAMEWORK (FOUNDATIONS)
Meal timing is less important than the internet wants you to believe. But structure still matters.

You don't need to eat every three hours to "stoke your metabolism." That's a myth. You also don't
need to fast for 16 hours every day. For most men focused on building or maintaining muscle,
extreme fasting protocols create more problems than they solve.

What actually matters is having a consistent eating structure that ensures you hit your protein target
and supports your training.

The Apollo Default: 3 Main Meals + Optional Snack

For most men, three solid meals per day with an optional snack or post-workout protein is the sweet
spot. This provides:

  • Enough eating occasions to spread protein intake effectively (30-50g per meal)

  • Sufficient time between meals to experience actual hunger (important for appetite regulation)

  • Flexibility for real-world schedules

  • No requirement for constant meal prep or eating every few hours

Meal Timing Around Training:

Pre-Training: Eat a balanced meal 2-3 hours before training, or a lighter snack 60-90 minutes before.
You want fuel in your system, but not a full stomach. Some men train well fasted; most perform
better with food.

Post-Training: The "anabolic window" is not 30 minutes. It's several hours. That said, eating a
protein-rich meal within 1-2 hours after training is a good practice. Your muscles are primed for
nutrient uptake, and you're likely hungry anyway.

Don't overthink this. If you're eating three solid meals and training falls somewhere between those
meals, you're fine.

Breakfast Matters:

Skip the cereal. Skip the toast and jam. Skip the sugar-laden granola marketed as health food.

Breakfast sets the tone for your day. A protein-rich breakfast stabilises blood sugar, reduces
cravings, and helps you hit your protein target. A carb-heavy, protein-poor breakfast spikes blood
sugar, crashes it by mid-morning, and leaves you hungry and reaching for snacks.

Good breakfast options:

  • Eggs (any style) with vegetables and avocado and cottage cheese

  • Greek yogurt with berries and nuts

  • Eggs with smoked salmon

  • Leftovers from dinner (there's no rule that breakfast has to be "breakfast food")

  • Protein smoothie with Greek yogurt, protein powder (cleanest option you can find, keep that in
   mind), fruit, and nut butter (if you're in a rush)

Evening Eating:

Eating carbs at night won't make you fat. Eating too many total calories makes you fat. If your total
intake is appropriate, when you eat, those calories matter very little.

That said, a lighter evening meal (more protein and vegetables, moderate carbs) may improve sleep
quality for some men. Experiment and see what works for you.

Consistency Over Perfection:

The best meal timing is the one you'll actually follow. If you hate breakfast, don't force it, just make
sure your first meal is protein-rich. If you prefer two larger meals over three moderate ones, that can
work. The principles matter more than the specific schedule.

Food Quality — What to Eat and What to Avoid
NUTRITION FRAMEWORK (FOUNDATIONS)
Not all calories are created equal. The source of your food matters as much as the quantity.

The Apollo approach is simple: eat foods that existed 100 years ago. If your great-great-grandfather
wouldn't recognise it as food, be skeptical.

BUILD YOUR DIET AROUND THESE:

Protein Sources:

  • Beef (all cuts — don't fear red meat)

  • Chicken and turkey

  • Lamb

  • Fish (salmon, cod, sardines, mackerel, tuna)

  • Shellfish (shrimp, mussels, oysters)

  • Eggs (whole eggs, not just whites)

  • Greek yogurt and cottage cheese and feta cheese

  • Quality cheese

Vegetables:

  • Leafy greens (spinach, kale, arugula, lettuce)

  • Cruciferous (broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, cabbage)

  • Alliums (onions, garlic, leeks)

  • Nightshades (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant)

  • Root vegetables (carrots, beets, turnips)

  • Others (zucchini, mushrooms, asparagus, green beans)

Fruits:

  • Berries (blueberries, strawberries, raspberries, blackberries)

  • Citrus (oranges, lemons, limes, grapefruit)

  • Apples, pears, bananas

  • Stone fruits (peaches, plums, cherries)

  • Tropical fruits in moderation (mango, pineapple)

Quality Carbohydrates:

  • Rice (white or brown)

  • Potatoes (all varieties)

  • Sweet potatoes

  • Oats

  • Quinoa

  • Whole grain bread/Sourdough (actual whole grain, not processed "wheat" bread)

  • Legumes (beans, lentils) if tolerated

Quality Fats:

  • Extra virgin olive oil

  • Butter and ghee

  • Avocado and avocado oil

  • Coconut oil

  • Nuts (almonds, walnuts, macadamias, cashews)

  • Seeds (chia, flax, pumpkin)

  • Fatty fish

MINIMIZE OR ELIMINATE:

Ultra-Processed Foods: Anything with a long ingredient list of things you don't recognise. Chips,
crackers, cookies, packaged snacks, frozen meals, fast food. These foods are engineered to
override your satiety signals, designed in labs to hit the "bliss point" that makes you keep eating
past fullness.

Industrial Seed Oils: Canola oil, soybean oil, corn oil, vegetable oil, sunflower oil, safflower oil.
These are in almost every processed food and restaurant meal. They're inflammatory, heavily
processed, and contribute to an unhealthy omega-6 to omega-3 ratio. Cook with olive oil, butter,
ghee, or coconut oil instead.

Added Sugars: Sugar in whole fruit is fine, it comes with fibre and nutrients. Added sugar in
processed foods, sodas, juices, and sweets is the problem. You don't need to eliminate it entirely,
but it shouldn't be a daily staple.

Artificial Ingredients: Artificial sweeteners, colours, flavours, and preservatives. If the ingredient list
reads like a chemistry experiment, put it back.

The 90/10 Principle:

Eat according to these guidelines 90% of the time. The other 10% is for life - birthday cake, pizza
with friends, a beer on the weekend.

Rigid perfection isn't the goal.

Consistent quality is.

Building Your Kitchen
(FOUNDATIONS)
You can't eat well if your kitchen is stocked poorly. What you buy determines what you eat.

This lesson gives you practical shopping lists at three budget levels. Use them as templates and
adjust based on your preferences.

THE PRINCIPLES:

Shop the perimeter. In most grocery stores, the whole foods are on the outside edges - produce,
meat, dairy. The processed stuff is in the centre aisles. Spend most of your time on the perimeter.

Read ingredients, not marketing. "Natural," "healthy," and "whole grain" on the front of a package
mean nothing. Flip it over. Read the actual ingredients. If there are more than 5-7 ingredients or
things you don't recognise, reconsider.

Buy what you'll actually eat. The healthiest food in the world is useless if it rots in your fridge. Be
realistic about your cooking habits and preferences.

KITCHEN AUDIT
(FOUNDATIONS)
Before your next shopping trip, clean out your kitchen.

Remove:

  • Chips, crackers, and packaged snacks

  • Cookies, candy, and sweets

  • Sodas, juices, and sweetened drinks

  • Anything with seed oils as a main ingredient

  • Frozen meals and processed convenience foods

If it's not in your house, you won't eat it. Make the healthy choice the easy choice.

Eating Out and Travel
(FOUNDATIONS)
Real life doesn't happen in a controlled kitchen. You'll eat at restaurants. You'll travel. You'll attend
social events. The Apollo approach works everywhere, you just need to know how to adapt.

RESTAURANT RULES:

Lead with protein. Look at the menu and identify the best protein option first. Grilled steak, roasted
chicken, fish, seafood. Build the meal around that.

Request substitutions. Most restaurants will swap fries for vegetables or salad. Ask. The worst
they can say is no.

Watch the hidden stuff. Restaurant food often contains more oil, butter, and salt than you'd use at
home. This is fine occasionally, but be aware. Sauces and dressings can add significant hidden
calories - ask for them on the side.

Skip the bread basket. Don't fill up on empty carbs before your meal even arrives.

One indulgence per meal. Want the appetiser? Skip dessert. Want the dessert? Skip the appetiser.
Don't let one restaurant meal become a complete free-for-all.

BY CUISINE:

Steakhouse: Easy mode. Steak or fish with vegetables. Skip the loaded baked potato and creamed
spinach.

Italian: Grilled protein with vegetables. If you want pasta, get it as a side portion, not the main
event.

Mexican: Fajitas without the tortillas, burrito bowls, grilled meats with salsa and guacamole. Skip
the chips and cheese-heavy dishes.

Asian: Stir-fries with protein and vegetables, steamed dishes, sashimi. Watch for sugary sauces.
Rice on the side.

TRAVEL:

Plan ahead. Research restaurants near your hotel. Identify grocery stores. Know your options
before you're hungry and desperate.

Hotel breakfast buffets: Eggs, meat, yogurt, fruit. Ignore the pastries, pancakes, and cereal.

Airports: Most airports now have decent options if you look. Grilled chicken, salads, nuts. Pack
protein bars or jerky as backup. But sometimes they don't, I always make sure to pack my own.

Stay hydrated: Travel dehydrates you. Drink water throughout, especially on flights.

SOCIAL EVENTS:

Don't be the guy who can't eat anything or makes a big deal about his diet. That's not the Apollo
way.

At parties and gatherings:

  • Eat a protein-rich meal before so you're not starving

  • Focus on the protein options available (meat, cheese, shrimp)

  • Fill a plate once rather than grazing all night

  • Enjoy yourself - one evening won't derail you

The goal is to make good choices easy and automatic, not to be inflexible or antisocial.

Supplements — What's Worth It
FRAMEWORK (FOUNDATIONS)
Supplements are exactly what the name implies - supplemental. They fill gaps. They don't replace a
solid diet, and they won't fix a broken one.

The fitness industry makes billions selling supplements with exaggerated claims and minimal
evidence. Most of it is marketing, not science. Here's what actually works, what might help, and
what's a waste of money.

TIER 1 — WORTH IT FOR MOST MEN:

Creatine Monohydrate

The most researched supplement in existence. Proven to increase strength, power output, and
muscle mass. Also shows cognitive benefits. Safe for long-term use.

Dose: 5g daily. No need to load. No need to cycle. Just take it consistently.

Form: Plain creatine monohydrate. No need for fancy versions, they're just more expensive with no
added benefit.

Vitamin D3

Most men are deficient, especially if you live in northern latitudes or spend most of your time
indoors. Vitamin D affects testosterone, immune function, mood, and bone health.

Dose: 2,000-5,000 IU daily, depending on your levels. Ideally, get tested and dose accordingly.

Take with fat for absorption.

Omega-3 Fish Oil

Supports heart health, brain function, and reduces inflammation. Most men don't eat enough fatty
fish to get adequate omega-3s.

Dose: 2-3g of combined EPA and DHA daily. Check the label - total fish oil content is different from
actual EPA/DHA content.

Quality matters: Choose a reputable brand that tests for purity and oxidation.

Magnesium

Involved in hundreds of enzymatic reactions. Supports sleep, recovery, and muscle function. Many
men are deficient due to soil depletion and low vegetable intake.

Dose: 200-400mg daily. Magnesium glycinate or bisglycinate are well-absorbed forms. Take in the
evening - it can support sleep.

Zinc

Zinc is critical for testosterone production - deficiency is directly linked to lower testosterone levels.
It also supports immune function, protein synthesis, wound healing, and DNA repair.

Dose: 15-30mg daily.

Forms: Zinc picolinate and zinc glycinate are well-absorbed. Zinc oxide (common in cheap
supplements) is poorly absorbed — avoid it.

Timing: Take with food to avoid nausea. If you're also supplementing magnesium, take them at
different times as they can compete for absorption.

TIER 2 — SITUATIONALLY USEFUL:

Protein Powder (Whey or Casein)

Not magic. Just convenient protein. Use when you can't hit your protein target through whole foods,
or for convenience around training.

Whey: Fast-digesting, good post-workout or when you need quick protein. Casein: Slow-digesting,
good before bed or when you want sustained release.

Choose quality brands with minimal added ingredients.

Caffeine

Proven performance enhancer. Improves focus, strength, and endurance. You probably already
consume it.

Dose: 3-6mg per kilogram bodyweight for performance benefits. For an 80kg man, that's
240-480mg — roughly 2-4 cups of coffee.

Don't take it within 8-10 hours of sleep. My personal cut off is at midday.

Electrolytes

If you train hard, sweat a lot, or eat low-carb, you may need additional sodium, potassium, and
magnesium. Especially important in hot climates or during longer training sessions.

TIER 3 — PROBABLY NOT WORTH IT:

BCAAs: If you're eating adequate protein, BCAAs are redundant. Your whole food protein already
contains them.

Testosterone Boosters: Almost universally ineffective. Save your money.

Fat Burners: Mostly caffeine with other stimulants. Potentially dangerous, minimally effective.

Most Pre-Workouts: Overpriced caffeine with proprietary blends. If you want a pre-workout boost,
drink coffee, have a tablespoon of honey and a date or two.

THE BOTTOM LINE:

Get your diet right first.

Body Composition — Losing Fat, Building Muscle
NUTRITION FRAMEWORK (FOUNDATIONS)
Understanding how body composition actually works gives you control over your results.

THE FUNDAMENTAL EQUATION:

Body composition changes based on energy balance and how you partition that energy.

Fat Loss: Requires a caloric deficit - consuming fewer calories than you burn. There's no way
around this. Every diet that works for fat loss works because it creates a deficit, whether it
acknowledges it or not.

Muscle Gain: Requires adequate protein, progressive resistance training, and often a slight caloric
surplus (especially for beginners or those focused primarily on gaining size).

Body Recomposition: Losing fat while building muscle simultaneously. Very possible, especially for
beginners, those returning to training, and those with higher body fat. Requires eating at
maintenance or a slight deficit while training hard and prioritising protein.

PRACTICAL FAT LOSS:

If fat loss is your goal:

 1. Set protein high — Protein preserves muscle during a deficit.

 2. Create a moderate deficit — 300 calories below maintenance. Aggressive deficits lead to
    muscle loss, metabolic adaptation, and rebound. Slow and steady wins.

 3. Train hard — Resistance training is non-negotiable during fat loss. It's the signal that tells your
    body to keep muscle.

 4. Be patient — Aim for 0.5-1% of bodyweight lost per week. For an 80kg man, that's 0.4-0.8kg
    weekly. Faster than that and you're likely losing muscle.

 5. Track progress properly — Weigh daily, take the weekly average. Weight fluctuates daily due to
    water, food volume, and other factors. The trend matters, not any single data point.

PRACTICAL MUSCLE GAIN:

If building muscle is your goal:

 1. Eat enough — You likely need to be at maintenance calories or slightly above. Most men who
   "can't gain muscle" are simply not eating enough.

 2. Protein remains king

 3. Progressive overload — You must get stronger over time. If you're not progressing in the gym,
   you're not optimising muscle growth.

WHAT THE SCALE DOESN'T TELL YOU:

Scale weight is one data point. It doesn't differentiate between muscle, fat, water, and food in your
digestive system.

Better indicators of progress:

  • How clothes fit

  • Progress photos (same lighting, same time of day)

  • Strength progression in the gym

  • Waist measurement

  • Energy levels

A man who gains 2kg but drops a belt notch is making excellent progress, regardless of what the
scale says.

THE APOLLO APPROACH:

For most men in Phase 1, the goal is body recomposition - lose some fat, build some muscle,
improve overall composition. This happens naturally when you:

  • Train consistently with progressive overload

  • Eat adequate protein

  • Focus on whole foods

  • Sleep properly

  • Let the process work

Don't obsess over bulk/cut cycles until you've built a foundation.

Just execute the basics and let your body adapt.

Nutrition Summary and Implementation
NUTRITION FRAMEWORK (FOUNDATIONS)
Here's everything you need to execute Apollo Nutrition in Phase 1:

THE NON-NEGOTIABLES:

 1. Hit your protein floor daily

 2. Use the Apollo Plate Method — Every main meal has quality protein, substantial vegetables,
   appropriate carbs, and quality fats

 3. Eliminate ultra-processed foods — No chips, cookies, crackers, sugary snacks as staples

 4. Eliminate liquid calories — No sodas, juices, sweetened drinks. Water, black coffee, and tea
   are your defaults

 5. Cook with quality fats — Olive oil, butter, ghee. No seed oils

 6. Eat enough vegetables — Half your plate at main meals

THE STRUCTURE:

  • 3 main meals per day, each with 30-50g protein

  • Optional snack or post-workout protein if needed

  • Breakfast is protein-focused, not carb-focused

  • Pre and post-training nutrition doesn't need to be complicated — eat a meal 2-3 hours before,
   eat within 1-2 hours after

THE MINDSET:

  • Quality over quantity

  • Consistency over perfection

  • Intuition over obsession

  • Food is fuel and pleasure, not a math problem

ACTION ITEMS FOR THIS WEEK:

 1. Audit your kitchen — Remove the processed junk. Stock quality proteins, vegetables, and
   whole food carbs.

 2. Track protein for 5 days — Just protein, not everything. Build awareness of where you actually
   are versus where you need to be.

 3. Practice the Plate Method — At every main meal, visualise the framework. Protein anchor,
   vegetables filling space, quality carbs, cooked in good fat.

 4. Drink more water — 2- 3 litres daily. More if you train hard or live in a hot climate.

You don't need to be perfect. You need to be consistent. Eighty percent adherence to these
principles will produce better results than 100% adherence to a complicated system you can't
maintain.

Eat like someone who respects his body.

The rest takes care of itself.
