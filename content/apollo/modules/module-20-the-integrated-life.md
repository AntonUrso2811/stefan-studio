## Intro
You have spent the last ninety-plus days building a body, optimising your health, and developing
habits that most men will never adopt. The question now is: how do you sustain this?

Not for another 30 days. For the rest of your life.

This module is about designing a life that makes excellence automatic. Environment, systems,
energy management, and the ability to maintain your protocols anywhere in the world. This is where
the programme stops being a programme and becomes how you live.

## Environment Design
> You are a product of your environment more than you are a product of your willpower.

The Apollo Environment Audit:

Walk through your living space with fresh eyes. Every element should either support your goals or
be neutral. Anything that actively works against your goals needs to go.

Kitchen:

  • Protein sources visible and accessible (front of fridge, eye level in pantry)

  • Ultra-processed foods removed entirely - if it is in the house, you will eat it eventually

  • Quality cooking equipment that makes meal preparation easy (cast iron pan, decent knife,
   cutting board, rice cooker)

  • Supplements organised and visible (you take what you see)

  • Water bottle on the counter, filled

Bedroom:

  • Blackout curtains or a quality sleep mask

  • No television in the bedroom

  • Phone charging station outside the bedroom (or at minimum, across the room)

  • Cool temperature capability (fan, AC, or cooling mattress pad)

  • Quality bedding - you spend a third of your life in bed, invest accordingly

Workspace:

  • Standing desk or adjustable desk (alternate sitting and standing)

  • Monitor at eye level

  • Quality chair with lumbar support if sitting

  • Light therapy lamp if natural light is insufficient

  • Water and healthy snacks within reach

  • Phone in a drawer or face-down during deep work blocks

Training Space:

  • Gym bag packed and ready at all times

  • Training programme printed or saved on phone - you never walk in without a plan

  • If home gym: equipment accessible, not buried in a garage corner

The Friction Principle:

Make good behaviour easy and bad behaviour hard. Every action has a friction level, the number of
steps between you and doing the thing. Reduce friction for what you want to do. Increase friction for
what you want to avoid.

Examples:

  • Sleep in your training clothes → lower friction to morning training

  • Remove social media apps from home screen → higher friction for mindless scrolling

  • Meal prep Sunday → lower friction for eating well during the week

  • Leave the TV remote in a drawer → higher friction for defaulting to screens in the evening

  • Put the book you are reading on your pillow → lower friction for reading before sleep

## Systems Over Goals
Goals tell you where you want to go. Systems get you there. And once you arrive, systems keep you
there while goals leave you directionless.

The man who says "I want to lose 10kg" has a goal. The man who has a system for training 4x per
week, eating adequate protein, and sleeping 7-9 hours will lose the weight and keep it off. The goal-
oriented man hits his target and often regresses because the motivation was external. The systems-
oriented man does not regress because the system is his life.

Building Your Operating System:

Morning Protocol — The first 60-90 minutes of your day should be systematised. Same wake time,
same sequence, every day. This eliminates decision fatigue and ensures the non-negotiables
happen regardless of how you feel.

Example Apollo Morning Protocol:

 1. Wake at [set time] — no snooze

 2. Hydrate — 500ml water

 3. 10 minutes morning light exposure (combine with step 4)

 4. Movement flow from Phase 3 Module 1 (15 minutes)

 5. Breathing practice (5 minutes)

 6. Cold shower (optional — 30-60 seconds)

 7. First meal or coffee (60-90 minutes after waking for caffeine)

Total time: 30-40 minutes. This is an investment, not a cost.

Evening Protocol — The last 60-90 minutes of your day should also be systematised. This protects
sleep quality and creates a clear transition from active day to recovery.

Example Apollo Evening Protocol:

 1. Last meal finished 2-3 hours before bed

 2. Screens off / blue light blocking 60-90 minutes before bed

 3. Dim lighting throughout the house

 4. Skincare routine (3 minutes)

 5. Reading, journalling, or light conversation

 6. Mouth tape applied

 7. Lights off at [set time]

Weekly Rhythms:

Your week should have a predictable structure:

  • Training days scheduled and immovable

  • Meal prep time blocked (Sunday afternoon for many)

  • One weekly review (15-30 minutes — see Lesson 3)

  • Social commitments planned, not reactive

  • Recovery time protected

> The Power of Defaults:

A default is what happens when you do not make a conscious decision. Most men's defaults are
destructive, default to processed food when busy, default to screens when bored, default to alcohol
when socialising, default to late nights when stressed.

Rewire your defaults: default meal when you have not planned is [insert your go-to healthy meal].
Default activity when bored is reading or walking. Default evening activity is something restorative,
not stimulating.

## Energy and Time Management
> You have two finite resources: energy and time. Every person, system, and commitment in your
life either adds to these or drains them. Most men never audit this and wonder why they feel
perpetually depleted.

The Energy Audit:

For one week, track how you feel after every major activity, interaction, and commitment. Rate it:
energising (+), neutral (0), or draining (-).

Common energy drains that men tolerate: toxic relationships, excessive news consumption, social
media scrolling, unnecessary meetings, obligations maintained out of guilt, alcohol, poor-quality
food, and insufficient recovery.

Common energy sources that men neglect: time in nature, quality sleep, meaningful connection with
people who elevate them, creative work, physical training, and solitude.

Once you have the data, restructure. Reduce or eliminate the drains. Increase and protect the
sources.

Time Blocking:

Your calendar should reflect your priorities. If health is a priority but training is not on your calendar,
it is not actually a priority, it is a wish.

Block the following as immovable appointments:

  • Training sessions

  • Sleep (yes, schedule your bedtime)

  • Meal preparation

  • Deep work blocks (most demanding cognitive work)

  • Recovery time

Everything else works around these. Not the other way around.

The Weekly Review (15-30 minutes):

Every Sunday evening, review the week:

 1. Did I hit all training sessions?

 2. Did I hit protein targets consistently?

 3. How was my sleep quality and consistency?

 4. What went well this week?

 5. What needs adjustment?

 6. What are my priorities for the coming week?

 7. Is my calendar aligned with my values?

> This practice takes minutes but prevents weeks of drift. The men who review consistently rarely
go off track for more than a few days. The men who do not review can lose months before realising
they have slipped.

## Travel and Lifestyle Maintenance
Travel is where most men abandon everything they have built. The routine breaks, the food changes,
the sleep shifts, and within a week they have lost momentum that takes another week to rebuild.

This is unacceptable for an Apollo man. The principles do not change because the location does.

Training While Travelling:

  • Research gym access before you arrive. Most hotels have basic facilities. Most cities have day-
   pass gyms. If neither is available, bodyweight training from the calisthenics progressions in
   Phase 3 Module 3 requires nothing but floor space.

  • Pack a resistance band — this single piece of equipment adds dozens of exercises to a hotel
   room workout.

  • Minimum viable training while travelling: 20-30 minutes of bodyweight compounds (push-ups,
   squats, lunges, rows using a table edge, planks). This maintains stimulus. You are not trying to
   make progress while travelling — you are preventing regression.

Nutrition While Travelling:

  • Protein remains the priority. In any restaurant, in any country, you can order protein and
   vegetables.

  • Carry portable protein: beef jerky, protein powder (in a zip-lock bag — do not carry the whole
   tub), nuts.

  • Intermittent fasting is a powerful travel tool — skip breakfast, eat a solid lunch and dinner with
   adequate protein. This simplifies logistics significantly.

  • Hydrate aggressively. Travel dehydrates. Carry a water bottle and refill constantly.

  • One indulgent meal per day is acceptable — experience local food. But make it one, not three.

Sleep While Travelling:

  • Bring an eye mask and earplugs. Non-negotiable travel items.

  • Melatonin (0.5-1mg) at the new bedtime for 3-5 nights to reset circadian rhythm after time zone
   changes.

  • Morning light exposure at the destination immediately upon arrival.

  • Maintain your evening routine as closely as possible — skincare, reading, no screens before bed.

Jet Lag Protocol:

  • Begin shifting sleep timing 2-3 days before departure (30-60 minutes per day toward new time
   zone).

  • On the flight: set your watch to the destination time. Sleep if it is night there, stay awake if it is
   day.

  • Upon arrival: bright light in the morning, avoid sunglasses. Meals at local times. Train at your
   normal local time if possible.

  • Short-term melatonin at the new bedtime.

  • Accept 2-3 days of adjustment for every 3 time zones crossed.

The Travel Mindset:

Your standards do not take holidays. You can enjoy travel, food, experiences, rest without
abandoning everything you have built. The man who maintains 80% adherence while travelling
returns home with momentum. The man who abandons everything returns to square one.

## The Apollo Lifestyle
> This is not a programme you finish. It is an operating system you run for the rest of your life.

Every module in every phase connects. Training supports hormonal health. Nutrition supports gut
health. Gut health supports brain function and recovery. Sleep supports everything. Longevity
protocols protect what you have built.

Remove any one pillar and the others weaken. Maintain all of them and the compounding effect over
years and decades produces a man who is physically, mentally, and aesthetically decades ahead of
his peers.

The Apollo Standards:

  • Train 3-5 times per week. Every week. For life.

  • Hit your protein target daily. No exceptions.

  • Sleep 7-9 hours. Consistent timing. Protected like a meeting with the most important person in
   your life because it is.

  • Eat whole foods. Minimal processing. Adequate fibre. Fermented foods frequently.

  • Manage stress actively. Breathe. Move. Recover.

  • Protect your skin. Sunscreen, retinoid, consistency.

  • Monitor your health. Annual blood work. Track what matters.

  • Design your environment. Make good behaviour automatic.

  • Review weekly. Adjust. Improve. Do not drift.

The Compound Effect:

In one year: you will look and feel better than 90% of men your age. In three years: the gap will be
visible to everyone around you. In ten years: you will be a different category of human entirely -
stronger, leaner, sharper, healthier, and more capable than men a decade younger who did not do
this work.

This is not exaggeration. This is mathematics. Small, consistent inputs compounded over time
produce extraordinary outputs. Einstein called compounding the eighth wonder of the world. He
was not talking about money.
