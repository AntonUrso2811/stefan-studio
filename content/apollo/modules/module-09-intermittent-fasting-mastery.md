## Frameworks / protocols

**The Metabolic States**
- Fed state — 3–5h after a meal; insulin high, cells absorbing
- Post-absorptive state — insulin drops, glucagon rises, liver releases glycogen
- Fasted state — begins 8–12h after last meal; insulin low, fat oxidation rises, GH begins climbing

**The Five Effects of Extended Fasting (12–24h)**
1. Increased fat oxidation (research shows significant rise past 12–14h)
2. Autophagy activation (markers rise around 16–18h; more significant at 24h+)
3. Growth hormone increase (up to 5-fold during a 24-hour fast; muscle-sparing, not muscle-building)
4. Improved insulin sensitivity
5. Norepinephrine + mental clarity (neurochemical, not placebo)

**The Four Honest Mechanisms Behind IF's Effectiveness**
1. Simplifies eating decisions (fewer meals = fewer poor-choice moments)
2. Often naturally reduces caloric intake (skipping breakfast removes 400–600 cal for most)
3. Creates clear structure (eating windows prevent grazing)
4. Leverages fat-oxidation metabolic states

**What Fasting Does NOT Do**
- Boost metabolism (short-term effect is minimal; prolonged fasting actually reduces it)
- Magically outperform caloric restriction (equal calories + protein = equal fat loss)
- Detox anything (liver + kidneys handle that 24/7)
- Become necessary for fat loss

## Full body

The Science of Fasting

Introduction
You have heard about intermittent fasting. You have probably seen the claims - burn fat faster, live
longer, boost testosterone, increase mental clarity. Some of it is true. Some of it is overhyped. Most
of it is presented without the nuance you need to make it actually work for you.

We held this back from Phase 1 for a reason. Intermittent fasting is a tool, not a foundation. Trying to
implement IF before you have locked in protein targets, meal quality, and consistent eating patterns
sets you up for failure. You end up undereating protein, feeling like garbage, and blaming the
protocol when the real issue was attempting optimisation before fundamentals.

You have now spent 30 days building those fundamentals. You know how to hit your protein. You
understand meal composition. You have a rhythm with food that works.

> Now you are ready to add IF as a strategic tool - understanding exactly what it does, who it works
for, and how to implement it without sacrificing the muscle you are building.

The Fed-to-Fasted Transition
> Before you implement any protocol, you need to understand what is actually happening in your
body when you fast. Not the Instagram version. The real physiology.

When you eat, your body enters the "fed state." Blood glucose rises, insulin is released, and your
cells take up nutrients for immediate use or storage. This fed state lasts roughly 3-5 hours after a
meal, depending on meal size and composition.

After the fed state, you enter the "post-absorptive state." Your body begins shifting from using
incoming nutrients to mobilizing stored energy. Insulin levels drop. Glucagon rises. Your liver begins
releasing stored glycogen to maintain blood glucose.

The "fasted state" typically begins 8-12 hours after your last meal. By this point:

  • Insulin levels are low and stable

  • Liver glycogen is partially depleted

  • Fat oxidation increases as the body shifts fuel sources

  • Growth hormone begins to rise

This is the metabolic state that fasting protocols aim to extend and leverage.

What Happens During Extended Fasting (12-24 hours) -
Increased Fat Oxidation

As glycogen depletes and insulin remains low, your body up-regulates fat burning. Hormone-
sensitive lipase becomes more active, releasing fatty acids from adipose tissue. These fatty acids
are transported to cells and oxidised for energy.

Research shows that fat oxidation rates increase significantly after 12-14 hours of fasting. This is the
primary mechanism behind fasting's fat loss benefits - you spend more time in a fat-burning
metabolic state.

However, increased fat oxidation does not automatically mean fat loss. Total energy balance still
determines whether you lose fat. You can fast for 16 hours, then overeat in your feeding window and
gain fat. The laws of thermodynamics are not suspended by meal timing.

Autophagy Activation

Autophagy - literally "self-eating" - is a cellular recycling process where damaged proteins,
organelles, and cellular debris are broken down and recycled. Think of it as your cells taking out the
trash and recycling usable materials.

Autophagy occurs at baseline levels constantly, but it is up-regulated during fasting when nutrients
are scarce. The body becomes more efficient at recycling existing cellular components rather than
relying on incoming nutrients.

Research in humans suggests autophagy markers begin increasing around 16-18 hours of fasting,
with more significant activation at 24+ hours. The exact timing varies by individual and is difficult to
measure directly in living humans.

The longevity community is excited about autophagy because it may help clear dysfunctional
proteins associated with aging and disease. However, most autophagy research is in animals or cell
cultures. The human data is promising but not definitive. Do not fast expecting autophagy to make
you immortal.

Growth Hormone Increase

Fasting significantly increases growth hormone secretion. Studies show GH can increase 5-fold
during a 24-hour fast. This is a protective mechanism - GH helps preserve muscle mass while the
body mobilises fat for fuel.

This GH increase is one reason why short-term fasting does not cause the muscle loss many fear.
Your body is not stupid. It has evolved mechanisms to protect lean tissue during brief periods
without food.

However, this does not mean fasting is anabolic. The GH increase helps preserve muscle, but you
still need adequate protein and training stimulus to build muscle. Fasting is muscle-sparing, not
muscle-building.

Improved Insulin Sensitivity

Extended periods without food allow insulin levels to remain low, which improves cellular sensitivity
to insulin over time. When you do eat, your cells respond more efficiently to insulin signalling.

This is particularly beneficial for metabolic health. Improved insulin sensitivity means better blood
glucose control, more efficient nutrient partitioning, and reduced risk of type 2 diabetes.

Research shows that intermittent fasting can improve insulin sensitivity independent of weight loss,
though the effects are amplified when combined with fat loss.

Norepinephrine and Mental Clarity

Fasting increases norepinephrine (noradrenaline) release. This is an evolutionary adaptation - when
food is scarce, you need to be alert and focused to find it.

Many people report improved mental clarity and focus during fasted states. This is not placebo - it is
a real neurochemical response. The combination of stable blood glucose (no post-meal crashes),
elevated norepinephrine, and ketone production (which the brain can use for fuel) creates a state
many find optimal for cognitive work.

What Fasting Does NOT Do
It does not "boost metabolism." Short-term fasting has minimal effect on metabolic rate. Very
prolonged fasting (multiple days) can actually reduce metabolic rate. The "stoke the metabolic fire
with frequent meals" myth has been thoroughly debunked, but so has the idea that fasting speeds
up metabolism.

It does not magically burn more fat than caloric restriction. When calories and protein are
equated, intermittent fasting produces similar fat loss to continuous caloric restriction. IF is a tool
that makes caloric restriction easier for some people - it is not metabolically superior.

It does not "detox" anything. Your liver and kidneys handle detoxification 24/7 regardless of meal
timing. "Detox" is marketing, not physiology.

It is not necessary for fat loss. Plenty of people get extremely lean eating 5-6 meals per day. IF is
one approach, not the only approach.

The Honest Assessment

Intermittent fasting works primarily because:

 1. It simplifies eating decisions - fewer meals means fewer opportunities to make poor choices

 2. It often naturally reduces caloric intake - skipping breakfast removes 400-600 calories for most
   people

 3. It creates clear structure - eating windows provide boundaries that prevent grazing

 4. It leverages metabolic states that favour fat oxidation

The metabolic benefits (autophagy, GH, insulin sensitivity) are real but modest for most healthy
people. The practical benefits - simplicity, time savings, mental clarity - are often more impactful
day-to-day.

> IF is not magic. It is a useful tool that works well for certain people in certain situations.
Understanding this prevents both dismissing it entirely and expecting miracles.

Intro
> There is no single "intermittent fasting" protocol. Multiple approaches exist, each with different
tradeoffs. The right choice depends on your lifestyle, goals, and how your body responds.

16:8 Protocol (Leangains)
The most popular and sustainable approach. Fast for 16 hours, eat within an 8-hour window.

Typical implementation:

  • Stop eating at 8pm

  • Skip breakfast

  • First meal at 12pm (noon)

  • Last meal by 8pm

What you get:

  • 16 hours in fasted/fat-oxidizing state

  • Mild autophagy activation

  • Sustainable for most lifestyles

  • Easy to maintain socially (lunch and dinner are normal)

Who it works for:

  • Most people as a starting point

  • Those who are not hungry in the morning anyway

  • Busy professionals who want simplified mornings

  • Anyone seeking sustainable fat loss

Who should avoid it:

  • Hard gainers struggling to eat enough calories

      • Those with very high caloric needs (3500+ calories)

      • Anyone with history of disordered eating

      • Those who train early morning and cannot adjust

I personally use this protocol.

18:6 Protocol
A tighter window. Fast for 18 hours, eat within 6 hours.

Typical implementation:

      • Stop eating at 8pm

      • First meal at 2pm

      • Last meal by 8pm (two meals, possibly a snack)

What you get:

      • Extended fasting benefits

      • Stronger autophagy signal

      • Greater fat oxidation window

      • Often natural caloric reduction

Who it works for:

      • Those who have adapted to 16:8 and want more

      • Fat loss phases where aggressive deficit is needed

      • People who genuinely do well on two meals

      • Those with sedentary jobs and lower caloric needs

Who should avoid it:

      • Anyone struggling to hit protein targets

      • High-volume trainers

      • Those who feel significant energy crashes

> Before moving onto a more challenging fast, always acclimate your body and work your way up to
it.

20:4 Protocol (Warrior Diet)
Fast for 20 hours, eat within a 4-hour window. Essentially one large meal with possible small
additions.

Typical implementation:

  • One main meal in the evening

  • Possible small snack or appetizer before main meal

  • All calories consumed within 4-hour window

What you get:

  • Maximum fasting benefits

  • Strong autophagy activation

  • Deep fat oxidation

  • Very simplified eating

Who it works for:

  • Experienced fasters who have adapted progressively

  • Those who genuinely prefer one large meal

  • Short-term fat loss phases (not sustainable long-term for most)

  • People with specific schedules that make this practical

Who should avoid it:

  • Most people, honestly

  • Anyone prioritizing muscle gain

  • Those who train intensely

  • Anyone with digestive issues (huge meals stress the gut)

5:2 Protocol
Not daily fasting, but weekly. Eat normally five days per week, severely restrict calories (500-600) on
two non-consecutive days.

What you get:

  • Flexibility on most days

  • Extended fasting benefits on fast days

  • Weekly caloric reduction without daily restriction

Who it works for:

  • Those who hate daily restriction

  • People with highly variable schedules

  • Those who prefer periodic challenge over daily discipline

Who should avoid it:

  • Anyone who struggles with all-or-nothing patterns

  • Those who tend to overcompensate on eating days

  • Most people focused on consistent training performance

   Implementing IF Without Losing Muscle

The Apollo Recommendation
For most men in this community, start with 16:8.

It provides meaningful fasting benefits while remaining sustainable and practical. It allows adequate
time to hit protein targets across 2-3 meals. It works with normal social schedules. It does not
require heroic willpower.

If 16:8 works well and you want to experiment, you can tighten to 18:6 during fat loss phases. But
16:8 is sufficient for most goals and most people.

Do not start with aggressive protocols.

How to Choose Your Protocol

Ask yourself:

 1. What is my primary goal right now? (Fat loss favours tighter windows; muscle gain favours
   longer feeding windows)

 2. When do I train? (You need fuel around training)

 3. What is my natural hunger pattern? (Work with your body, not against it)

 4. What is sustainable for my lifestyle? (Social obligations, family meals, work schedule)

> The best protocol is the one you will actually follow. Theoretical optimization means nothing if you
cannot execute it.

The Protein Priority
This is where most people screw up intermittent fasting. They focus on the fasting window and
forget that the feeding window determines results.

You can fast perfectly and still lose muscle if you do not eat correctly when you do eat. Protecting
muscle requires intentional nutrition within your feeding window.

Your protein target does not change because you are fasting. You still need 0.8-1g per pound of
bodyweight daily. The difference is you now have fewer meals to hit that target.

With a 16:8 protocol and two main meals plus a snack, you need roughly 50-70g of protein per meal
for most men. This requires planning.

Example for 80kg man (target: 145-160g protein):

  • Meal 1 (12pm): 50g protein

  • Snack (4pm): 25g protein

  • Meal 2 (7pm): 50g protein

  • Pre-bed (before 8pm): 25g protein

  • Total: 150g

> This is achievable but requires every meal to be protein-focused. You cannot waste meals on low-
protein foods and expect to hit your target.

Protein Distribution Within Feeding Window
Research suggests that distributing protein across multiple feedings optimizes muscle protein
synthesis better than consuming it all at once. The muscle-full effect means there is a ceiling to how
much protein can stimulate MPS in a single meal - roughly 40-60g for most people.

This means eating 150g of protein in one meal is less effective for muscle building than spreading it
across 3-4 feedings within your window.

Practical application:

  • Aim for 3-4 protein feedings within your eating window

  • Each feeding should contain 30-50g protein

  • Do not rely on one massive meal to hit your daily target

Pre and Post-Training Nutrition
Training in a fasted state is possible (covered in Lesson 4), but you should not remain fasted long
after training. The post-workout period is when your muscles are primed for nutrient uptake.

> If you train fasted in the morning and your feeding window does not start until noon, you are
missing the optimal window for post-workout nutrition. This is suboptimal for muscle growth.

Solutions:

 1. Shift your feeding window to start after training

 2. Train during your feeding window when possible

 3. Use a small protein feeding to break the fast post-training (even if you delay your main meal)

> The "anabolic window" is not as narrow as old-school bodybuilding claimed, but eating protein
within 2-3 hours of training is still advisable for maximising muscle protein synthesis.

Pre-Sleep Protein
If your feeding window ends at 8pm and you sleep at 11pm, you have 3 hours without protein
before an 8-hour overnight fast. That is 11 hours without amino acids available for muscle repair.

A slow-digesting protein source as your final feeding helps bridge this gap. Cottage cheese, or
Greek yogurt provide sustained amino acid release during sleep and are light for the gut.

This is not mandatory, but it is optimising.

> If your goal is maximum muscle retention during a fasted protocol, pre-sleep protein helps.

What to Eat When Breaking the Fast
Your first meal matters. After 16 hours without food, you want:

 1. Protein first - Start with your protein source. This ensures you prioritise what matters most.

 2. Moderate fat - Helps with satiety and slows digestion for sustained energy.

 3. Controlled carbohydrates - You do not need to avoid carbs, but a massive carb-heavy meal
   after fasting can spike blood glucose and cause an energy crash. Include carbs, but balance
   them with protein and fat.

 4. Fiber and vegetables - Support digestion and provide nutrients.

Example first meal:

  • 200g chicken breast or steak (45-50g protein) or 4 eggs with added egg white

  • Large serving of roasted vegetables

  • Half an avocado or olive oil dressing

  • Moderate portion of rice or potatoes

Avoid breaking your fast with:

  • Pure sugar or refined carbs (blood glucose spike, then crash)

  • Massive volume that overwhelms digestion

  • Low-protein meals that waste your limited feeding opportunities

Supplementation During Fasting
What breaks a fast? Technically, anything with calories. Practically, the question is whether the
calories are significant enough to disrupt the fasted state's benefits.

Will not meaningfully break your fast:

  • Black coffee (essentially zero calories)

  • Plain tea

  • Water (obviously)

  • Electrolytes without calories

  • Most supplements in capsule form

Will break your fast:

  • Protein shakes

  • BCAAs/EAAs (these are amino acids - they trigger an insulin response)

  • Anything with significant calories

  • Cream or sugar in coffee

  • Bulletproof coffee (high fat content)

Grey area:

  • Small amounts of cream in coffee (technically breaks fast, practically minimal impact)

  • Pre-workout supplements (depends on ingredients)

> The purist approach: nothing but water, black coffee, and plain tea during fasting window.

The practical approach: if a splash of cream in your coffee helps you sustain the fast and makes no
meaningful difference to your results, do not stress about it.

   Fasted Training

Signs You Are Losing Muscle
Monitor these indicators:

  • Strength decreasing in the gym over multiple weeks

  • Visible loss of muscle fullness beyond normal daily fluctuation

  • Rapid weight loss (more than 1% of bodyweight per week)

  • Excessive fatigue and poor recovery

> If these appear, you are likely undereating protein, undereating calories overall, or both. Adjust
before continuing.

What the Research Says
Can you train in a fasted state? Yes.

Should you? It depends.

Fasted training is not inherently better or worse than fed training. It is a tool with specific
applications, benefits, and drawbacks. Understanding these helps you decide if and how to
incorporate it.

Performance: Most studies show no significant difference in strength performance between fasted
and fed training, provided the fast is not excessively long (under 16-20 hours). Your body has
sufficient glycogen stores and can mobilise fatty acids for fuel.

However, some individuals do experience decreased performance when training fasted, particularly
for high-volume or high-intensity work. This is individual - you need to experiment.

Fat Oxidation: Training in a fasted state does increase fat oxidation during the workout. You burn a
higher percentage of calories from fat when glycogen is depleted and insulin is low.

But - and this is important - increased fat oxidation during exercise does not necessarily mean more
fat loss over 24 hours. Your body compensates. If you burn more fat during fasted training, you may
burn more carbohydrates later. Total daily energy balance still determines fat loss.

Muscle Protein Synthesis: Training in a fasted state does not negatively affect MPS, provided you
consume protein reasonably soon after training. The key is not being fasted during training - it is not
remaining fasted long after training.

Muscle Breakdown: There is some concern that fasted training increases muscle protein
breakdown. This is technically true but overblown. The increase is modest, and as long as you eat
adequate protein post-training, net protein balance is maintained.

Who Should Train Fasted
Fasted training works well for:

 1. Morning trainers who are not hungry - If you train at 6am and do not want to eat at 5am,
   fasted training is practical and fine.

 2. Those who feel sluggish after eating - Some people perform worse with food in their stomach.
   If you feel heavy and slow after eating, fasted training may suit you.

 3. Fat loss phases - The increased fat oxidation and lower insulin during fasted training can be a
   useful tool during cuts, though the effect is modest.

 4. Low-to-moderate intensity sessions - Zone 2 cardio and lighter training sessions are well-
   suited to fasted states.

Who Should Avoid Fasted Training
Fasted training is not ideal for:

 1. High-volume or high-intensity sessions - Heavy leg days, high-rep hypertrophy work, and
   intense conditioning benefit from pre-workout fuel.

 2. Those who feel weak or dizzy when fasted - Listen to your body. If you cannot perform, the
   training is compromised.

 3. Anyone prioritizing maximum performance - If your goal is peak strength or athletic
   performance, pre-workout nutrition optimizes output.

 4. Very long training sessions - Sessions over 90 minutes likely benefit from pre or intra-workout
   nutrition.

   **Caffeine and Fasted Training**

Fasted Training Protocols
Train with nothing but water, black coffee, or plain tea. Consume your first meal post-training when
your feeding window opens.

Best for: Morning training when feeding window starts at noon or later, low-to-moderate intensity
sessions, those who feel fine training fasted.

Consume a small protein serving (20-25g) 30 minutes before training. This technically breaks the
fast but provides amino acids for muscle protection without significantly spiking insulin or disrupting
fat oxidation.

Best for: Those concerned about muscle loss, longer or more intense fasted sessions, compromise
between fully fasted and fully fed.

Adjust your feeding window to include pre-workout nutrition. If you train at 7am, your feeding
window could be 6am-2pm instead of 12pm-8pm.

Best for: Those who perform significantly better with pre-workout food, anyone prioritising training
performance over fasting convenience.

For sessions over 60-90 minutes, intra-workout nutrition can help regardless of whether you started
fasted:

  • EAAs (Essential Amino Acids): 10-15g sipped during training

  • Carbohydrates: 20-40g fast-digesting carbs for very long or intense sessions

  • Electrolytes: Sodium, potassium, magnesium for hydration

> For most standard 45-75 minute sessions, intra-workout nutrition is unnecessary. Water is
sufficient.

Caffeine is your friend for fasted training. It:

  • Increases fatty acid mobilisation

  • Improves focus and reduces perceived exertion

  • Blunts hunger

  • Has essentially zero calories

200-400mg caffeine (1-2 cups of coffee) 30-60 minutes before training improves fasted training
performance for most people.

Be mindful of your total daily caffeine intake and timing relative to sleep.

Practical Recommendations

 1. Experiment - Try fasted training for 2-3 weeks and assess performance, energy, and results.
    Your response is individual.

 2. Match intensity to state - Save your hardest sessions for times when you have fuel. Use fasted
    training for moderate work.

 3. Prioritise post-workout nutrition - However you train, eat protein within 2-3 hours after.

 4. Do not force it - If fasted training consistently compromises your performance, it is not worth it.
    Training quality matters more than training timing.

   **Adaptation timeline:**

Hunger Management
Intermittent fasting is not universally easy. Most people face challenges during adaptation and
implementation. Knowing how to troubleshoot these issues prevents unnecessary suffering and
program abandonment.

Hunger during fasting is normal, especially during the first 1-2 weeks. Your body is adapted to
eating at certain times - it will signal hunger at those times regardless of actual need.

  • Hunger comes in waves. It peaks and passes. If you wait 20-30 minutes, it often subsides.

  • Much of what we call hunger is actually habit, boredom, or thirst.

  • Hunger does not continuously increase. After the initial wave, it often decreases.

  • Ghrelin (the hunger hormone) adapts to new eating patterns within 1-2 weeks.

 1. Stay hydrated - Drink water consistently throughout the fast. Often what feels like hunger is
   actually thirst. Aim for 2-3 litres during your fasting window.

 2. Black coffee and tea - Caffeine blunts hunger. Use it strategically during hunger peaks.

 3. Stay busy - Hunger is worse when you are bored and thinking about food. Stay occupied,
   especially during the final hours of your fast.

 4. Electrolytes - Sodium, potassium, and magnesium can reduce hunger and improve energy
   during fasting. Add salt to water or use a zero-calorie electrolyte mix.

 5. Ride the wave - When hunger hits, set a timer for 20 minutes. Do something else. The wave will
   usually pass.

  • Days 1-3: Most difficult. Strong hunger at usual meal times.

  • Days 4-7: Hunger begins decreasing. Body starts adapting.

  • Weeks 2-3: Hunger largely normalised. Fasting feels natural.

  • Week 4+: Many people report decreased hunger overall, even during eating windows.

> If hunger remains unbearable after 3 weeks of consistent practice, IF may not be right for you.
That is fine. It is not mandatory.

Energy Crashes
Some people experience energy crashes during fasting, particularly in the first few weeks.

Causes and solutions:

 1. Blood sugar dysregulation - If you have been eating high-carb, frequent meals, your body is
   adapted to glucose as primary fuel. The transition to fat oxidation takes time.

> Solution: Reduce carbohydrate intake during adaptation. This speeds metabolic flexibility. Ensure
your meals include adequate fat and protein.

 1. Electrolyte imbalance - Fasting increases water and electrolyte excretion. Low sodium
   especially causes fatigue, headaches, and brain fog.

> Solution: Increase sodium intake. Add salt to water. Use electrolyte supplements. This alone fixes
many energy issues.

 1. Caffeine timing - If you rely on caffeine and typically have it with breakfast, delaying it can
   cause withdrawal-like fatigue.

> Solution: Have black coffee at your normal time. Caffeine does not break your fast.

 1. Under-eating in feeding window - Some people eat too little during their feeding window,
    creating an excessive caloric deficit.

> Solution: Ensure you eat sufficient calories during your feeding window. Track for a few days to
verify.

Social Situations
Fasting protocols can create friction with social eating - breakfast meetings, family meals, dinner
events outside your window.

Strategies:

 1. Flexibility over rigidity - Occasional deviation from your window is fine. One breakfast meeting
    does not ruin anything. Adjust that day and return to normal the next.

 2. Shift your window - If you have a dinner event at 9pm, shift your window later that day. Start
    eating at 2pm instead of noon.

 3. Communicate simply - You do not need to explain intermittent fasting to everyone. "I'm not
    hungry in the morning" or "I had a late breakfast" handles most situations.

 4. Focus on the average - What matters is your pattern over weeks and months, not any single
    day. 90% adherence is excellent.

Sleep Disruption
Some people experience sleep issues when fasting, particularly if their eating window ends too
early.

Causes and solutions:

 1. Going to bed hungry - If your window ends at 6pm and you sleep at 11pm, hunger can disrupt
    sleep.

> Solution: Shift your window later, or ensure your final meal is large and includes slow-digesting
protein and fat.

 1. Eating too close to bed - Conversely, eating large meals immediately before bed can also
    disrupt sleep through digestion.

> Solution: Finish eating at least 2 hours before bed.

 1. Blood sugar drops - Some people experience blood sugar drops during the night that cause
   waking. I've personally encountered this issue.

> Solution: Include complex carbohydrates and protein in your final meal. Consider a small protein
serving before bed.

   IF and Body Composition

When IF is NOT Right for You
Intermittent fasting is not appropriate for everyone. Consider avoiding or discontinuing IF if:

 1. You have a history of disordered eating - IF can trigger restrictive patterns and unhealthy
   relationships with food. If you have struggled with anorexia, bulimia, or binge eating, proceed
   with extreme caution or avoid entirely.

 2. You are significantly underweight or struggling to gain weight - IF makes eating enough
   calories harder. If you need to eat 3500+ calories, an 8-hour window is challenging.

 3. You experience persistent negative symptoms - If after 3-4 weeks of consistent practice you
   still feel terrible, have no energy, and cannot perform, your body may not respond well to fasting.

 4. You are under significant stress - Fasting is a stressor. Adding it during periods of high life
   stress can push you over the edge. Consider implementing during calmer periods.

 5. You have certain medical conditions - Diabetes (especially Type 1), thyroid disorders, and
   other metabolic conditions require medical guidance before fasting.

 6. You are a competitive athlete in-season - When performance is the priority, maximise nutrition
   timing rather than restricting it.

> There is no shame in deciding IF is not for you. It is one tool among many. The goal is results, not
adherence to any particular method.

   During Fat Loss

IF During Fat Loss Phases
Intermittent fasting is not one-size-fits-all across different body composition goals. How you
implement IF should change based on whether you are losing fat, maintaining, or building muscle.

This is where IF shines brightest. The protocol naturally supports caloric restriction.

Why IF works well for fat loss:

 1. Natural caloric reduction - Eliminating meals eliminates calories. Most people naturally eat less
   with fewer eating opportunities.

 2. Extended fat oxidation - More hours in the fasted state means more hours preferentially
   burning fat.

 3. Simplified compliance - Fewer meals means fewer decisions. Structure supports adherence.

 4. Hunger management - Counterintuitively, many people experience less hunger with IF than
   frequent small meals. Larger, satisfying meals can be more satiating than constant snacking.

 5. Psychological benefits - Clear rules and structure help some people stick to deficits better than
   vague "eat less" approaches.

Fat loss IF protocol:

  • Use 16:8 as baseline

  • Consider tightening to 18:6 during aggressive cuts

  • Maintain protein (higher end during deficit)

  • Train during or just before feeding window when possible

  • Use caloric deficit of 20-25% below maintenance

  • Include 1-2 re-feed days per week at maintenance calories

IF During Maintenance
At maintenance calories, IF is a lifestyle choice rather than a fat loss tool. Some people prefer it.
Others do not.

Considerations for maintenance IF:

 1. Sustainability - Can you see yourself eating this way indefinitely? If not, consider a different
   approach.

 2. Social fit - Does your eating window align with your lifestyle, family meals, and social events?

 3. Training performance - Are you able to fuel training adequately within your window?

> If IF feels natural and supports your lifestyle at maintenance, continue. If it feels forced, there is no
metabolic advantage to maintaining it.

Maintenance IF protocol:

  • 16:8 is typically sufficient

  • No need for tighter windows

  • Flexibility on weekends or social occasions is fine

  • Focus on consistency over perfection

IF During Muscle Gain Phases
This is where IF becomes more challenging and potentially counterproductive for some people.

The challenge:

Building muscle requires a caloric surplus - you need to eat more than you burn. This is harder with
restricted eating windows.

If you need 3200 calories to gain muscle and you have 8 hours to eat them, that is 400 calories per
hour, or roughly 1000+ calories per meal if eating three times. This is a lot of food in a compressed
timeframe.

Additionally, muscle protein synthesis is optimally stimulated with protein distributed across multiple
feedings. Compressing all protein into a short window may leave MPS understimulated for portions
of the day.

Should you use IF when gaining?

It depends:

IF can work for gaining if:

  • Your caloric needs are moderate (under 3000 calories)

  • You can comfortably eat large meals

  • You prefer fewer, bigger meals naturally

  • You use a 16:8 window (not tighter)

IF is probably not ideal for gaining if:

  • Your caloric needs are high (3500+ calories)

  • You have a small appetite or feel sick eating large meals

  • You are a hardgainer who struggles to eat enough

  • You are prioritising maximum muscle growth

Muscle gain IF protocol (if using):

  • Stick to 16:8 maximum - do not use tighter windows

  • Consider 14:10 for easier caloric intake

  • Front-load protein early in the window

  • Use calorie-dense foods to hit targets

  • Include a protein serving immediately before window closes

  • Be willing to abandon IF if you cannot eat enough

Alternative approach for gaining:

If you want some fasting benefits while prioritising muscle gain, consider:

  • Shorter overnight fast (12-14 hours) rather than 16

  • 5:2 approach - fast two days per week, eat freely five days

  • Occasional 24-hour fast once per week or every other week while eating normally otherwise

These provide some fasting benefits without the daily constraint on eating.

Cycling IF Based on Goals
A practical approach is cycling IF based on your current phase:

Cutting phase: Implement 16:8 or 18:6. Leverage IF's fat loss benefits.

Maintenance phase: Use 16:8 if it suits your lifestyle. Flexible if not.

Gaining phase: Relax or abandon IF. Prioritize eating enough. Return to IF when cutting again.

This gives you the benefits of IF when they matter most (fat loss) without fighting against it when it is
counterproductive (muscle gain).

Tracking Progress

When using IF for body composition, track:

 1. Scale weight - Weekly averages, not daily fluctuations

 2. Waist measurement - Most reliable indicator of fat change

 3. Progress photos - Monthly, same lighting and conditions

 4. Strength performance - Are lifts maintaining or improving?

 5. Energy and wellbeing - Sustainable approaches feel manageable

> If you are losing fat, maintaining strength, and feeling decent, IF is working. If you are losing
strength, feeling terrible, or not progressing, something needs adjustment.
