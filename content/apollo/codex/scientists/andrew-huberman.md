## Plain English

A Stanford neuroscientist whose Huberman Lab podcast turned laboratory protocols into consumer language. The reason words like *protocol* and *light exposure* now sit in a casual gym conversation is, in large part, his work over the last five years.

## What Apollo uses

Apollo runs on the protocol register Huberman established. Specifically:

- **Morning sunlight within 30 to 60 minutes of waking.** Anchors the circadian rhythm and brings melatonin onset forward by evening. M02 and M18 both load this protocol verbatim.
- **Deliberate cold exposure for catecholamine and recovery effect.** Apollo's cold finisher in M07 sits inside Huberman's "Tools for Stress Recovery" framework, dosed conservatively rather than maximally.
- **Pre-bed ambient temperature drop and screen discipline.** M18 follows the temperature-onset model Huberman draws from Matthew Walker's lab work and others.

The key contribution Apollo borrows is *protocol legibility*. The idea that a member can be told a stimulus, a dose, and a window, and that this is enough.

## What Apollo does not echo

The breadth of Huberman's interests does not transfer cleanly into a 90-day system. Apollo is selective:

- Apollo does not stack supplements. The Founder Dossier flags supplementation as a category-default trap, and the brand abstains by design.
- Apollo does not chase peak protocols. Where Huberman teaches the upper range of an intervention (cold for maximum BAT activation, light for maximum alertness), Apollo dials to the sustainable middle. The doctrine *effortless not extreme* prevents drift into protocol-maximalism.
- Apollo does not replicate Huberman's pace of new tools. The 90-day protocol is fixed. New podcast episodes do not change the plan.
