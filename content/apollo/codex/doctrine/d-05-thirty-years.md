## The Doctrine

> "Most men optimise for the next 30 days. You are going to optimise for the next 30 years."

The Phase 4 pivot. The work changes target. The member stops asking *how do I look in summer* and starts asking *how do I move at 70*.

## Why this doctrine matters

This is where Apollo separates from cosmetic fitness entirely. The Greek body remains the visible payoff. The actual deliverable is a body that is still capable of training, of carrying, of standing up off the floor unassisted, three decades from now.

The doctrine maps cleanly onto research members already trust. Peter Attia's frame is exactly this. Grip strength, VO₂max, leg power are not bodybuilding metrics. They are mortality predictors. The Codex carries this doctrine through with the studies that make the case in numbers.

## The default that should flow from it

When this doctrine is internalised, training decisions stop being driven by the next eight weeks. The man does Zone 2 not because it shows up in a mirror but because it builds mitochondrial density that cannot be built any other way. He prioritises grip strength because the literature is unambiguous on what weak grip predicts. He guards his sleep because deep-stage sleep is non-negotiable for a 30-year horizon.

The default question becomes: **does this protocol still serve me at 65?**

## The drift check

> "In the last quarter, did I make a training, eating, or recovery decision that bought short-term results at the cost of decade-scale capacity?"

Common drift signals: cutting sleep to add training volume; skipping Zone 2 because it is not glamorous; dropping mobility work once the aesthetic is locked in. Each is a 30-day decision in a 30-year frame.
