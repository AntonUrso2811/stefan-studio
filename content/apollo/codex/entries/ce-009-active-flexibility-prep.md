## The Stance

Stefan's stance: warm up by moving, not by stretching. Five minutes of dynamic prep before training is the difference between the first set feeling rusty and the first set being trainable.

## Plain English

The prep block is short and active. Thoracic rotations, hip openers, scapular work, ankle mobility, leg swings. Move through the range you are about to load. The point is to wake the joints up, not to make them more flexible.

Static stretching before lifting reduces force output for the next 30 to 60 minutes. The literature is clear on this. Save the long holds for after the session or for separate mobility windows.

## Technical

Dynamic movement preparation raises tissue temperature, increases synovial fluid mobility, primes the central nervous system, and produces transient improvements in active range of motion without the force-output penalty seen after static stretching. The mechanism is largely neural rather than muscular: motor units recruit more efficiently after dynamic prep, especially at end-range.

## The Studies

In Applied Physiology, Nutrition, and Metabolism (2016), Behm and colleagues reviewed acute stretching effects and confirmed that dynamic prep outperforms static stretching for force-output retention immediately pre-exercise.

In Sports Medicine (2015), McGowan and colleagues reviewed warm-up strategies across sports, supporting the position that movement-based prep is more effective than passive prep for downstream performance.
