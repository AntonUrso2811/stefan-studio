## The Stance

Stefan's stance: stop counting calories. Anchor protein, fill the rest with whole food, and the calories sort themselves out. Protein leverage is the reason this works.

## Plain English

The body has a protein target it defends. If the food on the plate does not contain enough protein, the body keeps registering hunger until the target is met. In a modern diet of refined, low-protein-density foods, the path to that target is over-eating total calories.

The protocol is simple. Put the protein on the plate first, in a portion that matches the body's target for that meal. Then fill the rest with vegetables, whole carbohydrates, and fats to hunger. The calories track without a scale.

## Technical

The protein leverage hypothesis (Simpson and Raubenheimer) posits that across mammals, total energy intake is dynamically regulated to defend protein intake at a species-specific target. When dietary protein density falls, food intake rises until the absolute protein target is reached, often producing caloric surplus. Empirical work on humans across high, medium, and low protein density diets shows the predicted overconsumption pattern when protein density drops.

## The Studies

In Obesity Reviews (2005), Simpson and Raubenheimer proposed the protein leverage hypothesis as the mechanism connecting macronutrient composition to total energy intake.

In Obesity Reviews (2014), Gosby and colleagues reviewed the human evidence for protein leverage, including controlled-feeding studies showing predictable overconsumption of energy when protein percentage of diet is reduced.
