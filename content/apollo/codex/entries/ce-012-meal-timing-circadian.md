## The Stance

Stefan's stance: when a meal lands matters as much as what is in it. Eat the last meal at least three hours before bed and watch the protocol get easier without anything else changing.

## Plain English

The body has a master clock in the brain (driven by light) and peripheral clocks in the liver, gut, and muscle (driven by food). When food consistently arrives late in the evening, the peripheral clocks pull out of phase with the master clock. Sleep gets shallower. Glucose handling drops. Inflammation creeps. The same daily protein, the same daily training, run worse.

The fix is timing. Last meal three hours before bed. Eating window weighted earlier in the day. No other changes required.

## Technical

Peripheral oscillators are entrained by feeding time independently of the suprachiasmatic nucleus. Late-evening or post-bedtime feeding produces phase misalignment between central and peripheral clocks, with measurable effects on glucose tolerance, lipid handling, and slow-wave sleep duration. Early time-restricted feeding (eating window weighted earlier in the day) produces metabolic improvements even in the absence of weight loss.

## The Studies

In Current Biology (2017), Wehrens and colleagues demonstrated that meal timing alone, holding all other variables constant, shifts peripheral circadian phase in humans. Eating five hours later shifted plasma glucose rhythms by approximately the same magnitude.

In Cell Metabolism (2018), Sutton and colleagues showed that early time-restricted feeding (6 hour eating window, dinner before 3pm) improved insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, and oxidative stress in men with prediabetes, with no weight change.
